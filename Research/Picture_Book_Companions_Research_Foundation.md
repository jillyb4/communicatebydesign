## 1. Product Line Definition

Picture Book Companions are SDI-aligned instructional companions for picture books already present in K–3 classrooms. The companion — not the book — is the product. The companion:

- Adds the AAC/AT access layer the publisher did not provide
- Provides the read-aloud structure (IRA framework) the teacher uses during shared reading
- Sequences vocabulary pre-teaching and post-teaching relative to reading
- Connects to RL/RI standards appropriate for K–3

**What it is NOT:** A retelling worksheet. A comprehension packet. A generic "extension activity."

## 2. Three-Framework Foundation

The Picture Book Companions line is built on three converging frameworks. Every instructional activity must be traceable to at least one of these.

### 2a. Interactive Read-Aloud (IRA)

**Source:** Barrentine (1996); Beck & McKeown (2001); Fisher, Flood, Lapp & Frey (2004)

**Definition:** A structured shared reading approach in which the teacher reads aloud while actively engaging students through planned think-alouds, text-based questions, and vocabulary stops — before, during, and after reading.

**Key findings:**
- IRA produces significantly greater vocabulary acquisition than unstructured read-aloud, particularly for Tier 2 academic vocabulary (Beck & McKeown 2001)
- Pre-reading vocabulary instruction (before the book is opened) dramatically improves comprehension of target words in context
- Planned stopping points during reading ("text talk" moments) must be tied to specific words or concepts identified in advance — not improvised
- Student response during IRA should be active and varied: gesture, symbol selection, verbal, written — AAC users must have equal access pathways

**CbD application:**
- Every companion specifies pre-reading vocabulary stops, during-reading text talk moments, and post-reading consolidation activities
- Vocabulary stops are not improvised — they are pre-scripted in the teacher-facing companion
- All response formats include AAC access pathways (symbol cards, communication boards, gaze boards)

---

### 2b. Dialogic Reading

**Source:** Whitehurst et al. (1988); Zevenbergen & Whitehurst (2003); Justice & Ezell (2002)

**Definition:** An evidence-based read-aloud technique in which the adult shifts from reading TO the child to reading WITH the child. The adult uses PEER prompts (Prompt, Evaluate, Expand, Repeat) to draw out child language, vocabulary, and comprehension during and after reading.

**PEER Sequence:**
1. **Prompt** — ask the child to say something about the book
2. **Evaluate** — evaluate the response (correct, approximate, unclear)
3. **Expand** — add to or enrich the child's utterance
4. **Repeat** — have the child repeat the expanded form

**CROWD Prompt Types:**
- **Completion** — "The girl used her ___ to climb" (fill-in)
- **Recall** — "What did Jennifer do at the Capitol?"
- **Open-ended** — "What do you notice on this page?"
- **Wh-** — who, what, where, when, why
- **Distancing** — connect book to child's life/experience

**Key findings:**
- Dialogic Reading produces significant gains in expressive vocabulary and narrative comprehension for preschool through grade 2 (Whitehurst et al. 1988; Zevenbergen & Whitehurst 2003)
- Effects are strongest when repeated across 3–5 readings of the same book
- Effects transfer to children with language delays when adult prompting is systematic and consistent (Justice & Ezell 2002)
- AAC users can participate in PEER/CROWD prompts via symbol selection, gesture + eye gaze, or partner-confirmed approximation — the key is that the ADULT models response formats on the student's system (Aided Language Stimulation) concurrent with prompting

**CbD application:**
- Every companion provides partner-facing CROWD prompt cards (pre-scripted for each page spread or key moment)
- Prompt cards specify expected response format: symbol + AAC system / gesture / verbal / combination
- Partners are coached to model responses ON the student's system before asking for a response
- Companion is designed for 3+ readings (initial read, vocabulary focus re-read, student-led re-read)

---

### 2c. Aided Language Stimulation (ALS / ALgS)

**Source:** Goossens' (1989); Goossens', Crain & Elder (1992); Cafiero (2001); Binger & Light (2007)

**Definition:** A strategy in which communication partners model vocabulary ON the student's AAC system while providing verbal input — simultaneously pointing to symbols as they speak. The partner demonstrates the use of the AAC system; the student is not required to imitate or produce.

**Key findings:**
- ALS increases symbol use by AAC users across all age groups (Goossens' et al. 1992; Cafiero 2001)
- Effects are cumulative — consistency of adult modeling across multiple contexts and multiple partners is the strongest predictor of student symbol use
- ALS does NOT require the student to be looking at the system or to respond — it is input, not instruction
- Partner training is essential: untrained partners default to verbal prompting and do not use the system (Binger & Light 2007)
- ALS during shared book reading specifically increases comprehension vocabulary for students with complex communication needs (Cafiero 2001)

**CbD application:**
- Every companion includes an Aided Language Stimulation Guide (one-page pullout) with symbol locations for key vocabulary mapped to page-by-page prompts
- Partner mode guidance (Mode 1/2/3) is defined per activity
- ALS is framed as the partner's role — not the student's — to avoid "over-prompting" culture
- Core vocabulary (already on most systems) is used for ALS first; fringe vocabulary (pre-programmed by SLP) is introduced only after SLP adds it to the system

## 4. What the Research Says Students with AAC Need to Succeed with Picture Books

### 4.1 Vocabulary Access Before the Book Is Opened

Students who use AAC cannot access new fringe vocabulary in real time during a read-aloud. If the word is not already on their system, they cannot use it to participate. This means:

- **Fringe vocabulary must be pre-programmed before the first read** (SLP lead time: 1–2 weeks)
- **Core vocabulary** (already on most systems) can and should be used during the read regardless
- The Communication Access Packet (CAP) is the SLP handoff document — it specifies exactly which words need to be added to the student's system and at what location

### 4.2 Multiple Exposures Across Multiple Readings

A single read of a picture book is not sufficient for vocabulary acquisition — for any student, and especially for students with language delays or AAC users (Justice & Ezell 2002; Beck & McKeown 2001). The companion is designed for 3 readings minimum:

- **Reading 1:** Full text IRA — establish story, introduce vocabulary stops
- **Reading 2:** Vocabulary-focus re-read — deep stops at target words, CROWD prompts
- **Reading 3:** Student-led / partner-prompted — student initiates CROWD prompts (role reversal)

### 4.3 Partner Training Is Not Optional

The most robust finding across IRA, Dialogic Reading, and ALS research: the adult is the variable. Partner training = biggest predictor of student outcomes. The companion must do the training, because teachers and paras were not trained to:

- Use PEER/CROWD prompts
- Model on a student's AAC system simultaneously with verbal input
- Shift between Mode 1 (instructional) and Mode 2 (partnership) — most partners default to Mode 1 everywhere
- Wait after a prompt (typical partner wait time: 1–3 seconds; research recommendation: 5–10 seconds)

**CbD response:** All companion materials are structured as partner-facing instruction. The product teaches the practice, not just the content.

### 4.4 Symbol-Based Reading Access

Students with complex communication needs who cannot decode printed text can still access picture books when:

- Symbol-supported text is present (printed word + corresponding symbol)
- The partner reads aloud and models vocabulary simultaneously on the student's system
- Response formats include non-verbal pathways (eye gaze, gesture, symbol selection)
- The student's participation is full, not modified — different access, same expectation

**CbD position:** The scaffold varies; the expectation does not. Every K–3 student engages with the same text. The companion provides the access layer; it does not create a different task.

## 5. Vocabulary Selection — Picture Book Companions (K–3)

**Vocabulary type (from framework):** IRA Read-Aloud Vocabulary + Thematic Conceptual Vocabulary

**Word selection sequence (from `_Operations/memory/vocabulary_selection_reference.md`):**
1. Identify the RL/RI standard being targeted (one skill per companion)
2. Tier 2 academic words that serve both the standard AND transfer to other K–3 texts
3. Thematic conceptual words that are central to the book's meaning (disability, accessibility, activism for All the Way to the Top)
4. Apply AAC lens — core (already on system) vs. fringe (SLP must add)
5. Identify response vocabulary — words student uses to respond to CROWD prompts
6. Select Top 5 per layer for CAP priority page

**K–3 specific rules:**
- Max ~15–20 words per companion (cognitive load ceiling for K–3 instruction)
- Every word must be ARASAAC-symbolizable and 1–2 touch accessible on most SGDs
- Prioritize words that appear across 3+ readings — vocabulary carryover drives retention
- Thematic conceptual vocabulary (e.g., *disability*, *rights*, *access*) counts as Tier 3 but may be Explicit Instruction Target if central to the RL standard being taught

## 6. Standards Alignment — K–3

Picture Book Companions target RL (Reading Literature) standards at K–3 grade bands. One standard per companion.

| Standard cluster | What it looks like at K–3 | Example — All the Way to the Top |
|-----------------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|
| RL.K-3.1 — Key details | Ask + answer questions about key details | Who is Jennifer? What did she do? Why? |
| RL.K-3.2 — Theme/central message | Identify the central message | Persistence. Disability rights. Everyone belongs. |
| RL.K-3.3 — Character response | Describe how characters respond to challenges | How did Jennifer feel? What did she decide to do? |
| RL.K-3.4 — Word meaning | Ask + answer questions about word meanings | What does *protest* mean? What does *access* mean? |
| RL.K-3.7 — Illustrations | Use illustrations to support understanding | What does the illustrator show? What does it tell us? |

**All the Way to the Top primary standard:** RL.K-3.3 — Character response to challenge (Jennifer's decision to climb the Capitol steps)

**Secondary standard:** RL.K-3.4 — Word meaning (*disability*, *access*, *protest*, *rights*)
