# Communicate by Design — AAC Resource Book List
*Started: 2026-04-07 | Curated by Jill McCardel*
*Purpose: Ongoing reference list of picture books and children's titles that feature AAC users, nonspeaking characters, communication-themed narratives, or disability representation aligned to CbD values. Separate from the pipeline build queue — this is the broader awareness and curation list.*

---

## How This List Works

Titles here are **not necessarily in the pipeline build queue.** They are tracked here for:
- Reference when educators ask for book recommendations
- Future Substack content (reading lists, awareness posts)
- Pinterest/Instagram resource sharing
- Potential pipeline candidates not yet gate-reviewed
- Community relationship building (author/illustrator awareness)

**Pipeline candidates** (titles that have passed initial screening and are being considered for companion builds) are tracked in `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`. Some titles appear in both places.

## Section 1 — AAC Device Users / Nonspeaking Characters

*Books that explicitly feature a child who uses an AAC device, a tablet to communicate, or is described as nonspeaking.*

---

### A Day with No Words
**Author/Illustrator:** Tiffany Hammond / Kate Cosgrove
**Grade:** K–2
**AAC representation:** Aidan is nonspeaking and uses a tablet to communicate
**Why it matters:** Written by Tiffany Hammond (@fidgetsandfreckles), mother of a nonspeaking child and one of the most trusted voices in the AAC community. Aidan is shown as a full person — connected, curious, loved. This is not a story about overcoming; it is a story about living. The most community-trusted explicit AAC device representation in current K–2 picture books.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 5 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### Bitsy Bat, Team Star
**Author/Illustrator:** Kaz Windness
**Grade:** K–1
**AAC representation:** Enzo Owl, supporting character, uses AAC device + sign language
**Why it matters:** Enzo is nonspeaking but fully present — a classmate, a teammate, a friend. The story is about jealousy and learning to celebrate others; Enzo is part of the team. Normalizes AAC in the classroom as just how a kid communicates.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 3 candidate (gate review needed)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### I'm Non-speaking, But I Hear You
**Author/Illustrator:** Erechia Kano
**Grade:** K–1
**AAC representation:** Sadie is nonspeaking; communicates through actions, presence, and connection
**Why it matters:** Quiet and affirming. Challenges the assumption that communication requires speech. Centers Sadie's inner life.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 2 candidate (gate review needed)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### My Brother Otto and the Birthday Party
**Author/Illustrator:** Meg Raby
**Grade:** K–1
**AAC representation:** Otto, autistic, uses AAC (implied); sibling narrator
**Why it matters:** Sibling perspective on navigating a birthday party with a brother who has sensory needs and communication differences. Practical, compassionate, and affirming of accommodation.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 3 candidate (gate review needed)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

## Section 2 — Communication Identity / All Ways of Communicating

*Books that celebrate multiple forms of communication — including AAC, signing, stimming, writing, gestures — as equally valid. Not disability-specific, but deeply aligned to CbD's AAC ecosystem philosophy.*

---

### This is How We Talk
**Author/Illustrator:** Jessica Slice / Parwinder Singh
**Grade:** K–1
**Communication representation:** Signing, stimming, clapping, device use, writing, gestures, smiling — all named and celebrated
**Why it matters:** Disability-authored. Joyful and explicit — every double-page spread names a different way of communicating. The message is CbD's brand thesis made visible: communication is not one thing. There is no hierarchy.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 5 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### A Voice Like Yours
**Author/Illustrator:** Frank Murphy
**Grade:** K–1
**Communication representation:** Broad — art, action, expression, and non-speech communication as voice
**Why it matters:** Encourages children to see their voice as more than speech. Not disability-specific but opens conversations about communication access.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 2 candidate (assess differentiation from *I Talk Like a River* before queuing)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### You Can Say Hi With Only a Smile
**Author/Illustrator:** Merriam Sarcia Saunders
**Grade:** K–1
**Communication representation:** Speaking, signing, devices, smiling — many ways to greet and connect
**Why it matters:** Practical, accessible, social-skills framing. Teaches children to recognize and respect communication differences.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 2 candidate (assess differentiation from *This is How We Talk* before queuing)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### More than Words
**Author/Illustrator:** Roz Maclean
**Grade:** K–1
**Communication representation:** Nathan observes classmates communicating via signing, gestures, smiles, devices
**Why it matters:** Observational narrator notices that expression goes beyond speech. Inclusive classroom message.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 2 candidate (gate review needed; assess against similar titles)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### I Talk Like a River
**Author/Illustrator:** Jordan Scott / Sydney Smith
**Grade:** K–2
**Communication representation:** A boy who stutters finds his voice; communication and identity
**Why it matters:** Caldecott recognition (Sydney Smith). The metaphor of words as a river — rushing, stopping, swirling — is both lyrical and AAC-applicable. Communication is the entire subject.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 5 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** CbD internal pipeline research, April 2026

## Section 3 — Disability Rep / Neurodiversity (Broader)

*Books featuring characters with disabilities or neurodivergent identities. Not all focus on communication; all center disabled children as full agents.*

---

### Jo's Special Gifts
**Author/Illustrator:** Mariam Shapera
**Grade:** K–1
**Disability rep:** Jo, autistic; uses headphones + AAC (implied); passions and sensory needs honored
**Why it matters:** Celebrates neurodiversity and special interests. Written to affirm autistic children's way of experiencing the world.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 2 candidate (gate review needed; community credibility TBD)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### We Are the Scrappy Ones
**Author/Illustrator:** Rebekah Taussig
**Grade:** K–2
**Disability rep:** Ensemble cast — children with various disabilities navigating an inaccessible world
**Why it matters:** Written by Rebekah Taussig (disabled author, wheelchair user, writer of *Sitting Pretty*). Celebrates resilience, creativity, and adaptability. Honest about barriers — not inspirational, but empowering.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 3 candidate (gate review needed; author community credibility is high)
**Source:** MaiStoryBook curated list, April 2026

---

### A Friend for Henry
**Author/Illustrator:** Jenn Bailey / Mika Song
**Grade:** K–1
**Disability rep:** Henry, autistic protagonist and narrator
**Why it matters:** Henry narrates his own experience. Core vocabulary is dense and natural — one of the strongest AAC vocabulary books in the genre. Strong classroom use.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 5 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** CbD internal pipeline research, April 2026

---

### Ian's Walk: A Story About Autism
**Author/Illustrator:** Laurie Lears / Karen Roseberry
**Grade:** K–1
**Disability rep:** Ian, autistic; sibling narrator
**Why it matters:** Ian is the character, not the problem. Sibling learns to understand Ian's sensory world. Strong for inclusive classroom conversations.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 4 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** CbD internal pipeline research, April 2026

---

### My Friend Isabelle
**Author/Illustrator:** Eliza Woloson / Bryan Langdo
**Grade:** K–1
**Disability rep:** Isabelle, Down syndrome; peer narrator
**Why it matters:** Early inclusion classic. Honest, joyful, realistic. Written by a parent of a child with Down syndrome. Zero saccharine — genuine friendship.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 4 — currently in pipeline, built April 2026 — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** CbD internal pipeline research, April 2026

---

### All the Way to the Top
**Author/Illustrator:** Annette Bay Pimentel / Nadin Matheis
**Grade:** K–2
**Disability rep:** Jennifer Keelan, wheelchair user, disability rights activist
**Why it matters:** Jennifer was eight years old when she crawled up the Capitol steps. Child as agent of enormous historical change. First CbD Picture Book Companion — **LIVE on TPT.**
**Pipeline status:** ✅ LIVE on TPT (April 4, 2026)
**Source:** CbD flagship companion

## Section 4 — Civil Rights / Advocacy

*Books featuring children as agents of change — the Jennifer Keelan model. Connects to CbD's nonfiction line (grades 6–10).*

---

### Emmanuel's Dream: The True Story of Emmanuel Ofosu Yeboah
**Author/Illustrator:** Laurie Ann Thompson / Sean Qualls
**Grade:** 1–2
**Rep:** Emmanuel, born with one leg, cycles across Ghana to change attitudes about disability
**Why it matters:** Non-Western disability story. Child as agent. Near-zero TPT competition for AAC companion.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 4 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** CbD internal pipeline research, April 2026

---

### All Are Welcome
**Author/Illustrator:** Alexandra Penfold / Suzanne Kaufman
**Grade:** K–1
**Rep:** Ensemble — includes child who uses a wheelchair; many faiths and cultures represented
**Why it matters:** Explicit disability representation + repetitive text structure. "All are welcome, all belong" = exceptional Predictable Chart Writing anchor.
**Pipeline status:** Priority 4 candidate — see `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`
**Source:** CbD internal pipeline research, April 2026

## How to Add to This List

When adding a new title, include:
- Title, author, illustrator
- Grade band
- What kind of representation (AAC device, nonspeaking, disability, communication identity)
- One-sentence "why it matters" aligned to CbD values
- Pipeline status (is it in `CbD_PictureBook_Pipeline_Research.md`? What priority?)
- Source (where we found it / who recommended it)
