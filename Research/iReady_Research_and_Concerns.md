# i-Ready: Research, Criticism & Accessibility Concerns
*Compiled April 9, 2026 — For CbD advocacy reference and future position statement use*

---

## Why This Matters for Communicate by Design

i-Ready is used by 13+ million K–8 students nationwide and is widely mandated in school districts, including for students with disabilities. CbD's position is grounded in the following concerns:

1. **The platform does not support meaningful formative assessment** — the foundational mechanism for adjusting instruction is removed, which is antithetical to effective teaching, especially for students with learning barriers.
2. **The platform is inaccessible to many students who use AAC, eye gaze, switch access, or alternative input methods** — meaning students with complex communication and motor needs are being assessed on a system that was never built for them.
3. **There is no independent evidence it improves outcomes** — and no disability-specific efficacy data whatsoever.

---

## The Efficacy Problem

### What the Research Actually Shows

- **Zero randomized controlled trials** have been conducted on i-Ready
- **Zero top-tier peer-reviewed journal articles** support its efficacy
- The most-cited studies (Johns Hopkins) were **conducted in partnership with Curriculum Associates** — the company that makes i-Ready — creating a fundamental conflict of interest
- The most favorable math result: **7.8% improvement vs. 7.0% for non-users** — a less-than-1-percentage-point difference, costing over **$535,000 across ~7,600 students**
- The reading study: **no statistically significant results at all**

*Source: "i-Ready: 13 Million Students, Zero Meaningful Evidence" — The Digital Delusion (Substack)*
*Source: 2019 Impact Evaluation of Reading i-Ready Instruction — ERIC/Johns Hopkins (ED604746)*

### The Formative Assessment Design Flaw — Critical for CbD

Effective formative assessment requires teachers to **see diagnostic data and adapt instruction in response**. i-Ready gives teachers summary dashboards only — teachers **never see the child's actual responses**. This removes the core mechanism that makes assessment useful.

This is not a minor limitation. This is foundational to effective teaching practice, and it is especially damaging for:
- Students with learning disabilities who require precise instructional adjustments
- Students who need differentiated access pathways
- Teams making IEP decisions based on assessment data

If the data can't inform instruction, it isn't assessment — it's surveillance.

*Source: Nancy Bailey, PhD / NEPC blog: "The Murky World of i-Ready"*

## Accessibility: Who i-Ready Was NOT Built For

### What Curriculum Associates Claims to Support
- Audio support
- Closed captioning
- Keyboard navigation
- Screen reader compatibility (with significant limitations)
- Visual design features for low vision

### What Is Explicitly Missing
Curriculum Associates' own accessibility documentation **does not mention**:
- **Switch access** technology
- **Eye gaze** input
- **AAC user accommodations**
- **Motor disability** accommodations beyond basic keyboard navigation
- Alternative input methods beyond keyboard

For students with complex motor needs, the platform directs users to "contact your i-Ready Partner Success Manager" — which is not an accessibility solution. It is a deflection.

### Real-World Impact
Students who use:
- Eye gaze devices (e.g., Tobii, EyeTech)
- Switch access (1-switch, 2-switch scanning)
- Head tracking
- Partner-assisted scanning
- Alternative pencils / PECS / low-tech AAC

...cannot meaningfully access or be accurately assessed by i-Ready. These students are being placed on a platform that was never designed for them, and their assessment data is therefore invalid.

**No efficacy data exists for students with disabilities on this platform.** The entire research base — thin as it is — was conducted on general education populations.

## Public Criticism: Who Is Raising This and With What Weight

### Substantive (Evidence-Based) Critics

| Source | Who | What They Found | Evidence Weight |
|--------|-----|-----------------|-----------------|
| The Digital Delusion (Substack) | Independent education researcher | Zero meaningful evidence after 15 years; deep analysis of Johns Hopkins studies | Strong — cites primary research |
| Nancy Bailey, PhD / NEPC | Educational leadership scholar | Teachers can't see student responses; misuse for grading; privacy concerns | Moderate — cites studies + news |
| Johns Hopkins Study (2016/2019) | Academic researchers (commissioned by CA) | No significant reading gains; <1% math difference | High — this is the primary source itself |
| Math Exchanges Blog (Kassia Omohundro Wedekind) | Classroom educator/math specialist | "Why i-Ready is Dangerous" — algorithmic misclassification, inappropriate placements | Moderate — practitioner evidence |

### Legal / Formal Channels

- **Active lawsuit filed by EdTech Law Center** on behalf of parents: alleges Curriculum Associates harvests student data, shares with dozens of third parties for commercial profit, and builds **psychological and behavioral profiles** on children
  - *Note: Allegations only — not yet proven. Case is in early stages.*
  - *Source: Scrolling2Death podcast / edtech.law*

### What Is Notably Absent
- No formal position statement from **NEA, AFT, CEC (Council for Exceptional Children), ASHA, or CASE**
- No investigative reporting from **Education Week, Chalkbeat, or The 74** specifically on efficacy
- No peer-reviewed challenge to efficacy from an independent research body
- **No disability-specific efficacy data from any source — including Curriculum Associates itself**

The criticism is real and the evidentiary gap is real, but it is currently being carried largely by independent bloggers, parent advocates, and practitioners — not institutional bodies. **The lawsuit could change this** if it advances and discovery produces documentation of data practices.

## CbD Position — Working Notes

### Core Argument
i-Ready represents a systemic failure on three levels that align directly with CbD's brand thesis:

1. **Curriculum problem:** Assessment that cannot inform instruction is not formative assessment — it is compliance theater.
2. **Data problem:** Data that can't be seen by teachers, can't be tied to instructional adjustments, and was never validated for students with disabilities is not IEP-appropriate data. Using i-Ready scores to inform IEP goals is not supported by evidence.
3. **Access problem:** A platform that does not support switch access, eye gaze, or AAC users is not an equitable assessment tool. Mandating it for students with complex access needs is an ADA/IDEA concern.

### Potential Angles for CbD Content
- Substack post: "What i-Ready Is Actually Measuring (And Why It Can't Tell You What You Need to Know)"
- IEP team guidance: Why i-Ready data should not be the primary evidence base for IEP goals

- Professional development angle: What real formative assessment looks like for complex communicators
