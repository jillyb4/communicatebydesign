# CbD Vocabulary Selection Framework
*Communicate by Design | Compiled: 2026-03-29 | Version 1.0*

---

## Purpose and Use

This document is the research-backed framework governing how vocabulary is selected, classified, and built into all Communicate by Design products. It exists because vocabulary selection is the central instructional function of every CbD product line — and without a principled, evidence-based methodology, there is no reliable QC standard for what belongs in a unit, what gets a symbol, what goes on a device, or what gets explicitly taught.

**This framework answers three operational questions:**

1. **What language do we decide to teach?** (Word selection criteria)
2. **How do we classify it?** (Layer and type system)
3. **How does a student access it?** (Motor planning, symbol, and device considerations)

It applies to every product line. The criteria shift by line — but the framework is the same.

**Cross-reference:** Every new product line idea should be evaluated against Section 7 (Product Line Application) and the Section 8 QC Checklist before build begins.

## Part 1: The Three Research Layers

CbD products sit at the intersection of three evidence bases. Vocabulary decisions must be defensible across all three simultaneously.

### Layer 1: ELA Vocabulary Instruction Research

**Primary framework: Beck, McKeown & Kucan (2002, 2013) — Tier System**

The foundational ELA vocabulary research establishes a three-tier model for selecting which words warrant explicit instruction:

| Tier | Description | Examples | Instructional Treatment |
|------|-------------|---------|------------------------|
| **Tier 1** | Basic, high-frequency words students already know | *go, big, house, happy* | No explicit instruction needed |
| **Tier 2** | High-utility academic words appearing across content areas and genres | *analyze, evidence, advocate, compare, significant* | **Primary instructional target** |
| **Tier 3** | Low-frequency, domain-specific technical words | *thalidomide, ADA, photosynthesis* | Teach as needed within the specific unit |

**The key research finding:** Tier 2 words are the highest-leverage instructional targets because they transfer across texts, subjects, and contexts. A student who learns *evidence* in a nonfiction reading unit uses it in science, history, and argument writing. A student who learns *thalidomide* uses it in exactly one context.

**How many exposures?** Beck's research and the broader vocabulary literature converge on 12 or more meaningful encounters before a word moves into active use. "Meaningful" means: not repetition of the same activity, but varied encounters — definitional, contextual, generative, in reading and writing. This is not a threshold any single lesson meets alone.

**Robust vocabulary instruction** (Graves, 2006, 2016; Stahl & Fairbanks, 1986) requires:
- Definitional AND contextual information together — not definitions alone
- Active processing — students must do something with the word, not just receive it
- Deep engagement — comparison, analogy, categorization, personal connection
- Multiple modalities — visual, verbal, written, motor (for AAC users: symbol + device output + written text)

**CCSS Language Standards anchor:**
- **L.4–6** define grade-specific vocabulary acquisition through context clues, morphology (roots and affixes), and nuanced meaning
- **L.6** requires "acquiring and using accurately general academic and domain-specific words" — a standard that applies regardless of communication modality

### Layer 2: Disability, HLP, and Special Education Evidence Base

**Primary framework: CEC High-Leverage Practices (McLeskey et al., 2017)**

The HLPs most relevant to vocabulary instruction:

| HLP | Name | Application to Vocabulary |
|-----|------|--------------------------|
| **HLP 12** | Systematically design instruction toward a specific learning goal | Vocabulary selection must be aligned to the learning goal — not comprehensive, not incidental |
| **HLP 13** | Adapt curriculum tasks and materials | Vocabulary access is an adaptation — same words, different access mode (symbol, motor plan, graphic organizer) |
| **HLP 14** | Teach cognitive and metacognitive strategies | Students learn strategies for figuring out words — not just words themselves |
| **HLP 16** | Use explicit instruction | Direct vocabulary instruction: definition, example, non-example, guided practice, check for understanding |

**The critical HLP 12 principle for CbD:** Every word in a Communication Access Packet must be traceable to a specific learning goal. If you cannot answer "why does a student need this word to engage with this standard?" it may not belong in the unit.

**UDL connection (CAST, udlguidelines.cast.org):**
- Pre-teaching vocabulary before reading is a UDL representation strategy, not a remediation
- Barriers to learning are in the design of instruction, not in individual learners
- Multiple means of representation for vocabulary = symbol + text + audio + motor output

**Participation Model (Beukelman & Mirenda, 2013):**
This is the lens that reframes vocabulary selection from what teachers want students to know to what students need to communicate. Two categories of barriers:

1. **Opportunity barriers** — the vocabulary simply isn't available on the student's device or materials (this is the gap CbD fills)
2. **Access barriers** — the student has the vocabulary but cannot access it fluently (motor planning, visual scanning, array size)

The correct question is: *What does this student need to communicate in order to participate in this lesson activity?* That question generates a very different vocabulary list than *What words appear in this passage?*

### Layer 3: SLP and AAC Vocabulary Research

**Core vs. Fringe: The foundational AAC distinction**

| Category | Definition | Size | Characteristics |
|----------|-----------|------|----------------|
| **Core vocabulary** | High-frequency words that make up ~80% of daily communication across contexts | ~300–400 words | Typically already on robust AAC systems; abstract and versatile; pronouns, verbs, question words, social words |
| **Fringe vocabulary** | Topic-specific, low-frequency words used within narrow contexts | Unlimited | NOT typically pre-programmed; concrete; content nouns, proper nouns, domain vocabulary |
| **Heart words** | Irregular high-frequency words — phonics exception words | Subset | Memorized as whole words; frequently appear in academic text |

**The participation implication:** A student cannot use a fringe word in class if it has not been pre-programmed on their device. This is not a programming error — it is a service delivery failure. The Communication Access Packet exists to prevent it.

**Recommended programming lead time:** 1–2 weeks before a unit begins. SLP and AAC team must receive the fringe word list in advance.

**Motor planning: LAMP (Language Acquisition through Motor Planning)**

LAMP (Halloran & Halloran; Center for AAC and Autism) establishes that fluent AAC access depends on consistent, unique motor patterns — not visual search. This has direct vocabulary selection implications:

- Words used frequently enough to develop automaticity should be **1-hit accessible** (core words, high-frequency academic words that appear across units)
- Words used infrequently can require navigation (unit-specific fringe vocabulary)
- The motor plan for a word must be consistent every time — "evidence" cannot appear on two different pages or two different button locations
- Pre-programming fringe vocabulary without a consistent home page location defeats motor learning

**Unity/Minspeak semantic compaction:** Uses consistent symbol placement across a grid with multi-meaning symbols accessed through sequences. Motor memory develops because the pattern is consistent, not the grid location. This means symbol placement in vocabulary materials should be stable across lessons.

**Aided Language Modeling / Aided Language Stimulation (ALgS):**
Communication partners model vocabulary on the student's AAC system during instruction. This requires:
- Teachers/paras know where target words live on the student's device
- The Communication Access Packet identifies word locations or suggests where to program them
- Every activity specifies which words a partner should model during instruction

**Symbol-based communication and vocabulary learning:**

Symbol + text pairing is not optional — it is dual coding. When a student sees both the ARASAAC symbol and the printed word simultaneously:
- They form both visual-symbolic and orthographic memory traces
- They build a bridge between symbol communication and text literacy
- They can eventually shift from symbol reliance to text-based access as literacy develops

**Light & McNaughton (2009, 2014, 2025):** Literacy is not separate from AAC — it is the pathway to independence from pre-programmed vocabulary. A student who learns to read and write can communicate anything, not only what has been programmed. Every CbD product should be building toward literacy, not away from it.

**Visual Scene Displays (VSDs) — contextual vs. grid-based vocabulary:**
Traditional grid displays abstract vocabulary from context (a grid of symbols for all emotions, decontextualized). VSDs embed vocabulary in a meaningful, real-world scene. Research (Soto et al., 2019; Drager et al., 2003; PMC8375490) shows VSDs are more effective for beginning communicators and for fiction/narrative vocabulary specifically — because narrative vocabulary is about events and relationships that a scene captures.

Grid displays are more effective for systematic, categorical vocabulary access once communication is established.

## Part 2: The CbD Word Classification System

The core/fringe distinction is necessary but not sufficient. CbD products serve two simultaneous functions — **AAC access** and **vocabulary instruction** — and these require different classification logic.

### Dimension 1: AAC Access Layer

*Answer: Does the student already have this word, or does someone need to add it?*

| Classification | Definition | Example | Product Action |
|---------------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Core** | Already on most robust AAC systems; no pre-programming required | *think, feel, because, stop, want* | Include in Communication Access materials; do not assume daily use without modeling |
| **Fringe** | Unit-specific; not pre-programmed; must be added before the lesson | *disability, radium, ADA, captivity* | **Flag for SLP 1–2 weeks before unit.** Include symbol card. Programming guidance required. |
| **Heart** | Irregular high-frequency word; often already known as a sight word | *the, was, said, have, does* | Note connection to phonics program; reinforce in UFLI context only |

### Dimension 2: Instructional Layer

*Answer: What is this word's role in the actual lesson?*

| Classification | Definition | Example in 504 Sit-In | Product Action |
|---------------|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Explicit instruction target** | This word is central to the standard or skill; students are learning the word itself | *advocate, protest, equal, rights* | Vocabulary Preview Routine; Word Bank; sentence frames; multiple activities; Top 5 priority flag |
| **Background knowledge word** | Student needs to understand this word to access the text, but it is not the instructional focus | *federal, occupy, building, section* | Define in Vocabulary Reference table; no extended teaching activity |
| **Generative/response word** | Student needs this word to produce their evidence-based response | *claim, evidence, because, prove* | Sentence frames; communication board; core word modeling during activities |

### Combined Classification (How to tag a word)

Every vocabulary word in a CbD product should be tagged with **both** dimensions:

| Word | AAC Layer | Instructional Layer | Action |
|------|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| *protest* | Fringe | Explicit instruction target | Top 5; symbol card; vocabulary preview; sentence frames; 2-week lead time |
| *because* | Core | Generative/response word | Model during activities; core word board |
| *federal* | Fringe | Background knowledge | Symbol card; brief definition; not in Top 5 |
| *advocate* | Fringe | Explicit instruction target | Top 5; symbol; multiple activities; Tier 2 treatment |

**The Top 5 Priority Vocabulary selection rule:**
Top 5 Core = core words most essential for expressing the unit's big idea
Top 5 Fringe = fringe words that are both explicit instruction targets AND critical for response

## Part 3: Word Selection Decision Process

This is the sequence for identifying which words belong in any CbD unit's vocabulary layer.

### Step 1: Start with the standard and the learning goal

What does a student need to understand, discuss, and demonstrate about this text in order to show mastery of this standard? List the concepts. Do not list vocabulary yet.

*Example — 504 Sit-In (RI.6.8): A student needs to evaluate how different sources present the same event, assess the reliability of evidence, and make an argument about the significance of the event.*

### Step 2: Identify the Tier 2 words the standard demands

Which academic vocabulary words does this learning goal require — words that are critical across many contexts, not just this text?

*Example: evaluate, evidence, claim, reliable, significant, compare, perspective*

These are Explicit Instruction Targets. They belong in the Vocabulary Preview Routine, Word Bank, sentence frames, and at least 3 activities.

### Step 3: Identify the Tier 3 words this specific content requires

Which topic-specific words must a student understand to access this particular text? These are Background Knowledge words or explicit content targets.

*Example (504 Sit-In): ADA, disability, protest, section, federal, occupation, advocacy*

These get symbol cards and definitions. Some become Explicit Instruction Targets (disability, protest, advocacy = thematically central); others remain Background Knowledge.

### Step 4: Apply the AAC Access lens — core or fringe?

For each word identified above, ask: Is this word likely already on a robust AAC system, or does the SLP need to add it?

Core words (already on systems) → reinforce through modeling, no programming needed
Fringe words (not pre-programmed) → flag for lead time, build symbol card, specify device page location if known

### Step 5: Identify the generative/response vocabulary

What words does a student need to *produce* in order to demonstrate learning? These are almost all core words used in evidence-based response structures.

*I think ___. The text says ___. Because ___. I agree/disagree because ___.*

Core words that appear in every sentence frame: *because, think, feel, show, prove, true, wrong, same, different, agree, disagree*

These go on the communication board for every nonfiction unit activity.

### Step 6: Select Top 5 (per layer)

From the full word list, identify:
- Top 5 Core: the core words most frequently required to express the unit's ideas
- Top 5 Fringe: the fringe words that are both Tier 2/3 instructional targets AND not already on most systems

## Part 4: Product Line Application

### UFLI Phonics

**Nature of vocabulary:** Decode targets, not instruction targets. The words exist to teach phoneme-grapheme patterns — students are learning to decode the words, not learning the words as vocabulary.

**Core/fringe source:** Lesson configs (`ufli_lesson_configs.js`) are authoritative and fixed. Do not add, remove, or reorder. Core/fringe designation = research-based AAC frequency classification, not our invention.

**What "fringe" means in UFLI:** A fringe word in a UFLI lesson is a word that a student using AAC is unlikely to already have programmed. It needs a symbol card so the student can point to the meaning while decoding the form. The decoding demand stays the same — the access to meaning is the scaffold.

**Motor planning implication for UFLI:** The auditory confirmation loop (CbD design principle) closes the loop that motor planning depends on. When a student selects a response, they must hear the phoneme confirmed. The selection without the sound is not a complete phonemic task.

**Vocabulary exploration in the database:** UFLI words in the Vocabulary Explorer reflect which lessons each word appears in, enabling teachers to see cumulative vocabulary load across the program.

---

### Nonfiction Reading Units (Grades 6–10)

**Nature of vocabulary:** Two types simultaneously — Tier 2 academic vocabulary (the standards demand) and Tier 3 content vocabulary (this specific text demands). The Communication Access Packet serves the AAC access function. The unit document serves the instructional function.

**What the current build does well:**
- Core/fringe classification for the CAP is accurate
- Top 5 Priority Vocabulary is correctly identified
- Symbol cards built for all fringe words

**What the framework now clarifies:**
1. Words classified as fringe should also be tagged as Explicit Instruction Target or Background Knowledge — these require different treatment in the unit document
2. The Tier 2 academic words (e.g., *evidence, claim, prove, advocate*) in a unit are often fringe for AAC purposes AND explicit instruction targets. They deserve the most attention across both the unit and the CAP.
3. "Disability" belongs in Capitol Crawl — it is thematically central (ADA, Jennifer Keelan) and should be in the fringe word list for the re-upload.

**Selection rules:**
- Maximum ~20–30 fringe words per unit (beyond this, pre-programming burden becomes impractical)
- Always include the Tier 2 academic words tied to the targeted standard (evidence, argument, reliable, compare, perspective, etc.) — these transfer to other units
- Every unit in the same product line should share core response vocabulary (because, show, prove, true, wrong, same, different) to support motor automaticity across units
- Content fringe words (radium, thalidomide, ADA, boarding school) are unit-specific and labeled accordingly

---

### Fiction Anchor Texts (Grades 3–12)

**Nature of vocabulary:** Fundamentally different from nonfiction. Fiction vocabulary is about emotional and mental states, character attributes, relational language, and narrative structure — not domain content.

**The fiction vocabulary hierarchy (from fiction_reference.md — confirmed by research):**

| Priority | Type | Examples | Why central |
|---------|------|---------|-------------|
| **1st** | Emotional and mental state vocabulary | *feel, want, think, believe, scared, proud, confused, hope, wonder* | PMC7043286: this is the primary access gap for students with disabilities in fiction comprehension |
| **2nd** | Relational/causal language | *because, so, if, then, maybe, probably, decide, change* | Required for inference and character motivation — the heart of RL standards |
| **3rd** | Narrative fringe — description, not name | *tall, old, angry, wears, runs, hides, laughs, home, school* | Enables description-first access without requiring proper noun recall |
| **4th** | Skill-specific vocabulary | *character, setting, problem, trait, narrator, theme* | Unit-specific; tied directly to the targeted RL standard |

**Character names are always fringe.** Design for description. Never require name recall.

**Visual Scene Display principle:** For fiction, VSDs outperform decontextualized symbol cards for key scenes (PMC8375490). Vocabulary embedded in a scene is more accessible and less cognitively demanding than a grid of isolated symbols.

**Motor planning for fiction:** The emotional vocabulary words (*feel, want, scared, happy, hope, change*) are core words — they should be 1-hit accessible on any robust system. Character name vocabulary is fringe and may require navigation. Design activities so fringe is never the required response pathway.

---

### Picture Book Companions (K–3)

**Nature of vocabulary:** Interactive read-aloud vocabulary — the words children need to engage with a shared text through pointing, signing, device output, or eye gaze, and the vocabulary that extends thinking after reading.

**Key research:** Dialogic reading (Whitehurst et al.) + Aided Language Stimulation during read-aloud. Every vocabulary word in a picture book companion should be:
1. Visually representable with ARASAAC symbol
2. Modelable on a simple AAC system during the read-aloud
3. Reachable with a 1–2 touch motor plan

At K–3, the Tier distinction collapses somewhat — words like *brave, choose, belong, equal, fair* function as both Tier 1 (accessible to most students) and Tier 2 (conceptually rich, worth discussing). The companion's job is to make those concepts accessible through multiple access modes.

**For All the Way to the Top (Jennifer Keelan):**
Core vocabulary: *want, help, not stop, try, do, can, I, me, we, up, go, right, because*
Fringe vocabulary: *crawl, steps, Capitol, wheelchair, ADA, Jennifer, brave, rights, law, change*
High-priority picture book emotional vocabulary: *determined, fair, equal, belong, brave, give up*

---

### Chapter Books (Future line)

**Nature of vocabulary:** Sustained vocabulary development across 8–20+ chapters. The key difference from fiction anchor text is the cumulative load — students encounter the same character vocabulary, relational vocabulary, and thematic vocabulary over weeks.

**Framework additions:**
- Track which vocabulary words first appear and in which chapter
- Build a vocabulary progression (words introduced early should be reviewed/reused in later activities)
- Motor plan consideration: Words that appear in the first three chapters should be pre-programmed — not just chapter-by-chapter fringe lists
- Core vocabulary for the book-level discussion is established in Lesson 1 and used throughout

## Part 5: SGD Motor Planning Considerations

These guidelines apply to all product lines when making decisions about vocabulary programming.

### What should be pre-programmed (1-hit / home page)
- All identified core vocabulary for the unit
- Tier 2 academic vocabulary words that appear in 3+ lessons of the unit
- Response vocabulary (sentence frame words: *because, show, agree, think, claim*)
- High-frequency emotion vocabulary for fiction units

### What can be accessed through navigation
- Unit-specific fringe content vocabulary (used in 1–3 activities)
- Character proper nouns in fiction
- Domain-specific Tier 3 words

### Programming lead time guidance
- **Minimum:** 1 week before unit begins
- **Recommended:** 2 weeks — SLP receives fringe list, programs device, models new vocabulary in context before instruction starts
- **Communication Access Packet** is the handoff document. It must be clear enough that an SLP unfamiliar with the unit can program the device correctly.

### Consistent location rule
If a word is referenced in the Communication Access Packet, it should appear in a consistent location across all activities in that unit. Do not move core vocabulary locations between activities. Motor automaticity depends on consistency.

### Aided Language Stimulation note
Every teacher support document in a CbD product should include guidance on which words a communication partner should model during activities, and approximately where those words live on a typical robust AAC system (QuickTalker, Snap Core, LAMP Words for Life, etc.). This is not device-specific — it is partner training.

## Part 6: Symbol Selection and the ARASAAC Standard

CbD uses ARASAAC symbols (GPL 3.0 / Creative Commons attribution) as the standard symbol set for all products. This is consistent with the symbol_cache in `_Operations/symbol_cache/`.

### Symbol selection rules
1. **Symbol + text together** — always. Never a symbol alone in a CbD product. The text supports literacy development.
2. **One symbol per word** — consistency matters. Do not use different ARASAAC symbols for the same word across products.
3. **Fitzgerald Key border** — symbol card border color = Fitzgerald Key category. This is not decorative — it is a vocabulary organization system.
4. **If no ARASAAC symbol exists** — flag for custom illustration. Do not substitute a visually similar but semantically wrong symbol.
5. **Culturally specific fringe words** — if a symbol has cultural accuracy concerns (indigenous identity, historical context), flag for review before production.

### Fitzgerald Key application
| Color | Category | Example words |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Yellow | People / Pronouns | *I, you, he, she, we, they* |
| Green | Actions / Verbs | *go, want, stop, fight, help, show* |
| Orange | Descriptions / Adjectives | *big, brave, different, same, safe* |
| White / Gray | Nouns (default) | *whale, factory, school, Capitol* |
| Blue | Prepositions / Time / Location | *in, on, at, before, after, because* |
| Pink | Social / Feelings / Questions | *feel, want, what, who, please, sorry* |

Note: *because* in CbD is classified blue (Preposition/Time/Location — it marks logical relationship). This is a deliberate choice aligned with how core vocabulary systems typically classify it.

## Part 7: The CbD Vocabulary QC Checklist

Use this checklist for every new product or product line before build begins.

### Gate 1: Does this product belong in the CbD ecosystem?
- [ ] Is the product grounded in a specific ELA standard (RL, RI, L, or phonics/literacy)?
- [ ] Does the product serve students with AAC or complex communication needs specifically?
- [ ] Is there a gap in the market — no existing product does this with AAC access?
- [ ] Can the vocabulary in this product be classified through the CbD framework?

### Gate 2: Word Selection (pre-build)
- [ ] Have the Tier 2 academic vocabulary words been identified (the standard demands these)?
- [ ] Have the Tier 3 content vocabulary words been identified (this text demands these)?
- [ ] Has the AAC Access Layer been applied (core vs. fringe for each word)?
- [ ] Has the Instructional Layer been applied (explicit instruction target / background / generative)?
- [ ] Is the total fringe word count reasonable for pre-programming (≤30)?
- [ ] Have Top 5 Core and Top 5 Fringe been identified?

### Gate 3: Symbol Coverage
- [ ] Does every fringe word have an ARASAAC symbol in the symbol_cache?
- [ ] Are missing symbols flagged for resolution before build?
- [ ] Do all symbols use the correct Fitzgerald Key border color?

### Gate 4: Motor Planning
- [ ] Are core/response vocabulary words identified as 1-hit accessible?
- [ ] Is the SLP lead time guidance included in the Communication Access Packet?
- [ ] Is aided language modeling guidance included in the teacher document?
- [ ] Is vocabulary location consistent across all activities in the unit?

### Gate 5: Instructional Coverage
- [ ] Does each Explicit Instruction Target appear in at least 3 activities?
- [ ] Is there a Vocabulary Preview Routine?
- [ ] Are sentence frames provided using response vocabulary + fringe targets?
- [ ] Are there multiple response pathways (symbol, verbal/gestural, generative)?

### Gate 6: Product Line Coherence
- [ ] Does this product's vocabulary share response/generative core words with other products in the same line?
- [ ] Would a student who has completed another product in this line have a motor advantage in accessing this vocabulary?
- [ ] Does the vocabulary support literacy development (symbol + text, word bank, word morphology)?
