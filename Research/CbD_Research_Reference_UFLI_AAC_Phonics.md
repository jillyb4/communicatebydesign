## Key Findings & Evidence

### 1. UFLI Foundations is an evidence-based, free phonics curriculum aligned to the Science of Reading

**Finding:** UFLI Foundations is published by the University of Florida Literacy Institute and is available free of charge. It teaches letter-sound correspondences systematically and cumulatively across 128 lessons, beginning with individual phonemes and building through CVC words, consonant clusters, digraphs, and beyond.

**Source:** University of Florida Literacy Institute (UFLI). *UFLI Foundations.* ufli.education.ufl.edu. (No individual author cited; institutional publication.)

**Why it matters for practice:** UFLI is rapidly becoming a dominant phonics program in U.S. schools. CbD supplemental materials aligned to its scope and sequence can be adopted without requiring teachers to learn a new instructional framework — they learn the adaptation layer, not a new curriculum.

---

### 2. The UFLI 8-step lesson structure is predictable and replicable across all 128 lessons

**Finding:** Every UFLI lesson follows the same sequence: (1) Phonemic Awareness — blend and segment, (2) Visual Drill — grapheme review, (3) Auditory Drill — phoneme review, (4) Blending Drill — cumulative word chains, (5) New Concept — introduction script, articulatory gesture, sound wall, letter formation, (6) Word Work — manipulative letter building, (7) Irregular Words — review and teach, (8) Connected Text — read and spell sentences plus decodable text.

**Source:** UFLI Foundations teacher's manual (transcribed directly in this session, Lessons 1–34).

**Why it matters for practice:** Steps 1–4 review prior learning; steps 5–8 address the new concept. This structure is the foundation for CbD's supplemental product design — materials that mirror this routine can be used in any order by any teacher who knows UFLI without additional training.

### 4. UFLI distinguishes stop phonemes from continuous phonemes in articulatory gesture instruction

**Finding:** The manual explicitly labels phonemes as either stop sounds (cannot be stretched out; use stop hand motion) or continuous sounds (can be stretched out; use continuous hand motion). Stop consonants in Lessons 1–34: /p/, /t/, /k/, /g/, /b/, /d/, /j/. Continuous consonants: /m/, /n/, /s/, /f/, /l/, /r/, /v/, /z/, /w/, /y/, /h/. All five short vowels are continuous.

**Source:** UFLI Foundations teacher's manual, articulatory gesture sections across Lessons 1–34.

**Why it matters for practice:** This distinction is critical for AAC users. Continuous phonemes can be modeled via auditory confirmation without ambiguity. Stop phonemes require more careful partner production to avoid adding a schwa (/tuh/ instead of /t/). Teacher training materials must address this distinction explicitly.

---

### 5. Word structure complexity follows a deliberate progression across the first 34 lessons

**Finding:** Word structure introduced in sequence: VC (Lesson 5), CVC (Lesson 5), CVCC (Lesson 22, introduced with /sk/ cluster), CCVC (Lessons 22–25, with /sk/, /sn/, /sm/, /br/, /cr/, etc.). Four-sound words appear first at Lesson 20 (-s plural). Consonant clusters are not introduced until Lesson 22, ensuring students have solid CVC automaticity first.

**Source:** UFLI Foundations teacher's manual, blending drill progressions and word lists, Lessons 1–34.

**Why it matters for practice:** For AAC users, each increase in word length increases the motor demand of selection-based responses (more choices, more steps). Materials should track word complexity alongside lesson number, and response format complexity should be calibrated accordingly.

### 6. The nasalized vowel (Lesson 11) is a distinct phonological concept requiring explicit instruction

**Finding:** When /m/ or /n/ follows short /ă/, the vowel changes quality — it becomes nasalized. This is not a new grapheme but a phonological environment rule. The manual teaches it explicitly with the analogy "makes the A sound kind of whiny." Words like *am*, *an*, *man*, *fan*, *Pam* all use this pattern. This is students' first introduction to a phoneme that varies by context rather than by spelling.

**Source:** UFLI Foundations teacher's manual, Lesson 11, pages 82–83.

**Why it matters for practice:** For AAC users, this lesson is a hidden complexity point. The symbol or picture cue for /ă/ may not adequately represent the nasalized version. SLP involvement is particularly important here to ensure the student's AAC vocabulary captures this distinction.

---

### 7. The -s /z/ rule (Lesson 21) is a voicing distinction, not a new grapheme

**Finding:** Lesson 21 teaches that S spells /z/ when it follows a voiced sound (dogs, fans, bins). This is not introduced as a new letter but as a rule about the same letter behaving differently in voiced environments. The manual explicitly states that /s/ and /z/ are produced identically except for voicing.

**Source:** UFLI Foundations teacher's manual, Lesson 21, pages 102–103.

**Why it matters for practice:** IEP goal language for decoding should acknowledge this distinction. A student who reads *dogs* as /dŏg-s/ rather than /dŏg-z/ has a specific phonological gap, not a decoding gap. Data collection on -s words should distinguish /s/ and /z/ endings.

### 8. The letter X represents two phonemes; QU represents two phonemes — both require dual instructional framing

**Finding:** Lessons 31 (x) and 32 (qu) each explicitly distinguish between blending tasks (treat as one unit: /ks/, /kw/) and segmenting/spelling tasks (treat as two separate phonemes: /k/+/s/, /k/+/w/). The manual uses two different notations in the same lesson intentionally. X does not appear on the sound wall because it represents a combination.

**Source:** UFLI Foundations teacher's manual, Lessons 31–32, pages 122–125.

**Why it matters for practice:** AAC phonics keyboards (e.g., PRC-Saltillo recorded phoneme buttons) would need separate handling for X and QU — both as blended units for reading and as component phonemes for spelling. This is an identified design challenge for the phonemic keyboard problem.

### 9. Students with complex communication needs (CCN) can develop phonemic awareness — speech production is not a prerequisite

**Finding:** Multiple independent research lines confirm that nonspeaking students can develop phonological awareness. The key predictor is language comprehension and exposure to letters and sounds, not speech production ability.

**Sources and evidence:**
- Bishop & Robson (1989). *British Journal of Psychology.* Individuals with congenital inability to speak could spell nonwords — proving phoneme-grapheme mapping without articulation.
- Foley & Pollatsek (1999). 12 participants with severe congenital speech impairments (6 completely anarthric) could judge phonological similarity of printed words. Concluded: "Articulatory coding is therefore not a necessary part of the phonological coding process."
- Dahlgren Sandberg & Hjelmquist (1996, 1997). No differences in phonological awareness between nonspeaking preschoolers with cerebral palsy and matched speaking controls.
- Card & Dodd (2006). *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.* Replicated above findings — no group differences for syllable identification, nonword reading, or spoken rhyme judgment.
- Barton-Hulsey, Sevcik, & Romski (2018). *American Journal of Speech-Language Pathology.* "Speech ability did not predict a significant amount of variance in phonological awareness beyond what would be expected by developmental skills of receptive language and print and letter-sound knowledge."
- Peeters, Verhoeven, de Moor, & van Balkom (2009). *Research in Developmental Disabilities.* Speech production was the strongest predictor of early reading success for children with CP — but facilitative, not prerequisite. Speech supports working memory manipulation tasks particularly.
- Barker, Saunders, & Brady (2012). *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.* Systematic review found only 8 studies (26 children total) attempting phonological awareness and word reading instruction with AAC users — thin but consistent evidence base.
- Light, McNaughton, Holyfield, et al. (2025). *AAC.* "Research has demonstrated that individuals with developmental disabilities who need or use AAC can successfully acquire a wide range of literacy skills when provided with effective tools and evidence-based, adapted instruction."

**Why it matters for practice:** The assumption that AAC users are "not ready for phonics" is not supported by research. Limiting this population to sight-word programs (Edmark, PCI) denies access to generative reading skills. Every IEP team should be able to cite this evidence when advocating for phonics instruction for nonspeaking students.

### 10. Limited literacy outcomes in students with CCN are caused by insufficient opportunity to learn, not by disability

**Finding:** Approximately 90% of school-age students with extensive support needs and CCN are educated in separate classrooms with little specialized literacy instruction. Limited skills result from restricted access, not from the disability profile itself.

**Sources:**
- Benson-Goldberg & Erickson (2024). Students make gains when they have access to AAC systems during instruction. Time devoted to instruction is not translating into increased opportunity for individual students.
- Browder et al. (cited in UFLI-AAC analysis). Only 10% of 128 studies on reading for students with moderate to severe intellectual disabilities targeted phonics.
- Erickson & Koppenhaver. *Comprehensive Literacy for All.* Brookes Publishing, 2020. Established that no different curriculum is required — the barriers are access, methodology, and opportunity.

**Why it matters for practice:** This reframes the IEP conversation. The question is not "Is this student ready for phonics?" but "Has this student been given adequate opportunity and adapted instruction?" Documentation of instructional opportunity is as important as documentation of skill level.

### 11. The auditory confirmation loop is a defined, stage-dependent practice in alternative pencil instruction

**Finding:** The auditory confirmation loop = the partner immediately produces a verbal response to every student letter selection. The *content* of that response depends on instructional stage:
- **Alphabet development stage:** Partner says the letter *name* ("That's M") — student is learning letter identity.
- **Phonics/UFLI stage:** Partner says the *phoneme* (/m/) — not the letter name, not "correct." Student already knows the letter and is now connecting it to sound.
Mixing these two signals in the same session creates competing input and interferes with both tracks.

**Source:** CbD Research Reference: Alternative Pencils and Emergent Literacy for Complex Communicators (March 2026), drawing on Hanser (2007, 2009) and CLDS protocols. Coined as a named practice by CbD.

**Why it matters for practice:** Partner training is the highest-leverage intervention for AAC literacy. Partners who say "correct!" instead of "/m/" are inadvertently disrupting the phoneme-grapheme connection being built. Training on auditory confirmation content is a discrete, teachable skill that paraprofessionals and families can learn.

### 12. The ALL (Accessible Literacy Learning) Reading Program is the most rigorously validated phonics program for AAC users

**Finding:** Developed by Light and McNaughton at Penn State. Teaches: sound blending, phoneme segmentation, letter-sound correspondence, single-word decoding, sight word recognition, and shared reading comprehension — entirely through touch, eye gaze, or scanning without verbal output. Uses continuous blending. Validated with children ages 3–16 with autism, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, and developmental apraxia.

**Key study:** Fallon, Light, McNaughton, Drager, & Hammer (2004). *Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research.* 3 of 5 participants successfully decoded untaught novel words — demonstrating true phonological generalization, not memorization.

**Why it matters for practice:** ALL is the research-validated proof of concept. CbD materials are supplemental to, not replacing, approaches like ALL. When recommending resources to IEP teams, ALL and aacliteracy.psu.edu should be first referrals.

### 13. Kate Ahern has specifically adapted UFLI Foundations for AAC users

**Finding:** Ahern uses UFLI as the core curriculum at Ahern Academy and presented "Literacy and Learners with CCN Using UFLI" at AAC in the Cloud, 2023. Her adaptations include: adjusted pacing, visual supports for lesson flow, choice arrays for active engagement, AAC modeling during instruction, minimum 2 hours of daily literacy instruction embedded across the school day.

**Source:** AAC in the Cloud presentation, 2023. Kate Ahern.

**Why it matters for practice:** Ahern's work validates the CbD product premise directly. POPARD (Provincial Outreach Program for Autism and Related Disorders, BC) has created free adapted UFLI lesson plans available at autismoutreach.ca — both confirm the gap CbD is building to fill.

## Practical Implications

### AT/AAC Service Delivery
- Phonics instruction requires **purpose-built materials separate from the AAC device.** The device is a communication tool — reconfiguring it for phonics drills risks disrupting its primary function and the student's sense of communication safety.
- Each UFLI lesson step maps to a specific response demand. SLPs should audit each step to identify where verbal output is assumed and design alternative response formats before instruction begins.
- The auditory confirmation loop requires partner training. SLPs should deliver this training to paraprofessionals and families as a discrete, named skill, not assume it will happen naturally.
- Access method evaluation (direct selection, partner-assisted scanning, eye gaze, switch scanning) determines which lesson steps need adapted materials and which can proceed without modification.

### IEP Team Capacity-Building
- IEP teams can cite the Benson-Goldberg & Erickson (2024) and Barton-Hulsey et al. (2018) findings to reframe eligibility conversations: the question is not whether the student can do phonics, but whether they have had adequate opportunity.
- Goal language should specify **response modality alongside the phonological skill.** Example: "When presented with a spoken CVC word and a field of three letter cards, student will identify the initial sound via [eye gaze/touch/partner-assisted scanning] with 80% accuracy across 4 of 5 sessions."
- Data collection should capture prompt level alongside accuracy. A data sheet that says only "correct/incorrect" without capturing modality and prompt level is insufficient for SDI documentation.
- Browder et al.'s finding (only 10% of 128 studies targeted phonics) should be used in IEP meetings where teams attempt to justify sight-word-only programming.

### Instructional Design
- CbD materials must use the same 8-step lesson structure as UFLI to allow immediate adoption by teachers who know the program.
- Three differentiation levels must be built into every activity: errorless (1 choice), emerging (2 choices), independent (3-4 choices).
- Word lists for each lesson are bounded by the cumulative phoneme set at that lesson — no letter can appear that has not yet been taught.
- Word complexity (VC → CVC → CCVC → CVCC) should be tracked per lesson and matched to AAC response field complexity.
- The auditory confirmation loop must be scripted into every teacher script card — including whether to produce a phoneme or a letter name, depending on lesson stage.

### Family Support
- The alternative pencil framework begins at the pre-alphabet stage — families do not need to wait for school readiness before beginning. Any letter-access tool counts.
- Home literacy environments are documented predictors of literacy outcomes (Koppenhaver, Evans & Yoder, 1991; Light & Kelford Smith, 1993). Family training is evidence-supported, not optional.
- Parents can ask IEP teams four specific questions: What phonics curriculum do you use? How is my child expected to respond? What happens when they can't speak the answer? What does the data look like, and how is prompt level tracked?

## Terms & Definitions

**Alternative pencil:** Anything that provides a person with access to all 26 letters of the alphabet for the purpose of writing, when a standard pencil is inaccessible. Examples: adapted keyboard, eye-gaze letter frame, flip chart alphabet, partner-assisted scanning alphabet, symbol-based AAC keyboard, velcro letter cards. (Hanser, 2009, CLDS/UNC Chapel Hill)

**Auditory confirmation loop:** The practice in which a communication partner immediately produces a verbal response — either the letter name (alphabet stage) or the phoneme (phonics stage) — every time a student makes a letter selection during literacy instruction. (CbD coined term, named practice drawn from CLDS protocols)

**Continuous phoneme:** A phoneme that can be stretched out and held without distortion (e.g., /m/, /s/, /f/, /l/, /r/, short vowels). Contrasts with stop phonemes. Associated with continuous hand motion in UFLI instruction.

**Stop phoneme:** A phoneme that cannot be stretched out — it terminates upon production (e.g., /p/, /t/, /k/, /b/, /d/, /g/, /j/). Associated with a stop hand motion in UFLI. Schwa addition (/tuh/ for /t/) is a common error to prevent.

**Nasalized vowel:** The quality change that occurs when short /ă/ precedes /m/ or /n/, making it sound slightly different than in other environments (e.g., *am*, *an*, *man*). Introduced explicitly in UFLI Lesson 11.

**Grapheme-phoneme correspondence (GPC):** The relationship between a written letter or letter combination (grapheme) and the sound it represents (phoneme). The foundational unit of phonics instruction.

**Scope and sequence:** The ordered progression of skills in a phonics curriculum — which graphemes are introduced, in what order, and how they build on each other.

**Blending drill (UFLI Step 4):** A cumulative chain of words using previously taught graphemes, practiced before introducing new content. Example: *at → mat → sat → at.* Builds automaticity with known sounds before adding new ones.

**Word work (UFLI Step 6):** Manipulative letter building in which students physically move letter tiles to form and transform words following a teacher-directed chain. The first step in UFLI where students produce words (rather than read or spell aloud).

**Constant time delay (CTD):** A prompting procedure in which the teacher initially provides the prompt at a zero-second delay (immediate model), then introduces a fixed delay (typically 4 seconds) to allow independent responding before prompting. The most research-validated prompting procedure for phonics instruction with students with CCN. (Ahlgrim-Delzell et al., 2016)

**Errorless learning:** A structured approach in which activities are designed so students make as few errors as possible — using highly scaffolded choice arrays (Stage 1: 1 choice; Stage 2: 2 choices; Stage 3: 3-4 choices; Stage 4: full independent array).

**Specially Designed Instruction (SDI):** Under IDEA, adaptation of the content, methodology, or delivery of instruction to address the unique needs of a student with a disability. For phonics with CCN students, this means adapted response modes — not different content.

**Complex communication needs (CCN):** Refers to individuals whose speech is insufficient to meet all of their daily communication needs, requiring AAC systems. Includes nonspeaking individuals and those with limited functional speech.

**ALL (Accessible Literacy Learning):** A validated phonics program from Penn State (Light & McNaughton) designed specifically for AAC users, teaching phonics through touch, eye gaze, or scanning without verbal output. Available at aacliteracy.psu.edu.

**Decodable text:** Connected reading passages that use only grapheme-phoneme correspondences already taught, allowing students to apply newly learned phonics skills independently without guessing from context.

**High frequency words / Irregular words (UFLI):** Words that contain spelling patterns not yet taught or that include irregular elements. UFLI marks these with asterisks when "temporarily irregular" — they will become decodable once later patterns are taught. Tracked separately from fully decodable words.

## Gaps & Follow-Up

**Gap 1 — Articulatory gesture adaptation for nonspeaking students is not addressed in UFLI**
UFLI relies on students modeling and producing articulatory gestures (open your mouth, put your lips together, etc.). No published protocol exists for teaching articulatory awareness to students who cannot produce the sounds. Research on motor-visual approaches (watching mouth in mirror, using articulatory photos) exists but is not integrated into UFLI materials. CbD materials should address this — possibly by referencing mouth-photo sound walls and establishing a partner observation protocol.

**Gap 2 — The phonemic keyboard problem is partially resolved but no universal solution exists**
PRC-Saltillo has built-in recorded phoneme buttons and digraph keys. AssistiveWare (Proloquo2Go) has taken a position against phonics keyboards. Tobii Dynavox is neutral (ALL app sold separately). No single AAC system handles all 44 phonemes with appropriate recorded sounds by default. IEP teams need guidance on evaluating their specific device's phonics capacity — CbD Article 3 is planned but not yet published.

**Gap 3 — Inner voice instruction for AAC users is underresearched**
Erickson, Koppenhaver, and Cunningham (2017) assert that inner voice can and should be taught explicitly to AAC users, but the specific instructional procedures are not well-documented in peer-reviewed literature. This is noted in the project knowledge base as a clinical gap. Jane Farrall addresses it in practice-level writing but formal research is thin.

**Gap 4 — Nasalized vowel handling in AAC symbol systems**
No research identified on how common AAC symbol systems (ARASAAC, Mayer-Johnson, Mulberry) represent or distinguish the nasalized /ă/ quality. Symbol cards for phonics lessons at the Lesson 11 level may carry this ambiguity without resolution. Needs SLP input for individual student AAC vocabulary review.

**Gap 5 — Assessment instruments for phonological awareness without verbal output have limited validation samples**
The DAPA-AP (Barker, Bridges, & Saunders, 2014) was validated with 17 adults with intellectual disabilities. The APAR (Iacono & Cupples, 2004) was designed for non-speech administration but has limited published norms. Neither has been validated specifically with the school-age population that UFLI targets (K–2). IEP teams using these tools should document this limitation in evaluation reports.

**Gap 6 — Word complexity calibration for AAC response fields across Lessons 1–34 is not yet built**
The transcription document captures word lists per lesson but does not yet map these to recommended AAC response field size and format (number of choices, layout, visual contrast requirements) by lesson. This is a product development gap — needed before student materials can be designed for lessons beyond the pilot set.

**Gap 7 — Consonant cluster instruction (Lessons 22–27) and AAC response complexity**
Lessons 22–27 introduce CCVC and CCVCC words rapidly. The interaction between word length, response field complexity, and motor/scanning demands for AAC users at this stage has not been modeled in CbD materials. A pacing decision (slow down at Lessons 22–27, or provide additional scaffolding) needs to be made before materials at this level are built.
