# CbD IEP Goal Ecosystem
*Communicate by Design | Compiled: 2026-03-29 | Version 1.0*

---

## Purpose and Use

This document defines the CbD IEP Goal Ecosystem — the framework for how Communicate by Design products support IEP teams in writing legally defensible, measurable annual goals that connect to the CbD instructional units. It is a support infrastructure document, not a product design document.

**The critical distinction:** CbD products are not IEP goals. The product is the instructional vehicle. The IEP goal is the legal commitment the team makes about what the student will demonstrate and how progress will be measured. The product supports the goal; the goal does not create the product.

IEP goal stems in CbD units exist so that a teacher who opens the unit can see, clearly, what a well-written goal connected to this unit would look like. The goal stem is a starting point — not a required form, not a compliance document, and not a substitute for the team's professional judgment about the individual student.

**This framework answers four operational questions:**

1. **What role do IEP goals play in the CbD ecosystem?** (Support infrastructure, not the product)
2. **What does IDEA require for measurable annual goals?** (Legal minimum standard)
3. **How do CbD products connect to IEP goals without writing them?** (Goal stem design)
4. **What is the accommodation vs. modification distinction, and why does it matter for goal writing?** (Legal defensibility)

## Part 1: The Legal Foundation

### IDEA and Measurable Annual Goals

**Primary source: IDEA 2004, 34 CFR §300.320(a)(2)**

IDEA requires every IEP to include:

> "A statement of measurable annual goals, including academic and functional goals, designed to: (i) Meet the child's needs that result from the child's disability to enable the child to be involved in and make progress in the general education curriculum; and (ii) Meet each of the child's other educational needs that result from the child's disability."

Three elements are non-negotiable:
1. **Measurable** — the goal must specify how progress will be measured (frequency, accuracy, consistency)
2. **Annual** — the goal represents one year of expected progress
3. **Connected to the general education curriculum** — the goal is not a separate curriculum; it enables participation in the same curriculum as peers

**What this means for CbD:**

CbD products target ELA standards from the general education curriculum. When a special educator uses a CbD nonfiction unit, the academic IEP goals that unit supports should reference the same ELA standards. The product provides the instructional vehicle; the IEP provides the commitment.

---

### The Endrew F. Standard

**Primary source: Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District, 580 U.S. 386 (2017)**

The Supreme Court in *Endrew F.* clarified the FAPE standard: a student's IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances."

The "de minimis" standard — that any progress, no matter how small, satisfies FAPE — was explicitly rejected. The *Endrew F.* standard requires ambitious goals, not minimal ones.

**The CbD implication:** IEP goals connected to CbD units should reflect genuine progress expectations — not "exposure to grade-level content," but measurable movement toward demonstrated skill.

---

### Accommodation vs. Modification — Goal Writing Implications

**Primary source: IDEA 34 CFR §300.160; Parent Center Hub (aligned with IDEA definitions)**

The accommodation/modification distinction directly governs how IEP goals should be framed when a student uses CbD's V1/V2/V3 system.

**Accommodation:** Same standard and content expectations; different access method.
- Goal language: "Given [V2 adapted text + sentence frame support], [student] will [skill] as measured by [rubric], achieving [criterion]..."
- The standard in the goal is the same grade-level standard as the general education classroom

**Modification:** Different or reduced standard; lowered content expectation.
- Used exclusively for students with IEPs who qualify for alternate achievement standards (the 1% rule)
- IDEA §300.160 prohibits modified academic achievement standards for all other students

**The practical test:** When writing a goal connected to a CbD unit, ask: "Is the ELA standard in this goal the same standard that general education students are working toward, just with a different access method?" If yes → accommodation-based goal. If the standard itself has been reduced → modification, and the legal basis needs to be documented.

**Most CbD users are working with accommodation-based goals.** The V1/V2/V3 system is designed to make this possible — the standard is the same across all versions.

## Part 2: The CbD Goal Stem — What It Is and What It Is Not

### What It Is

A CbD IEP goal stem is a model sentence that demonstrates the structure of a measurable annual goal connected to the unit's skill and standard. It follows IDEA's required elements:

```
Given [condition: version + specific supports],
[student] will [observable behavior verb] [skill + content standard]
as measured by [data method],
achieving [performance criterion + consistency criterion] by [date].
```

**Purpose:** To show teachers what a well-constructed goal for this unit's skill looks like, and to save time on the mechanics of IDEA-compliant goal language.

**What the goal stem is NOT:**
- A required IEP goal — teams write the actual goal; the stem is a starting point
- A compliance guarantee — the team's professional judgment about the individual student must be applied
- A substitute for SLP input on AAC-related goals
- Appropriate for every student — some students will have goals in foundational literacy skills, not grade-level ELA analysis

### Observable Verb Requirement

IDEA requires goals to be measurable. "Measurable" means the behavior must be observable — a different person reading the data must be able to determine whether the student met the criterion. This rules out a specific set of verbs:

**Never use in goal stems:**
- understand, know, appreciate, be aware of, learn

These verbs describe internal states, not observable behaviors.

**Observable verbs by CbD skill type:**

| Skill | Observable Verbs |
|-------|----------------|
| Close Reading / Annotation | identify, annotate, cite, locate, mark |
| Author's Purpose / Perspective | explain, distinguish, compare, defend, support with evidence |
| CER (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning) | state, identify, select, write, construct |
| Text Structure | label, organize, sequence, categorize |
| Sourcing / Corroboration | evaluate, rate, compare, justify |
| AAC communication output | indicate, select, produce, use [specify modality] |

## Part 3: Goal Stem Examples by Unit and Skill

These stems are starting points. Teams adjust for the individual student's baseline, IEP date, and team judgment.

### Nonfiction Units — Academic Goals

**Close Reading / Annotation (Keiko, Radium Girls)**

> Given adapted nonfiction text at [V2/V3 Lexile level] and an annotation code card, [student] will identify and mark text evidence using correct annotation codes (e.g., [DETAIL], [CONNECTION], [QUESTION]) as measured by rubric scoring on annotation activities, achieving correct annotation on 4 of 5 text selections across 3 consecutive sessions by [date].

**Text Structure — Cause and Effect (Zitkala-Ša)**

> Given [V2/V3] adapted nonfiction text and a graphic organizer, [student] will identify a cause and its effect with supporting text evidence as measured by rubric scoring on Text Structure activities, achieving 2 cause-effect pairs with cited evidence on 3 of 4 trials by [date].

**Author's Purpose / Perspective (504 Sit-In)**

> Given [V2/V3] adapted nonfiction text and a Perspective Comparison chart, [student] will identify two different perspectives on the same historical event and cite text evidence for each as measured by rubric scoring on Perspective Comparison activities, achieving 2 perspectives with cited evidence on 3 of 4 trials by [date].

**CER (Frances Kelsey)**

> Given [V2/V3] adapted nonfiction text and a CER frame, [student] will construct a written claim supported by 2 pieces of aligned evidence as measured by rubric scoring on CER activities, achieving claim + 2 evidence pieces aligned on 4 of 5 trials by [date].

**Sourcing / Corroboration (Capitol Crawl)**

> Given [V2/V3] adapted nonfiction text and 3–4 primary and secondary sources, [student] will evaluate source strength and provide a justification using text evidence as measured by rubric scoring on Source Analysis activities, achieving 3 of 4 sources correctly rated with justification on 2 consecutive sessions by [date].

---

### AAC Communication Goals (Cross-Unit)

AAC goals connected to CbD units target communication behavior within the instructional context — separate from academic skill goals.

**Multi-symbol utterance output:**

> Given a CbD nonfiction lesson with a pre-programmed communication device and a communication partner using Aided Language Stimulation, [student] will produce a 2+ symbol utterance in response to comprehension questions as measured by Communication Session Tracker data, achieving 4 of 5 opportunities across 2 consecutive sessions with 2 different partners by [date].

**CER via AAC:**

> Given a CbD CER activity with a pre-programmed fringe vocabulary set and communication partner support, [student] will construct a claim + evidence response using AAC output (any modality) as measured by Communication Session Tracker data, achieving a complete CER via AAC on 3 of 5 trials by [date].

**Fringe vocabulary use in context:**

> Given a CbD nonfiction unit with pre-programmed topic-specific vocabulary, [student] will use a pre-programmed fringe vocabulary word in a contextually appropriate response during the lesson as measured by Communication Session Tracker data, achieving 4 of 5 opportunities across 2 sessions with 2 different partners by [date].

## Part 4: The Two-Goal Model

For students who use AAC in a CbD unit, two separate goals are recommended — one for academic skill and one for communication:

| Goal Type | What It Measures | Data Tool |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Academic ELA goal** | Did the student demonstrate the skill the standard requires? | CbD rubric on student response document |
| **AAC communication goal** | Did the student's communication output develop within the instructional context? | Communication Session Tracker |

**Why this matters:** Academic progress and communication progress are not the same thing and should not be collapsed into a single goal. A student can meet the academic criterion using a sentence frame and still not have demonstrated communication growth. A student can produce multi-symbol AAC output and still not meet the rubric criterion for the ELA skill. Both are meaningful; they require separate measurement and separate IEP goals.

## Part 5: Connecting Products to Goals — The Practical Workflow

When a teacher opens a CbD unit and wants to connect it to an existing or upcoming IEP goal:

**Step 1: Identify the skill and standard.**
What is this unit designed to teach? (The unit cover page names the standard and the skill type.)

**Step 2: Check the student's current IEP goals.**
Is there an existing goal for this skill? If yes, the CbD unit provides the instructional vehicle for that goal. The goal stays the same; the product is how the teacher delivers the instruction.

**Step 3: If no goal exists and this is a priority skill:**
Use the CbD goal stem as a starting point. Adjust for the student's baseline (from the current IEP data), the IEP annual review date, and the team's knowledge of the student's pace.

**Step 4: Confirm the goal is accommodation-based.**
Apply the accommodation/modification test. The ELA standard in the goal should be the same standard peers are working toward, with access accommodations named in the condition.

**Step 5: Confirm the data method.**
The "as measured by" clause in the goal should reference the CbD rubric, the Communication Session Tracker, or another method that is already embedded in the unit. Data collection cannot require additional forms the team will not use.

## Part 6: Common IEP Goal Errors and Corrections

| Error | Correction |
|-------|-----------|
| Goal uses "understand" or "know" | Replace with an observable verb from the skill type table |
| Goal has no performance criterion (how accurate?) | Add accuracy/frequency: "4 of 5 trials," "80% accuracy" |
| Goal has no consistency criterion (across how many sessions?) | Add: "across 3 consecutive sessions" |
| Goal has no data method ("as measured by...") | Name the tool: "as measured by rubric scoring on CER activities" |
| Academic and AAC goals are collapsed into one | Separate into two goals with separate data tools |
| Goal references modified standard for a student who doesn't qualify | Confirm the accommodation/modification distinction; revise to same standard + access accommodation |
| Goal stem copied verbatim with no student-specific adjustment | Adjust the condition (which version, which supports), the criterion (based on baseline data), and the date |

## Part 7: IEP Goal Stems in CbD Products — Build Standards

When writing goal stems for a new CbD product, follow these rules:

1. **One academic goal stem and one AAC goal stem per unit** — minimum. Units with multiple passages may have goal stems for multiple skill types.

2. **Always include the full IDEA structure:** Given [condition] → will [verb + skill] → as measured by [method] → achieving [criterion] → by [date].

3. **Name the version in the condition.** "Given V2 adapted nonfiction text" is more useful to a teacher than "given adapted text."

4. **Name the data tool in the "as measured by" clause.** Always reference a tool that exists in the product (rubric, Session Tracker). Do not reference a tool the teacher would have to create.

5. **Include the generalization criterion for AAC goals.** "Across 2 different partners" — not just "across sessions."

6. **Never use "understand," "know," "learn," or "appreciate."**

7. **Cross-reference the Assessment and Data Framework** for default mastery criteria by skill type before finalizing any performance criterion in a goal stem.
