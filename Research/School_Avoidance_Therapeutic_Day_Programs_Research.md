# School Avoidance & Therapeutic Day Programs
### Research Notes — April 14, 2026
*Personal/professional practice reference. Not a CbD product build — exploratory research for Jill's sub role in a therapeutic day school.*

---

## Context for This Research

Jill is the regular substitute teacher in a therapeutic day school classroom serving MS/HS students with school avoidance, anxiety, ASD, and ADHD who are in out-of-district placements. This is distinct from her Florence position work (complex communication, aggressive behaviors). The populations require fundamentally different approaches.

---

## 1. Language and Framing: Why It Matters

**School avoidance ≠ truancy ≠ refusal**

- **School avoidance** is the preferred current term. Driven by anxiety and emotional distress, not defiance or choice.
- **Truancy** = deliberate choice, often no distress, often without parent knowledge.
- **School refusal** (older clinical term) is still used in research but is being replaced by "avoidance" in practice.

Students in therapeutic day programs for avoidance are in genuine distress. Treating the behavior as defiance — punitive attendance contracts, threats, power struggles — consistently makes outcomes worse. The research on this is 20+ years consistent.

**Physical symptoms of avoidance** (especially at MS/HS age): nausea, headaches, panic attacks, somatic complaints with no medical cause. These are real, not performed.

---

## 2. Who This Population Is

The most common diagnostic picture in therapeutic day programs for this age group:

- **ASD** — anxiety is usually the primary driver of avoidance, not ASD traits per se
- **ADHD + anxiety comorbidity** — combined type shows highest avoidance rates
- **ASD + ADHD combined** — 68% bullying-related school refusal rate (2024 research); social trauma at school is often the specific trigger
- **Anxiety disorders** — generalized, social, panic
- **Depression** — often secondary; the longer the avoidance cycle runs, the worse the depression gets
- **Trauma histories** — may or may not be formally documented

**Key clinical insight:** These students often *want* to learn and *miss* school. What they cannot tolerate is the unpredictability, social threat, and sensory load of a traditional school building. Avoidance is a coping strategy, not a preference.

### PDA Profile (Pathological Demand Avoidance)

PDA is a profile within the ASD spectrum that shows up frequently in this population and is frequently misunderstood.

- Not defiance — it is an extreme anxiety response to perceived loss of control
- Even benign everyday demands (get your book out, time to transition) can trigger full fight-flight-freeze
- Students described across prior placements as "manipulative," "oppositional," or "exhausting" are often PDA profile students who were never correctly understood
- Standard token economies and behavior management approaches make PDA *worse*, not better
- Requires collaborative, low-demand, interest-driven approaches (see Section 5)

## 3. What Therapeutic Day Programs Are and How They Differ

### Structure
- Out-of-district placements on the LRE continuum — most restrictive academic setting
- Determined by IEP team when no less restrictive setting can meet the student's needs
- **Goal is always return to a less restrictive environment** — the transition plan is the point of the placement
- Small ratios: typically 6–8 students to 1 teacher + paraprofessional (sometimes smaller)
- On-site clinical staff (therapist, psychologist, social worker) integrated into the daily program

### Milieu Therapy — The Operating Model

In a therapeutic day school, therapy is not a separate 30-minute session. **The entire day is the therapeutic environment.** Every interaction, transition, lunch, and moment of frustration is a clinical opportunity.

This means:
- How a teacher responds in the hallway matters as much as what happens in class
- Every adult on the team must apply the same framework — consistency across adults is the single most important structural feature
- There is no "off duty" time from the therapeutic model

### How This Differs from Other SPED Settings

| | Typical SPED | Therapeutic Day (School Avoidance) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary vehicle | Academic intervention | Relationship and regulation |
| Behavior approach | Behavior management/compliance | Regulation and co-regulation |
| Academic sequence | Primary focus | Third, after safety and regulation |
| Progress metrics | Academic skill growth | Attendance, regulation, relationship stability |
| Family role | IEP stakeholder | Active treatment partner |
| Transition planning | Annual IEP review | Ongoing, central to placement purpose |

**The instructional sequence that must be followed:**

> **Safety → Regulation → Relationship → Academic Demand**

You cannot shortcut this sequence. A student who is not yet in the Relationship phase will not sustain academic engagement regardless of how well the instruction is designed.

## 4. What Behavior Looks Like in This Population (vs. Florence Position)

| Florence Position | Therapeutic Day (Avoidance) |
|---|---|
| Externalized behavior — aggression, self-injury, property destruction | Internalized responses — silence, flight to nurse, shutdown, crying without visible escalation |
| Behavior often has clear communicative function | Behavior is usually anxiety/avoidance driven |
| AAC system is the primary access tool | Verbal capacity is often intact; the barrier is nervous system state |
| Clear behavioral protocols to follow | Relational judgment calls in the moment |

**What avoidance looks like in the MS/HS therapeutic day room:**
- Student goes completely silent
- Student asks to go to the nurse, the bathroom, or to call home
- Student puts in earbuds and checks out
- Student starts crying without visible build-up
- Student negotiates endlessly about the task without engaging it
- Student who seemed fine suddenly hits a wall

**What to do when you see these signals:**
- Don't press. At all.
- Quiet, low-demand check-in: *"I can see this feels hard right now. I'm going to stay close if you need anything."*
- Let them access the regulation strategy that's in their plan — fidgets, headphones, quiet corner, movement break
- Do not draw peer attention to what's happening
- Document what you saw and when for the returning teacher

## 5. Evidence-Based Instructional Frameworks

### Trauma-Informed Instruction (Baseline — Not an Add-On)

The student's nervous system has been conditioned to perceive school as unsafe. You cannot motivate past this. The brain cannot access learning in a threat state.

**HEARTS program research:** 32% reduction in behavioral incidents year one; 87% reduction over five years. Key mechanisms: predictability, adult co-regulation, sensory awareness, relationship repair after conflict.

**Practical trauma-informed instruction:**
- Predictable daily structure — low-demand entry activities (first 10 minutes are never high-stakes)
- Explicit transition cues — never assumed
- No power struggles in front of peers
- Choices at every possible point — not unlimited, but genuine either/or options that restore agency
- "Refusal" during instruction = regulation signal, not behavior to punish

**Zero-tolerance and punitive approaches are contraindicated** — they confirm the student's belief that the environment is unsafe.

---

### PDA-Informed / Low-Demand Approaches

For PDA-profile students (common in this setting), standard behavior management will backfire. What works:

- **Collaborative Problem Solving (CPS) — Ross Greene's model.** Solve problems collaboratively *in advance*, not in the moment of crisis. Genuinely solicit and incorporate the student's perspective.
- **Demand framing matters:** *"Would you be willing to try the first problem?"* is processed differently than *"Take out your math worksheet."*
- **Interest-driven access:** Anchor academic content to intrinsic interest. A student who refuses any direct writing prompt may write three pages on a special interest.
- **Low-arousal adult affect:** Calm, neutral tone. Reduce sensory load. Avoid escalating language.

---

### DBT STEPS-A (Dialectical Behavior Therapy for Adolescents)

**DBT STEPS-A** is an evidence-based curriculum designed for MS/HS students, deliverable by trained teachers (not just clinicians). It is the primary explicit therapeutic curriculum in many therapeutic day programs.

Four modules:
1. **Mindfulness** — self-awareness, concentration, present-moment grounding
2. **Distress Tolerance** — skills for tolerating crisis states without making things worse *(the entry point for school avoidance students specifically)*
3. **Emotion Regulation** — understanding and reducing the intensity of distressing emotions
4. **Interpersonal Effectiveness** — making requests, saying no, maintaining relationships

**For school avoidance specifically:** Distress Tolerance is the entry point. These students are in chronic anticipatory distress about school. They need concrete tools for tolerating that distress without fleeing before any other skills can be built.

---

### CBT / ERP for School Avoidance

The clinical gold standard for school avoidance treatment. In a therapeutic day setting, this means the teacher and clinician are working from an explicit **exposure hierarchy** — not just managing day-to-day functioning.

- The student's avoidance behaviors are mapped (triggers, responses, relief pattern)
- A graduated exposure plan is built — very small, predictable steps toward the feared situation
- The teacher's role: support the student in *tolerating* distress during exposure steps, not removing the discomfort
- **Important tension:** Trauma-informed ≠ no challenge. It means challenge is calibrated and predictable. The exposure plan is the vehicle for that.

---

### Relationship-Based Learning

The research on this population is unambiguous: **academic progress does not happen without a felt sense of safety with at least one adult in the building.**

- Teachers invest in relationship *before* introducing academic expectations
- Check-in/check-out = daily relationship touchpoints, not just behavioral tracking
- **Repair after conflict is non-negotiable.** If a student had a crisis and the interaction ended hard, you go back and repair. Every time.
- Student voice in designing their own experience — what does engagement look like for *this* student

---

### Executive Function as Academic Intervention

After baseline regulation is established, EF deficits are often the academic barrier. Research supports **HOPS (Homework, Organization, and Planning Skills)** and similar programs:

- Time management and planning
- Materials organization
- Task initiation

For many students in this population, executive function deficits are what allowed avoidance to escalate — mounting academic failure created an overwhelming sense of being behind, and avoidance became the only escape.

## 6. Jill's Role as Regular Sub — Specific Guidance

### What the Regular Sub Relationship Gives You

Being the known, trusted sub means students have already done the unconscious threat assessment and decided you're safe. You have relational credit. But they will also test differently than with a stranger — more negotiating, more low-level avoidance to see if you'll hold the structure. Hold it warmly.

### What Your Job Actually Is on Sub Days

1. **Follow the schedule to the letter.** These students have it memorized and draw comfort from it. Novelty = threat.
2. **Keep affect low and steady.** Enthusiastically positive can spike anxiety — it reads as unpredictable and socially demanding. Low and warm is the register.
3. **Don't push academics.** If a student is working, great. If they put their head down, a quiet *"whenever you're ready"* and back off. Do not create a demand confrontation that the teacher has to repair for two weeks.
4. **Watch for internalized distress signals** — not aggression, but shutdown, flight, freeze. Respond with presence, not pressure.
5. **Use the regulation strategies that are already in place** — don't improvise new ones. Mirror whatever the teacher does.
6. **Side-door everything.** No public praise, no singling out, no drawing peer attention to one student's struggles.
7. **Document what you observe.** Your sub-day observations are genuine clinical data for the returning teacher.

### Social Dynamics Warning

These students often have significant social anxiety layered on everything else. How they are perceived by peers matters enormously. Never single a student out in front of the group — not even positively.

### Testing Behavior With You (vs. Stranger)

With a stranger: they often just shut down.
With you (known sub): they may negotiate, push low-level avoidance, tell you "the teacher always lets us..." 

The right response: *"I hear you, and we're going to stick with what's on the plan today."* Warm and firm. The structure is your co-regulator — holding it is not rigidity, it is safety.

---

## 7. What to Ask the Team (If You Can)

If you have a relationship with the regular teacher, ask for or offer to help build a one-page sub cheat sheet per student:
- What does early dysregulation look like for this student?
- What is the one thing that helps?
- What is the one thing to never do?

This doesn't exist in most IEPs. Your regular presence means you're accumulating this tacit knowledge — it's worth naming to the team and formalizing.

---

## 8. Research Gaps Worth Knowing

- Most evidence is built on anxiety-primary school refusal. Less research on the combined ASD+ADHD+trauma picture most common in actual therapeutic day programs.
- PDA is not yet in DSM-5, creating IEP documentation challenges.
- DBT STEPS-A has strong evidence as universal intervention but less evidence specifically in out-of-district therapeutic settings.
- The transition-back piece (returning to less restrictive settings) is chronically under-researched.
