# AAC, Phonemic Awareness, and Structured Literacy: Research Reference Document

**Title:** Phonemic Awareness, AAC Design, and UFLI Adaptation for Nonspeaking Learners
**Date compiled:** March 20, 2026
**Source conversation:** Multi-session working conversation covering brand audit of Communicate by Design (CbD), development of Substack Article 3 "The Phonemic Keyboard Problem," and instructional design principles for the CbD UFLI-aligned AAC phonics product line. Includes research pulled during article drafting, practitioner landscape review, and product design framework development.

---

## Section 1: Key Findings & Evidence

---

### 1.1 Speech production is not required for phonemic awareness development

**Finding:** Nonspeaking students can develop phoneme-grapheme mapping, phonological awareness, and literacy skills without ever producing a speech sound. Oral production facilitates the process — particularly for complex phoneme manipulation tasks — but is not a prerequisite.

**Sources:**
- Bishop, D.V.M., & Robson, J. (1989). "Accurate non-word spelling despite congenital inability to speak: phoneme-grapheme conversion does not require subvocal articulation." *British Journal of Psychology.*
- Foley, B.E., & Pollatsek, A. (1999). "Phonological processing and reading abilities in adolescents and adults with severe congenital speech impairments." *Augmentative and Alternative Communication, 15(3).*
- Dahlgren Sandberg, A., & Hjelmquist, E. (1996, 1997). Series of studies on preschool children with cerebral palsy (nonspeaking) vs. matched speaking controls. No group differences in phonological awareness found. Publication details: *Journal of Intellectual Disability Research* and related Swedish journals.
- Card, R., & Dodd, B. (2006). "The phonological awareness abilities of children with cerebral palsy who do not speak." *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.*
- Barton-Hulsey, A., Sevcik, R., & Romski, M. (2018). *American Journal of Speech-Language Pathology.* Study of 42 preschoolers with developmental disabilities. Key finding: speech ability did not significantly predict phonological awareness beyond receptive language and print/letter-sound knowledge.
- Peeters, M., Verhoeven, L., de Moor, J., & van Balkom, H. (2009). Longitudinal study of 52 children with cerebral palsy. *Research in Developmental Disabilities.* Finding: speech production was the strongest predictor of early reading success — but facilitates, not enables.

**Why it matters for practice:** Denying phonics instruction to nonspeaking students because "they can't say the sounds" is not supported by the research. The instructional design must change (response mode). The instructional expectation should not (phonemic processing demand stays).

---

### 1.2 The research base for literacy instruction with AAC users is thin but consistent

**Finding:** A 2012 systematic review found only 8 studies, totaling 26 children, that had attempted to teach phonological awareness and word reading to children with severe speech impairments using AAC. Volume is low. Consistency of findings is high — every rigorously conducted study that provided explicit, systematic instruction with adapted response modes showed meaningful gains.

**Source:** Barker, R.M., Saunders, K.J., & Brady, N.C. (2012). "Reading instruction for children who use AAC: Considerations in the pursuit of generalizable results." *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.* PMC3715742.

**Why it matters for practice:** The absence of evidence in this population has been misread as evidence of absence. IEP teams cite "no research" when what exists is "limited research that consistently supports instruction."

---

### 1.3 The 2025 field consensus: AAC users can acquire literacy with adapted instruction

**Finding:** A 2025 major review affirmed the evidence base conclusively.

**Source:** Light, J., McNaughton, D., Holyfield, C., et al. (2025). Review published in *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.* Direct quote: "Research has demonstrated that individuals with developmental disabilities who need or use AAC can successfully acquire a wide range of literacy skills when provided with effective tools and evidence-based, adapted instruction."

**Why it matters for practice:** This is the current authoritative summary of the field. Cite it when IEP teams push back on literacy instruction for nonspeaking students.

### 1.4 AAC device keyboards were designed for communication, not phonics instruction

**Finding:** Most AAC device keyboards vocalize letter *names* (bee, see, dee) rather than letter *sounds* (/b/, /k/, /d/) because their text-to-speech engines were built for literate users composing messages — not students learning the alphabetic code. This creates a direct conflict with structured literacy instruction.

**Source:** Discussed in conversation; also documented by:
- AssistiveWare (Sheldon, E., 2020). "Phonics, spelling, and keyboards for children who cannot speak." AssistiveWare blog. assistiveware.com/blog/teaching-phonics-children-cannot-speak
- Sullivan, A. (2025). "Phonics and AAC." phonicsforpupilswithspecialeducationalneeds.com

**Why it matters for practice:** When a student's device says "bee" instead of "/b/" during phonics instruction, the device is actively teaching incorrect information. Teachers and SLPs need to recognize this as a design limitation, not a user error.

---

### 1.5 The digraph problem: 26 keys cannot hold 44 phonemes

**Finding:** English has approximately 44 phonemes represented by over 280 letter combinations. A standard QWERTY keyboard — even one reprogrammed with letter sounds — cannot represent digraphs (/sh/, /ch/, /th/, /wh/) without dedicated additional keys. When a student spells "shop" on a standard phonics keyboard, the device produces /s/-/h/-/o/-/p/ instead of /sh/-/o/-/p/, fundamentally misrepresenting how the alphabetic code works. Digraphs appear in the most common English words (the, this, that, with, she, which).

**Source:** Discussed in conversation; supported by AssistiveWare (2020) blog post above; partially addressed by PRC-Saltillo Empower 1.17 update (documented at prc-saltillo.com).

**Why it matters for practice:** Any AAC-based phonics keyboard solution is incomplete unless it includes digraph keys with recorded (not TTS) phoneme audio.

### 1.6 AAC company positions on phonics keyboards diverge significantly

**Finding:** The three major AAC manufacturers have taken opposing positions:

- **AssistiveWare (Proloquo2Go):** Explicitly against phonics keyboards. Position: phonics keyboards oversimplify the code, associate one sound per letter, interfere with sight word acquisition, and are only useful during a narrow developmental window. Recommendation: use separate tools for phonics, not the AAC device.
- **PRC-Saltillo:** The only major company to build recorded phoneme buttons into their vocabulary systems (WordPower, Words for Life). Empower 1.17 added five digraph keys (ch, sh, th voiced, th unvoiced, wh) with recorded audio — not TTS. This is the most phonemically precise implementation available.
- **Tobii Dynavox (TD Snap):** Standard keyboards speak letter names. No built-in phonics keyboard. Sells the Accessible Literacy Learning (ALL) app separately ($79.99). Community practitioner Angela of OMazing Kids AAC Consulting created free phonics keyboard page bundles for Motor Plan 66/40/30 pagesets via Pageset Central.

**Sources:** AssistiveWare blog (2020); PRC-Saltillo Empower 1.17 release notes (prentrom.com); OMazing Kids AAC Consulting (omazingkidsllc.com/tag/aac-phonics-keyboards/).

**Why it matters for practice:** SLPs and AT specialists making device recommendations for students in structured literacy programs should know PRC-Saltillo is currently the only manufacturer with built-in, phonemically accurate phonics support. This is a legitimate differentiator for device trials.

### 1.7 The AAC device should not be the phonics instruction tool

**Finding:** Consensus across researchers and AAC companies: a student's primary AAC system (their voice) should remain separate from phonics instruction tools. Reconfiguring or using the AAC device for drills risks disrupting the student's communication access and conflates two distinct functions.

**Sources:**
- AssistiveWare (2020): "Be cautious using a student's AAC as the tool to teach discrete skills like phonics. Instead, use other tools to teach phonics."
- Erickson, K., & Koppenhaver, D. (2020). *Comprehensive Literacy for All.* Brookes Publishing. (Framework recommends separate word study tools.)
- Discussed and developed further in conversation as CbD design principle.

**Why it matters for practice:** This principle has direct IEP implications. Communication access and literacy instruction are separate service areas requiring separate materials and separate goals.

---

### 1.8 The Accessible Literacy Learning (ALL) program is the most rigorously validated intervention

**Finding:** ALL, developed by Janice Light and David McNaughton at Penn State, teaches six skills sequentially (sound blending, phoneme segmentation, letter-sound correspondence, single-word decoding, sight word recognition, shared reading comprehension) with all responses via touch, eye gaze, or scanning — no oral production required. Uses continuous blending. Validated with children ages 3–16 across autism, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, developmental apraxia, and multiple disabilities.

**Key study:** Fallon, K.A., Light, J., McNaughton, D., Drager, K., & Hammer, C. (2004). *Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research.* Five participants with CCN. Three of five decoded untaught novel words — demonstrating generalization of the phonological system, not rote memorization.

**Outcome data:** Caron, Light, & McNaughton (2020). *Research and Practice for Persons with Severe Disabilities.* Four of five participants increased accuracy reading targeted single words; four of five generalized to text-only displays.

**Why it matters for practice:** ALL is the benchmark program for this population. Any UFLI adaptation for AAC users should be evaluated against its design principles. Tobii Dynavox sells it; Penn State's AAC Literacy site (aacliteracy.psu.edu) provides free implementation sequences.

### 1.9 Explicit teaching of inner voice is required for AAC users

**Finding:** Typically developing readers externalize reading before internalizing it. AAC users must be explicitly taught inner voice from the start of literacy instruction because they cannot externalize to oral reading. Inner voice is required to hold words in working memory at the sentence level.

**Sources:**
- Erickson, K., Koppenhaver, D., & Cunningham, J. (2017). Word identification "does not require speech and is achieved subvocally or even neurologically during silent reading." (Citation in Comprehensive Literacy for All framework.)
- Farrall, J. (janefarrall.com). Citing Erickson (2003): "Inner voice is required to hold words in working memory long enough to process text at the sentence level and beyond."
- PrAACtical AAC. "5 Great Ways to Help People with AAC Needs Develop Inner Speech." praacticalaac.org

**Why it matters for practice:** Instruction that only provides a selection array without explicitly teaching the student to manipulate sounds internally may not be teaching phonemic awareness at all — it may be teaching guess-checking. Inner voice scaffolding is the mechanism that makes selection-based tasks genuine phonemic tasks.

### 1.10 Non-speech response formats validated in the research

**Finding:** Multiple response formats have been tested and validated for measuring phonemic awareness without oral output:
- Picture array selection (select the word matching blended phonemes) — Fallon et al. (2004); Coleman-Martin et al. (2005)
- Letter card selection (point/gaze to grapheme for target sound) — Vandervelden & Siegel (1999, 2001)
- Adapted Elkonin boxes (push chips, move manipulatives, drag digital tokens) — Blischak et al. (2004)
- Yes/no verification (confirm whether printed word matches spoken target)
- Sorting tasks (categorize by phonemic feature via point/gaze/scan)

**Why it matters for practice:** These formats are not accommodations in the accommodating-down sense. They measure the same phonemic skill using an alternative output channel. IEP goals can be written at full grade-level rigor using these response formats.

---

### 1.11 Two validated non-speech phonemic awareness assessments exist

**Finding:**
- **DAPA-AP** (Dynamic Assessment of Phonemic Awareness via the Alphabetic Principle): Barker, Bridges, & Saunders (2014). *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.* Validated with 17 adults with intellectual disabilities. No spoken response required. PMC4164607.
- **APAR** (Assessment of Phonological Awareness and Reading): Iacono, J., & Cupples, L. (2004). *Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research.* Administered via stimuli on clear Perspex sheet with eye-gaze indication.

**Why it matters for practice:** When an IEP team says "we can't assess her phonemic awareness because she can't talk," the response is: here are two validated tools designed specifically for this purpose.

---

### 1.12 Project Core implementation outcomes

**Finding:** Before Project Core implementation at one participating school, 93% of students scored at the lowest possible assessment level; 0% achieved mastery. After one year: lowest-level scores dropped to 58%; 13% achieved proficiency.

**Source:** ATIA (2021). Initial Evaluation of the Project Core Implementation Model. Geist et al. atia.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/V15_Geist_etal.pdf

**Why it matters for practice:** This is a dramatic shift in one year with a free, freely available framework. It is the most compelling single-school outcome data point in this literature for justifying implementation to administrators.

### 1.13 UFLI Foundations and AAC: the practitioner landscape

**Finding:** Kate Ahern (Ahern Academy) is currently the most prominent practitioner adapting UFLI for AAC users. She presented "Literacy and Learners with CCN Using UFLI" at AAC in the Cloud 2023 (YouTube, linked from aacvoices.org). Her specific adapted materials are behind the Ahern Academy membership paywall. Her publicly documented adaptations: slower pacing, visual schedule for lesson routine, choice arrays replacing verbal responses, AAC modeling during instruction, minimum 2 hours daily literacy embedded across the day. Hard rejection of Edmark/PCI sight-word-only programs.

**POPARD** (BC Provincial Outreach Program for ASD, autismoutreach.ca) has a Google Drive folder of adapted UFLI lesson plans available after free registration. The specific content of those plans is not publicly visible.

**Why it matters for practice (and product design):** No publicly available product systematically adapts all 8 UFLI steps with specific materials for every major AAC access method. Ahern's materials are paywalled. POPARD's are gated and BC-specific. The market gap is confirmed.

### 1.14 The Auditory Confirmation Loop — CbD design principle (developed in conversation)

**Finding/Principle:** This principle was developed and named during the conversation. It is not from a single published source but synthesizes the research above with Jill McCardel's classroom practice insight.

**Principle:** When a student responds via any access method (gaze, switch, PECS exchange, SGD, point), they must hear the phoneme confirmed back. Either:
(a) A trained communication partner produces the actual phoneme (not the letter name, not "correct") upon the student's selection, or
(b) The material is designed so that the student's selection triggers automated recorded audio of the phoneme.

This principle applies across all 8 UFLI lesson steps. The selection is incomplete without the sound firing back.

**The access method changes. The phonemic processing demand does not.**

**Why it matters for practice:** This distinguishes CbD's product philosophy from generic AAC accommodation worksheets, which provide selection arrays without specifying the confirmation mechanism. A student who guesses from a picture array has not demonstrated phonemic awareness. A student who hears a distinct phoneme string, processes it internally, selects the matching picture, and hears the confirmed word — has.

---

### 1.15 Partner phoneme delivery quality is more critical for AAC users than speaking students

**Finding:** Developed from conversation synthesis. A speaking student who mishears the teacher can self-correct by attempting the word. A nonspeaking student has no repair loop — if the teacher's phoneme delivery is rushed or unclear, the student has nothing to work with. The selection array becomes a guess.

**Practical implications:**
- Distinct phoneme production with clear boundaries — not connected speech
- Consistent wait time matching the student's AAC response rate
- Knowing when to re-present the phoneme string vs. prompt differently
- Not treating slow responses as wrong responses

**Source:** Discussed in conversation. Supported by general principles from partner-mediated AAC instruction literature (Kent-Walsh & McNaughton, 2005; referenced in ASHA Practice Portal on AAC).

**Why it matters for practice:** Partner training is instruction. The para who rushes through phoneme strings during the blending drill is actively undermining phonemic awareness development. Teacher support materials must address this explicitly.

## Section 3: Practical Implications

### AT/AAC service delivery
- Device selection for students in structured literacy programs should factor in whether the device has recorded phoneme buttons, not just letter-name TTS. PRC-Saltillo is currently the only major manufacturer with built-in digraph phoneme support.
- The AAC device and the phonics instruction tool should be distinct. Never reconfigure a student's primary communication system to serve as a drill tool.
- Every E-tran, response board, and selection array used in phonics instruction needs a defined confirmation mechanism — either partner-produced phoneme or automated recorded audio.

### IEP team capacity-building
- The DAPA-AP and APAR are the response to "we can't assess phonemic awareness without verbal output."
- IEP goals for phonemic awareness should specify: the phonemic skill, the array size, the access method, and the accuracy criterion. Example: "When presented with a spoken CVC word and a field of three letter cards, [Student] will identify the initial sound by selecting the correct letter card using eye gaze with 80% accuracy across 4 of 5 sessions."
- SLP role: leads AAC evaluation, phonological awareness assessment, staff training on device use, aided language stimulation during literacy. Teacher role: leads daily literacy instruction, collects progress data, embeds phonemic awareness across the day. These roles do not overlap — they interlock.
- Partner training is not optional. The quality of phoneme delivery during instruction directly determines whether a nonspeaking student can complete the phonemic task.

### Instructional design (CbD UFLI product line)
- Every adapted UFLI activity must specify: (1) how the student responds, and (2) what sound is produced in the room upon that response — by whom or by what.
- No selection is complete without a sound. That is the non-negotiable.
- The 8 UFLI steps each require a distinct adaptation mapping. Steps 1 (phonemic awareness), 2 (visual drill), 3 (auditory drill), and 4 (blending drill) require the most significant redesign because they assume oral output. Steps 5 (new concept) and 7 (irregular words) are most accessible as-is.
- Word Work (Step 6) is best adapted using physical or digital letter tiles where each tile is a single phoneme unit — meaning "sh" is one tile, not two.
- Sight-word-only programs (Edmark, PCI) are insufficient as literacy programs for this population. Kate Ahern (2023) and Erickson & Koppenhaver (2020) are both explicit on this.
- The competitive gap confirmed: no publicly available product systematically adapts all 8 UFLI steps with materials for all major AAC access methods. CbD's planned product line fills this gap.

### Family support
- The research consistently shows that exposure to print, letters, and sounds — regardless of speech ability — is what predicts phonological awareness development. Families can support this at home without AAC devices or specialist equipment.
- The Literacy Bill of Rights (Yoder, Erickson, & Koppenhaver, 1997) is a tool families can reference in IEP meetings when literacy instruction is withheld or deprioritized.

## Section 4: Terms & Definitions

**Auditory Confirmation Loop** — CbD design principle (developed in this conversation). The requirement that when a nonspeaking student selects a response via any access method, they hear the phoneme or word confirmed back — either by a trained partner producing the phoneme aloud, or by the material triggering recorded audio. The loop is not complete until the sound is heard.

**Partner-mediated phoneme production** — The partner's role in completing the auditory confirmation loop. Functionally equivalent to aided language stimulation but applied to phoneme-level instruction rather than vocabulary modeling.

**Phonemic awareness** — The ability to notice and manipulate individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words. Distinct from phonics (which involves print). Can be assessed and developed without oral speech production.

**Phonological awareness** — Broader term encompassing awareness of sounds at syllable, onset-rime, and phoneme levels. Phonemic awareness is the deepest level of phonological awareness.

**Complex communication needs (CCN)** — Umbrella term for individuals whose speech is insufficient to meet all communication needs. Includes nonspeaking individuals, those with limited functional speech, and those using AAC.

**Anarthric** — Having no functional speech output due to motor impairment, while retaining language comprehension and processing. Distinct from aphasia (language impairment) and intellectual disability (cognitive impairment).

**Continuous blending** — A phoneme-blending technique where sounds flow without pauses between them (/mmmaaat/ not /m/…/a/…/t/). Used in the ALL program. More effective than segmented blending for decoding acquisition.

**Inner voice** — The internal phonological loop used in reading. For AAC users, must be explicitly taught from the beginning of literacy instruction because they cannot externalize it through oral reading.

**Digraph** — A two-letter combination representing a single phoneme (sh, ch, th, wh, ph). The most common English words contain digraphs. A 26-key keyboard cannot represent digraph phonemes as single units without dedicated additional keys.

**Alternative pencil** — Any tool that provides writing access for a student who cannot hold or control a standard pencil. Includes adapted keyboards, eye-gaze keyboards, scanning alphabet frames, PECS-based letter selection, or partner-assisted alphabet boards.

**E-tran** — Eye Transfer board. A transparent response board that allows a student to indicate a selection using eye gaze, with the communication partner observing from the other side.

**DAPA-AP** — Dynamic Assessment of Phonemic Awareness via the Alphabetic Principle. Validated non-speech phonemic awareness assessment. Barker, Bridges, & Saunders (2014). *Augmentative and Alternative Communication.*

**APAR** — Assessment of Phonological Awareness and Reading. Validated non-speech phonemic awareness assessment. Iacono & Cupples (2004). *Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research.*

**ALL** — Accessible Literacy Learning. Evidence-based reading program for students with CCN. Developed by Light & McNaughton (Penn State). Sold by Tobii Dynavox ($79.99 app). No oral response required at any stage.

**UFLI Foundations** — University of Florida Literacy Institute Foundations. Free, evidence-based structured literacy program. Eight-step lesson routine. Designed for Tier 1 core instruction in primary grades or intervention in any grade. All lesson materials free at ufli.education.ufl.edu.

**Literacy Bill of Rights** — Yoder, Erickson, & Koppenhaver (1997). Statement of principle: "All persons, regardless of the extent or severity of their disabilities, have a basic right to use print." Frequently cited in AAC and literacy advocacy.

**3. The auditory confirmation loop lacks a formal citation**
The principle as articulated in this document was developed from conversation synthesis. It draws on partner-mediated AAC instruction literature (Kent-Walsh & McNaughton, 2005) and aided language stimulation research, but has not been formally named or studied as described here. This is CbD's original framing. Should be documented as such in product materials and not attributed to a published source without verification.

**4. Dahlgren Sandberg & Hjelmquist (1996, 1997) — publication details incomplete**
These are cited in the phonemic awareness literature as foundational Swedish studies. Full journal citations were not confirmed in this conversation. Search: *Journal of Intellectual Disability Research* and *Augmentative and Alternative Communication* for the relevant years.

**5. Coleman-Martin et al. (2005) — citation incomplete**
Referenced in validated non-speech response approaches. Full citation not confirmed. Likely *AAC* journal. Needs verification before use in published materials.

**6. Vandervelden & Siegel (1999, 2001) — citation incomplete**
Referenced in letter-card selection task validation. Full citations not confirmed. Needs verification.

**7. Blischak et al. (2004) — citation incomplete**
Referenced for adapted Elkonin box validation. Full citation not confirmed. Needs verification.

**8. The 2023 Norwegian trial**
Referenced as: "A 2023 Norwegian trial published in *Frontiers in Education* used ALL-based instruction with 40 nonverbal or minimally verbal students ages 6-14." Full author list and article title not confirmed. Needs DOI or full citation for use in published materials.

**9. Erickson, Koppenhaver, & Cunningham (2017) — citation incomplete**
Cited for inner voice and word identification without speech. The year 2017 was stated but not verified against the Comprehensive Literacy for All publication timeline (2020). May be a chapter or earlier publication. Needs confirmation.

**10. UFLI adaptation for upper elementary / secondary — underexplored**
Most existing UFLI + AAC practitioner work (Ahern, POPARD) addresses primary-age students. CbD's target population is grades 6–10. No published or publicly available adapted UFLI materials for this grade range with AAC support have been identified. This is both the most significant gap in the literature and the clearest product opportunity.

**11. The role of the paraeducator in the auditory confirmation loop — not yet addressed**
In most inclusive and resource settings, the communication partner during phonics instruction is often a paraeducator, not the teacher or SLP. Partner training requirements for accurate phoneme production have been noted but not yet developed into specific training content for CbD's teacher support documentation.
