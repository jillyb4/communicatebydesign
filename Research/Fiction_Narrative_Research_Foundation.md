# Fiction & Narrative Literacy — Research Foundation
*CbD Fiction Anchor Texts Product Line | Compiled: 2026-03-27*

---

## Purpose

This document is the evidence base for the CbD Fiction Anchor Texts product line. It supports the design of SDI companion units for traditional K–12 novels, grounded in professional society standards (CEC, ASHA, IRIS) and the peer-reviewed literature.

**Core principle:** Core academic standards (RL) are always the goal. SDI adapts methodology and delivery — not the standard.

### CEC High-Leverage Practices (highleveragepractices.org)
- **HLP 12 — Descriptive Teaching Model:** Core vocabulary used to describe new concepts rather than label them. Enables generative language, not recall. Directly applicable to character and theme instruction.
- **HLP 13 — Adapt Curriculum Tasks and Materials:** Story maps, graphic organizers, response boards, VSDs. SDI through material adaptation.
- **HLP 14 — Predictable Chart Writing / Write-Ables:** Group narrative construction. Students with AAC contribute words/phrases. Applicable to key fiction scenes.
- **HLP 16 — Use Explicit Instruction / Shared Reading:** Foundational delivery method for fiction. AAC access during shared reading is documented to improve listening comprehension.

### CAST — UDL Guidelines (udlguidelines.cast.org)
- Barriers to learning are in the design of instruction, not in individual learners.
- Multiple means of representation for fiction must include audiobook/read-aloud, visual scene supports, and discussion structures that externalize internal character reasoning.
- Pre-teaching vocabulary and figurative language before reading is a UDL representation strategy, not an accommodation.

## Key Research Findings

### 1. Story Grammar Instruction
**ERIC EJ871908** — Story grammar instruction shows statistically significant improvements in comprehension strategy use, story-structure knowledge, and reading comprehension. Gains are equivalent for students with and without disabilities. Story grammar is evidence-based SDI.

**Story grammar elements:** character, setting, problem/conflict, events/plot, resolution, theme.

These are the organizational schema students learn to use across any narrative — not just the specific book.

### 2. Narrative Language Predicts Reading Comprehension
**PMC7043286 (2020)** — Narrative macrostructure accounts for 8.2% more variance in reading comprehension than decoding and basic language microstructure alone, in students with mild intellectual disabilities.

Where students with disabilities struggle most:
- **Mental state language** — emotions, motivations, beliefs, thoughts of characters
- Consistent pronoun use across a narrative
- Cohesive transitions between narrative events

Where they show strength:
- Introducing story elements
- Basic character development
- Explaining conflicts and resolutions

**Implication:** Emotional and mental state vocabulary is not supplemental — it is the primary access gap for fiction comprehension.

### 3. Visual Scene Displays in Shared Storybook Reading
**PMC8375490** — VSDs in shared reading:
- Increase communication and participation of children with complex communication needs
- Enable rapid vocabulary acquisition
- Reduce cognitive load by embedding vocabulary within the storybook scene
- Improve interactions between children with complex needs and peers
- Teachers can create VSDs in under one minute per page

For fiction, VSDs are more effective than decontextualized symbol cards for key scenes because they situate vocabulary in narrative context.

### 4. Shared Reading with AAC Access
**Tandfonline (2025)** — Shared reading intervention with text simplification and communication boards:
- All participants demonstrated improved comprehension when provided appropriate AAC access
- Wh-word concept instruction (who, what, where, when, why, how) is an effective SDI approach for literal and inferential comprehension
- Least-to-most intrusive prompts improve comprehension response accuracy

### 5. Figurative Language and Theme Are Teachable
**PMC12649625 (scoping review, autism)** — Figurative language (metaphor, idiom, sarcasm/irony) IS teachable for students previously thought to be "stuck at literal." Effective programs use:
- Explicit, structured instruction with step-by-step modeling
- Visual supports (feature maps, semantic diagrams, concept maps)
- Multiple exemplars across diverse contexts
- Graduated prompting that fades as competence increases
- Immediate feedback

**Key finding:** The teaching strategy matters more than the communication mode. Generalization to untrained expressions occurs when instruction emphasizes underlying semantic relationships.

### 6. Inferencing with AAC
**TIES Institute** — Picture-based instruction removes listening/decoding barriers while teaching inference. Visual graphic organizers (flowcharts, concept maps) and scaffolded questioning (literal → inferential progression) are effective. Consistency between SLP, teacher, and AAC partners is critical.

### 7. Core and Fringe Vocabulary in Narrative
Core vocabulary (200–400 high-frequency words) enables flexible story construction. Fringe vocabulary in fiction is **descriptive**, not domain-specific — it is the vocabulary of appearance, action, emotion, and setting. This is different from nonfiction fringe vocabulary, which is content/topic specific.

## What This Means for Product Design

1. Story grammar instruction is the SDI framework — it is not a simplified alternative to literary analysis. It is the structure through which literary analysis becomes accessible.

2. Mental state and emotional vocabulary must be pre-taught before every unit. It cannot be assumed.

3. VSDs for key scenes are more effective than standalone symbol cards for fiction. Build them into every unit.

4. "Describe to Draw" as an output mode is research-consistent: it requires generative language production (higher-order than selection), demonstrates comprehension without name-recall barriers, and is accessible to any AAC system.

5. Questions should be designed around what students can access — description, action, attribute — not around what they may lack (specific proper nouns as fringe words).

6. Mode 2 (Partnership) during reading is not optional. It is the evidence-based delivery condition for shared reading with complex communicators.

7. The same RL standard is the target. Always.
