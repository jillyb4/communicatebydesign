## Key Findings & Evidence

### 1. Internal Reading Voice Develops Through Hearing, Not Speaking

**Finding:** Nonspeaking individuals develop an internal phonological loop — the "voice in your head" when you read — through auditory input, not speech production. A student does not need to speak a phoneme to learn it. They need to hear it reliably every time they select a letter.

**Sources:**
- Foley, B. E., & Pollatsek, A. (1999). Phonological processing and reading abilities in adolescents and adults with severe congenital speech impairments. *Augmentative and Alternative Communication, 15*(3), 156–173.
- Bishop, D. V. M. (1985). Spelling ability in congenital dysarthria: Evidence against articulatory coding in translating between graphemes and phonemes. *Cognitive Neuropsychology, 2*(3), 229–251.
- Erickson, K. A., & Koppenhaver, D. A. (2020). *Comprehensive Literacy for All: Teaching Students with Significant Disabilities to Read and Write.* Paul H. Brookes Publishing.
- Vandervelden, M. C., & Siegel, L. S. (1999). Phonological processing and literacy in AAC users and students with motor speech impairments. *Augmentative and Alternative Communication, 15*(3), 191–211.

**Why it matters:** This is the foundational research justifying the entire UFLI AAC adaptation. It means we can teach phonics to complex communicators — the student doesn't need to say the sounds, they need to hear them. Every UFLI step must preserve the "auditory confirmation loop": student selects a letter → partner (or device) says the sound → student hears it → internal phonological representation builds.

### 2. Predictive Text Interferes with Phonemic Processing

**Finding:** Word prediction has no lasting impact on independent spelling skills. Prediction short-circuits phonemic processing — the student learns "type H and HAT appears" instead of blending /h/ /æ/ /t/. Predictive text should be turned OFF during all phonics instruction. It can be introduced later as a writing efficiency tool once the student demonstrates independent decoding/encoding.

**Sources:**
- ASHA Leader (2025). [Article on word prediction and spelling development — specific title/author needed]
- EDC/NCIP (Education Development Center / National Center to Improve Practice). [Research on word prediction and literacy — specific citation needed]

**Why it matters:** This directly contradicts common classroom practice where teams leave prediction on because "it helps them write faster." Prediction is for writing fluency, not reading instruction. CbD products must include explicit guidance: turn prediction off during phonics, turn it back on for writing tasks. The exception: after a student demonstrates independent success with a word, prediction can serve as confirmation (not shortcut).

### 3. Prompting Framework: System of Least Prompts + Constant Time Delay

**Finding:** The evidence-based instructional spine for teaching complex communicators moves from simultaneous prompting (Pre-Alphabet) through constant time delay (early UFLI) to system of least prompts (mid-to-late UFLI). Prompt dependence is the #1 barrier to independent learning — paras who fill silence during wait time are stealing learning opportunities.

**Sources:**
- Hudler, H., Hurlburt, J., & Brock, M. (2025). [Research on prompting procedures for students with complex communication needs — specific title needed]
- Ahlgrim-Delzell, L., et al. (2016). [Research on systematic instruction and literacy for students with significant disabilities — specific title needed]
- Benson-Goldberg, S., & Erickson, K. A. (2024). [Research on instructional procedures for AAC users — specific title needed]

**Why it matters:** Every CbD communication partner script must include specific wait time instructions. The progression:

| Phase | Prompting Strategy | Wait Time | Field Size |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|------------|
| Pre-Alphabet | Simultaneous prompting (0-sec delay) | Heavy modeling | N/A |
| Early UFLI (1–10) | 0→3-sec constant time delay | 3 seconds | 2-choice → 4-quadrant |
| Mid UFLI (11–30) | 3→5→7-sec CTD; system of least prompts for review | 5–7 seconds | Expanding |
| Later UFLI (30+) | Independent for review; CTD for new patterns | 5–10 seconds | Full field |

### 4. Fitzgerald Key — Standard AAC Color-Coding System

**Finding:** The Fitzgerald Key is the standard color-coding system used across major AAC platforms (TD Snap, LAMP/Unity, Proloquo2Go, TouchChat) to categorize words by grammatical function. Using the same color system in low-tech materials (symbol cards, binder tabs, communication boards) creates consistency between a student's device and their print materials.

**Sources:**
- Fitzgerald, E. (1949). *Straight Language for the Deaf.* Volta Bureau. [Original system]
- Goossens', C., Crain, S., & Elder, P. (1992). *Engineering the Preschool Environment for Interactive, Symbolic Communication.* Southeast Augmentative Communication Conference Publications. [Adapted the system for AAC]

**Why it matters:** CbD's symbol card binder system uses Fitzgerald Key colors as the organizing structure. When a student's binder tabs match their device's color-coding, the motor plan for "where do verbs live" is the same whether they're filing a card or navigating a pageset. This is not decorative — it's functional literacy infrastructure.

**Categories used in CbD products:**

| Category | Color | Hex | Examples |
|----------|-------|-----|----------|
| People / Pronouns | Yellow | #D4A800 | I, you, he, she, we |
| Verbs / Actions | Green | #00A86B | run, read, sit, help |
| Descriptions | Orange | #FF8C00 | big, hot, sad, red |
| Nouns | Brown/Gold | #8B6914 | dog, cup, bed, hat |
| Little Words (Prepositions) | Blue | #4A90D9 | in, on, at, with |
| Social / Feelings | Pink | #E88CA5 | yes, no, hi, want, like |

### 5. Communication Partner Terminology

**Finding:** "Communication partner" is the professionally accepted term for anyone who supports a person who uses AAC. It replaces role-specific terms like "para" that narrow the scope of who can deliver instruction.

**Sources:**
- Light, J. (1988). Interaction involving individuals using augmentative and alternative communication systems: State of the art and future directions. *Augmentative and Alternative Communication, 4*(2), 66–82.
- Beukelman, D. R., & Mirenda, P. (various editions). *Augmentative and Alternative Communication: Supporting Children and Adults with Complex Communication Needs.* Paul H. Brookes Publishing.

**Why it matters:** Using "communication partner" instead of "para" means the product works for anyone delivering instruction — teachers, parents, RBTs, tutors, siblings, SLPs. It also signals to buyers that CbD understands AAC practice at a professional level. All CbD UFLI products use this term exclusively.

### 6. Capacity-Building vs. Expert Model

**Finding:** The capacity-building approach focuses on increasing the knowledge and skills of ALL education team members through coaching, resources, and on-demand training — rather than concentrating AT expertise in a single specialist. Every team member takes responsibility for AT services within their scope.

**Source:**
- RESNA Position Paper on AT Specialists in PreK-12 Educational Settings. [Purchased March 6, 2026]

**Why it matters:** This is CbD's brand philosophy. Every product is designed to build capacity — not create dependence on an expert. The Teacher Guide teaches communication partners HOW to deliver UFLI instruction to complex communicators. The symbol cards are print-and-cut, not "ask the SLP to program." The binder system is managed by whoever is with the student.

### 7. AT Device Definition — Legal Standard

**Finding:** Assistive technology is defined broadly: "Any item, piece of equipment, or product system, whether acquired commercially off the shelf, modified, or customized, that is used to increase, maintain, or improve functional capabilities of individuals with disabilities."

**Sources:**
- Tech Act of 1988 / Assistive Technology Device definition, reauthorized 2004
- IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act) — incorporates Tech Act definition, excludes surgically implanted devices
- WHO (2018). "Products that maintain or improve an individual's functioning and independence, thereby promoting their well-being."
- Scherer, M. J. (2002). *Assistive Technology: Matching Device and Consumer for Successful Rehabilitation.* APA.
- Peckham, A., et al. (2018). [Adopted by North American Observatory on Health Systems and Policies — specific title needed]

**Why it matters:** AT is not just devices. A laminated symbol card is AT. An alternative pencil is AT. An e-trans board is AT. CbD's products ARE assistive technology by this definition — they're items used to improve functional capabilities. This positions CbD's products as AT tools, not just "worksheets."

### 8. ARASAAC — Open-Source AAC Symbol System

**Finding:** ARASAAC (Aragonese Portal of AAC) provides 13,000+ pictographic symbols under a CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 license. Free API access, no authentication required. Symbols are available in multiple languages and are widely used in international AAC practice.

**Source:**
- ARASAAC (arasaac.org) — Government of Aragón, Spain. Licensed under Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 4.0 International.

**Why it matters:** ARASAAC gives CbD a legal, free, high-quality symbol set for every product. The CC BY-NC-SA license means CbD can use symbols in educational products sold on TPT as long as attribution is included and they're not sublicensed. Every CbD product with symbols includes: "Pictographic symbols (c) Government of Aragon. ARASAAC. Licensed under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0." Plus a teacher note: "Use symbols from your student's own AAC system first."

**Fallback sources:**
- Mulberry Symbols (mulberrysymbols.org) — 3,000+ SVGs, CC BY-SA 4.0
- OpenSymbols (opensymbols.org) — aggregator across ARASAAC + Mulberry + Sclera + Tawasol, requires API token

### 9. Symbol Card Sizing — Evidence Base

**Finding:** There is no single research paper prescribing an exact minimum size for AAC symbol cards. The field standard is approximately 2" x 2" for individual symbol cards. CbD uses 2.5" x 3.5" (trading card size), which exceeds the field standard and aligns with commercially available storage (9-pocket trading card binder pages).

**Sources:**
- WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 — Target Size (Minimum): interactive targets should be at least 24x24 CSS pixels. Applied to print: symbol targets should be large enough for the student's access method.
- TD Snap grid layouts — commercial AAC device grid sizes inform low-tech card sizing
- Low-tech AAC board standards — field practice, not a single published standard

**Why it matters:** CbD's 3-tier access sizing system (Level 1: 1.75" for beginning eye gaze; Level 2: 1.25" for eye gaze/direct selection; Level 3: 1.0" for independent reference) is based on clinical practice standards, not a single citation. This is an area where CbD is synthesizing field knowledge into a documented standard.

**Gap:** No single peer-reviewed paper prescribes AAC symbol card dimensions. CbD's sizing rationale is evidence-informed but practice-derived.

### 10. WCAG 2.2 AA — Document Accessibility Standard

**Finding:** All CbD documents must meet WCAG 2.2 Level AA conformance. The ADA Title II deadline for schools with 50,000+ population is April 24, 2026. Smaller districts have until April 26, 2027. Section 508 incorporated WCAG 2.2 in 2024. Washington State requires WCAG 2.2 AA by July 1, 2026.

**Sources:**
- W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.2
- ADA Title II Final Rule (April 2024) — first time ADA explicitly named a technical standard for digital accessibility
- Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act (refreshed 2017, WCAG 2.2 incorporated 2024)
- WaTech Policy USER-01 (Washington State Digital Accessibility Policy)
- IDEA — Accessible Educational Materials (AEM) requirements

**Why it matters:** CbD's audience is SPED teams — the people who enforce accessibility in schools. Delivering a disability rights curriculum in an inaccessible format is incoherent. Accessible documents are also a competitive differentiator: no TPT seller in the SPED space is marketing WCAG-conformant products. ### 11. AAC Core and Fringe Vocabulary

**Finding:** Core vocabulary consists of high-frequency words (approximately 200–400 words that make up ~80% of daily communication). These are typically already on a student's AAC device. Fringe vocabulary consists of topic-specific, lower-frequency words that need to be pre-programmed before a lesson. CbD labels every word in every product as core or fringe so the communication partner knows what to prep.

**Sources:**
- Discussed in conversation based on established AAC practice. Core/fringe distinction is standard in AAC clinical literature (Banajee, DiCarlo, & Stricklin, 2003; Yorkston et al., 1988).

**Why it matters:** The core/fringe label tells the communication partner: "This word is probably already on the device (core)" vs. "You need to find or add this word before the lesson (fringe)." Without this distinction, teachers don't know what to prep. Every CbD product includes a core/fringe word table in the Differentiating section and on symbol cards (marked with ★ for core).

### 12. UFLI Foundations — The Source Curriculum

**Finding:** UFLI Foundations is a structured literacy program designed for K-2 students, following the science of reading. It has 8 instructional steps per lesson. CbD's product provides an AAC access layer for all 8 steps — alternative response pathways so a nonspeaking student can participate in every step without oral speech.

**Source:**
- University of Florida Literacy Institute (UFLI). *UFLI Foundations.* [Published curriculum]

**Why it matters:** CbD is NOT creating a new phonics curriculum. CbD is creating the access layer that makes UFLI usable for complex communicators. The 8 UFLI steps don't change. The words don't change. What changes is HOW the student responds. CbD is the only product on TPT that provides this complete access layer.

### 13. Science of Reading for AAC Users

**Finding:** Complex communicators can learn to read and write through systematic phonics instruction when provided appropriate access methods. The research base supports structured literacy for students with significant disabilities, including those who are nonspeaking.

**Sources:**
- Erickson, K. A., & Koppenhaver, D. A. (2020). *Comprehensive Literacy for All.* Paul H. Brookes Publishing.
- The Literacy Bill of Rights (referenced in NWACS March 2026 newsletter)

**Why it matters:** This counters the persistent assumption that nonspeaking students "aren't ready" for phonics. Every student can be taught reading — the question is whether we provide the right access. CbD's entire UFLI line exists because of this research.

## Practical Implications

### AT/AAC Service Delivery
- Sound access is non-negotiable in phonics instruction — partner voice, Talking Mirror, or SGD
- The auditory confirmation loop (select → hear → internalize) replaces the oral response loop
- SGD is part of robust AAC but is NOT required for phonics instruction — low-tech tools work
- Do not default to "work with the SLP" — that's the expert model. Any team member can manage vocabulary within their scope

### IEP Team Capacity-Building
- Every CbD product builds team capacity, not specialist dependence
- Communication partner scripts tell the non-specialist exactly what to do and say
- Wait time instructions prevent prompt dependence (the #1 barrier)
- Prompting framework gives measurable, trackable data for IEP progress monitoring
- Families can request 1:1 SDI for structured literacy in the IEP (~2 hours/day of literacy instruction)

### Instructional Design / Product Development
- Assume nothing: student is a complex communicator with a willing teacher. CbD provides everything else
- Four tools per lesson: alternative pencil, e-trans board, symbol cards, phoneme sounds
- Pacing flexes, scope doesn't — complex communicators may spend 2–3 days on one lesson
- Teacher decides field size (2-choice, 4-quadrant, field of 6-8) — no fixed "levels"
- Fitzgerald Key color-coding creates consistency between low-tech cards and AAC devices
- Cumulative binder system means every product adds to the student's vocabulary library

### Family Support
- "Communication partner" terminology includes parents, siblings, tutors — not just school staff
- Parent letter template explains what school is working on and what families can do at home
- Family sub-line products priced for family budgets ($3–5), not school budgets
- Teach It Tuesdays goes beyond modeling: noticing, responding, environment engineering, expectant delay, aided language input

## Terms & Definitions

|------|-----------|--------|
| AAC | Augmentative and Alternative Communication — any method that supplements or replaces speech | Standard clinical term |
| Alternative pencil | Any tool that allows a student to select letters for writing without a traditional pencil — eye gaze board, adapted keyboard, flip chart, partner-assisted scanning | Erickson & Koppenhaver (2020) |
| Auditory confirmation loop | Student selects a letter/word → partner or device says it aloud → student hears it → phonological representation builds internally | Described in CbD Teacher Guide based on Foley & Pollatsek (1999), Bishop (1985) |

| E-trans board | Eye-transfer board — clear acrylic board with symbols/letters that partner holds at eye level. Student indicates by gaze | Standard AAC tool |
| Expert model | AT service delivery where one specialist (usually SLP) holds all AT knowledge and the team depends on them | Contrasted with capacity-building model in RESNA paper |
| Fitzgerald Key | Color-coding system for AAC vocabulary by grammatical function | Fitzgerald (1949), adapted by Goossens', Crain, & Elder (1992) |
| Fringe vocabulary | Topic-specific, lower-frequency words that need to be pre-programmed before a lesson | Standard AAC clinical term |

| SDI | Specially Designed Instruction — individualized instruction written into the IEP | IDEA term |
| System of least prompts | Prompting hierarchy: wait → gestural → verbal → model → physical. Start with least intrusive prompt | Standard special education instructional procedure |
