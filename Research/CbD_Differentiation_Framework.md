# CbD Differentiation Framework
*Communicate by Design | Compiled: 2026-03-29 | Version 1.0*

---

## Purpose and Use

This document is the research-backed framework governing how Communicate by Design differentiates instructional materials across text complexity versions. It exists because differentiation is the mechanism through which CbD delivers on its core commitment — that the standard does not change, only the access pathway. Without a principled, evidence-based methodology, version differences drift from "scaffolded access" toward "reduced expectation," which undermines the product's instructional validity and its legal defensibility under IDEA's access mandate.

**This framework answers four operational questions:**

1. **What changes across versions?** (The three differentiation mechanisms)
2. **What never changes?** (The locked constants)
3. **How is complexity reduced without reducing expectation?** (Sentence-level and activity-level tools)
4. **How do we know a V3 student is still meeting the standard?** (Evidence of access vs. evidence of mastery)

It applies to all Communicate by Design text-based product lines. The Lexile bands and word count targets shift by product line, but the differentiation logic is the same.

**Cross-reference:** Every new text-based product line should be evaluated against Section 5 (Product Line Application) and the Section 7 QC Checklist before build begins.

## Part 1: The Research Foundation

CbD differentiation sits at the intersection of three evidence bases. Every version decision must be defensible across all three simultaneously.

### Layer 1: Text Complexity Research

**Primary framework: Common Core State Standards (CCSS) Three-Part Text Complexity Model (2010)**

The CCSS established the most widely used model for describing text complexity across three dimensions:

| Dimension | Description | CbD Application |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Quantitative** | Lexile level, sentence length, word frequency | Lexile band targeting (V1/V2/V3) |
| **Qualitative** | Knowledge demands, text structure, language clarity, purpose | What the reader needs to know; coherence of the text |
| **Reader and Task** | What the reader brings; what the task requires | IEP goals, AAC access, prior knowledge, response modality |

**The critical finding:** Quantitative measures (Lexile) capture only one dimension of complexity. A text with short sentences can still make enormous knowledge demands. A text with complex syntax can be accessible if the student has rich background knowledge. CbD's V1/V2/V3 system addresses all three dimensions, not just Lexile.

**Lexile Framework (Stenner, Burdick, Sanford & Burdick, 2006; MetaMetrics):**

The Lexile framework measures text complexity using two variables: sentence length and word frequency. Higher Lexile = longer sentences + less common words. The research validates that:
- Moving within a 200L range constitutes a meaningful comprehension support
- Below 500L, passage length and knowledge load matter more than Lexile
- Lexile should be used as a targeting tool, not a ceiling — students regularly comprehend above their measured level when topic knowledge is high

**CbD Lexile Bands (LOCKED for all nonfiction units, Gr 6–10):**

| Version | Lexile Range | Typical Reader | Design Intent |
|---------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| **V1** | 900–1050L | Grade-level and above for Grade 6–10 | Same passage language as original source; minimal adaptation |
| **V2** | 650–800L | Two years below grade level; students with decoding support | Sentence restructuring; reduced clause complexity; same events and evidence |
| **V3** | 400–550L | Significantly below grade level; students with complex communication needs | Embedded vocabulary access; chunked sentences; choice-access activities |

**Flesch-Kincaid alignment:** V1 texts typically yield a Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level of 8–11. V2 texts yield 5–7. V3 texts yield 3–5. Grade level score is a check, not the target — Lexile is the primary measure.

### Layer 2: Scaffolding Research

**Primary framework: Wood, Bruner & Ross (1976) — Scaffolding Theory; Vygotsky (1978) — Zone of Proximal Development**

The foundational principle underlying all CbD differentiation: **a scaffold is a temporary support that keeps the task within the learner's Zone of Proximal Development without changing the goal of the task.**

Vygotsky's ZPD describes the space between what a student can do independently and what they can do with support. The purpose of a scaffold is to close that gap temporarily — not to permanently reduce the goal.

**What this means for CbD:**
- The standard is the goal. A scaffold gets the student to the standard; it does not replace the standard.
- A scaffold that changes the goal is an accommodation that reduces expectation, not an adaptation that maintains it.
- Over time, scaffolds should be withdrawn. A V3 student who never moves toward V2 is a signal about instruction, not about the product.

**Graves & Fitzgerald (2003) — Scaffolded Reading Experience:**

This framework, applied specifically to informational texts with diverse learners, identifies scaffolding at three stages:
1. **Pre-reading preparation** — activating background knowledge, pre-teaching vocabulary, setting purpose
2. **During-reading support** — structured note-taking, text chunking, embedded glosses
3. **Post-reading response** — structured response frameworks, sentence starters, evidence organizers

All three stages are present in CbD units. The differentiation framework governs what changes at each stage across versions.

**Browder, Ahlgrim-Delzell, Courtade, Gibbs & Flowers (2008) — Literacy for Students with Significant Disabilities:**

This research is specifically relevant to V3 design and to the AAC component. Key findings:
- Students with significant disabilities can and should engage with grade-level text through adapted formats
- The access pathway changes (symbol support, read-aloud, AAC response) — the content and expectation does not
- Evidence-based text access strategies include: picture-enhanced text, repeated reading with a partner, systematic vocabulary pre-teaching, and structured response opportunities with multiple modalities

**The CbD alignment:** Every CbD unit provides the evidence base that this research requires. V3 is not a simplified version — it is an accessed version.

### Layer 3: UDL and Access Mandate

**Primary framework: Universal Design for Learning (CAST, 2018, udlguidelines.cast.org)**

UDL Principle 1 — Multiple Means of Representation — is the direct research base for CbD's three-version model:

| UDL Checkpoint | Description | CbD Implementation |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **1.1** | Offer options for perceiving information | Three text versions; embedded Key Vocabulary boxes in V3 |
| **1.2** | Offer options for language and symbols | Symbol + text together; Fitzgerald Key color coding |
| **1.3** | Offer options for comprehension | Background knowledge activation; structured response frameworks |

**The UDL principle that governs differentiation:** Barriers to learning are in the design of instruction, not in individual learners. V1 is not the "real" version — it is one access pathway. V3 is not a lesser version — it is a different access pathway to the same standard. The unit is designed so that *all three versions are correct answers to the same instructional question.*

**IDEA SDI Mandate (34 CFR §300.39):**

Specially Designed Instruction is legally defined as adapting "the content, methodology, or delivery of instruction" to address the unique needs of a student with a disability. CbD's V1/V2/V3 system is a methodology adaptation. The legal requirement is that:
1. The IEP goal targets the same standard as general education peers
2. The adaptation addresses the identified disability-related need
3. The adaptation does not reduce the rigor of the standard

This framework ensures that every CbD differentiation decision is defensible under this mandate.

## Part 2: The Three Differentiation Mechanisms

### Mechanism 1: Text Complexity Reduction

Text adaptation moves from V1 → V3 through three specific tools. These tools are applied in order — not all three are required for every sentence, but all are available.

**Tool 1: Sentence decomposition**

Long, multi-clause sentences are broken into shorter sentences. The information is preserved; only the packaging changes.

| V1 | V3 |
|----|-----|
| "Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act stated that no person with a disability could be excluded from any program receiving federal money — not schools, not hospitals, not transit systems, not government offices." | "Section 504 was a law. It said people with disabilities could not be left out of programs that received money from the government. That included schools, hospitals, and transportation." |

**Hard rule:** Breaking a sentence may not remove information. Every fact, example, and piece of evidence present in V1 must appear in V3. The sentence is shorter; the content is not thinner.

**Tool 2: Syntactic simplification**

Complex syntactic structures are replaced with simpler ones:
- Passive voice → active voice ("The law was signed by the President" → "The President signed the law")
- Embedded clauses → separate sentences ("The march, which had been organized three months earlier, drew over a thousand participants" → "The march drew over a thousand people. It had been organized three months earlier.")
- Abstract noun phrases → concrete descriptions ("The administration's reluctance to implement the regulation" → "The government did not want to follow the new rules")
- Academic hedging removed or simplified ("It is worth noting that" → removed; "suggested that" → "said that")

**Hard rule:** Syntactic simplification may not change the author's meaning. "The government did not want to follow the new rules" is an interpretation of "the administration's reluctance to implement the regulation" — acceptable when the interpretation is accurate and the meaning is preserved. When in doubt, preserve the original wording.

**Tool 3: Knowledge demand reduction**

V3 passages include embedded vocabulary and context that V1 assumes the reader brings:
- Acronyms spelled out on first use in V3 (not required in V1 where reader familiarity is assumed)
- Historical background provided in a Key Vocabulary box before the passage, not within it
- Proper nouns introduced with a brief appositive ("ADAPT, a disability rights organization,...")
- Cause-effect relationships made explicit that V1 leaves to the reader to infer

**Format of V3 embedded support:**

```
> **[Term]:** [Plain-language definition in one sentence, written for the student, not the teacher]
```

This format appears as a callout box before or during the V3 passage. The box contains only vocabulary essential to accessing the passage — not a full glossary, not background instruction. Maximum 3–4 terms per passage.

### Mechanism 2: Activity Scaffold Gradient

Activities are the primary place where the standard is practiced and demonstrated. The scaffold gradient governs how activity format changes across versions without changing the skill being assessed.

**The three activity formats:**

| Format | Version | Structure | Example |
|--------|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Open-ended** | V1 | Blank space; full sentence expected | "Why do the government and the disability community describe the delay differently? Use evidence from the passage." |
| **Partial structure** | V2 | Sentence frame with key words; student provides evidence and analysis | "The government describes the delay as __________ because __________. The disability community describes it as __________ because __________." |
| **Choice/cloze access** | V3 | Options embedded in the task; student selects and indicates; sentence frame complete | "The government says the delay is about: (money / rights). Circle your answer. They say this because: __________ (from the passage)." |

**Hard rule — The Access Standard:** All three formats are assessing the same skill: the student's ability to identify and explain different perspectives on the same event using text evidence. The format changes; the cognitive task does not.

The critical test: Can a student who completes the V3 activity be said to have "demonstrated understanding of how different perspectives shape the telling of the same event, with textual evidence"? If yes, the scaffold is appropriate. If the V3 activity could be completed without engaging with the text, the scaffold has reduced the expectation.

**The sentence frame rule:**

Response sentence frames — the language students use to begin or structure their written/communicated response — must be present in ALL versions. The frames may simplify in language complexity V1→V3, but they must:
1. Require the student to supply content (not just copy a phrase)
2. Target the same sentence structure the standard demands (claim + evidence, cause + effect, etc.)
3. Use the shared response core vocabulary (because, show, prove, agree, different) — consistent motor pattern across versions

**What the sentence frame is NOT:** A sentence frame that supplies both the claim AND the evidence is no longer a scaffold — it is a fill-in-the-blank factual recall task. This collapses the standard from analysis to identification.

### Mechanism 3: AAC Response Pathway (Cross-Version)

This mechanism does not create a V4 — it is an overlay that applies to all three versions. Any student can access any version through the AAC response pathway.

**What the AAC pathway provides:**
- Core response vocabulary available on the communication board across all activities (because, show, agree, different, same, not, true, wrong)
- Fringe vocabulary pre-programmed 1–2 weeks before the unit (via CAP to SLP)
- Partner read-aloud is the assumed access mode for V3 students using eye gaze or scanning
- All activity prompts written to be read aloud — no activity depends on the student independently reading the prompt
- All response options are speakable — a student can indicate an answer orally, via AAC output, or in writing

**The version-AAC alignment:**

| Student Profile | Typical Version | AAC Consideration |
|----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Grade-level reader, AAC user | V1 | Full response via AAC; partner supports device navigation |
| Below grade-level, AAC user | V2 | Sentence frame supports motor planning; partner models fringe vocabulary |
| Significant reading support needs, AAC user | V3 | Partner read-aloud; Key Vocabulary boxes pre-teach fringe; choice/cloze format compatible with eye gaze or single switch |

**Hard rule:** Version assignment is NOT determined by AAC use alone. A student who uses a robust SGD and reads at grade level uses V1. A student who does not use AAC and reads significantly below grade level uses V3. Version selection is a reading access decision, not a communication access decision. These are separate IEP considerations.

## Part 3: The Locked Constants

These elements DO NOT change across V1, V2, or V3. If a version-to-version comparison reveals any of these have changed, the product has a differentiation error.

| Element | Why It Cannot Change |
|---------|---------------------|
| **The ELA standard being addressed** | SDI requirement: the IEP goal targets the same standard as peers |
| **The essential question of the unit** | The intellectual purpose of the lesson is the same for all students |
| **The vocabulary list** | Same vocabulary = same motor patterns; consistent AAC pre-programming; same CAP for all versions |
| **The Top 5 Core and Fringe words** | Same priority; same communication access goal |
| **The historical/factual events described** | Every fact in V1 must appear in V3; V3 does not omit events |
| **The skill type** (Close Reading, CER, Text Structure, etc.) | The skill is the instructional target; the scaffold supports it, does not replace it |
| **The response task category** | If V1 requires a claim + evidence response, V3 requires a claim + evidence response (format scaffolded, task category locked) |

## Part 4: Version Assignment — Decision Logic

Version assignment is an IEP team decision, not a teacher decision made at the moment of photocopying. CbD products provide all three versions; the unit introduction guides the team through version selection.

**The four-question version assignment protocol:**

1. **What is the student's independent reading level (Lexile or equivalent)?**
   - V1 if: student reads within 100L of the V1 band independently, or with read-aloud support and grade-level decoding
   - V2 if: student reads within the V2 band independently, or reads V1 with full read-aloud and paraphrasing support
   - V3 if: student reads below 500L, relies primarily on partner read-aloud, or has significant decoding needs alongside comprehension

2. **What is the student's primary response modality?**
   - This does not determine version — it determines what supports are activated within the version
   - An SGD user can access any version; the CAP activates the AAC pathway

3. **What does the student's IEP annual goal require?**
   - If the IEP goal references grade-level standards with accommodations → V1 or V2 with partner support
   - If the IEP goal references modified content standards → V2 or V3
   - If the IEP goal targets foundational literacy skills alongside grade-level content exposure → V3 as content access + separate foundational instruction

4. **What does the educational team agree constitutes FAPE for this student?**
   - Per *Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District* (2017), FAPE requires a plan "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." Version selection is part of that plan.

**Version assignment is not permanent.** A student who begins the year on V3 may appropriately move to V2 mid-year. The product supports this — all three versions are in the same purchase, and the vocabulary list is identical.

## Part 5: Product Line Application

### Nonfiction Reading Units (Gr 6–10)

**Lexile bands:** V1 (900–1050), V2 (650–800), V3 (400–550) — LOCKED
**Word count per passage:** V1 (550–650), V2 (450–550), V3 (330–400) — LOCKED per unit
**Activity scaffold:** V1 open-ended → V2 partial structure → V3 choice/cloze
**Vocabulary:** Same list across all versions; fringe words embedded as Key Vocabulary in V3 passage only
**AAC pathway:** Present in all versions via CAP + partner guidance in teacher document

**Common differentiation errors in nonfiction units:**
- Removing an event or piece of evidence from V3 to shorten the passage (forbidden)
- Changing the response task type between versions (e.g., V1 asks for analysis, V3 asks for identification only)
- Using different sentence frames across versions that target different grammatical structures
- Adding new information in embedded vocabulary boxes that does not appear in any version of the passage

### Fiction Anchor Texts

**Lexile bands:** TBD (not yet locked — requires fiction differentiation session)
**Differentiation logic:** Same three mechanisms apply; text adaptation follows same sentence-level tools
**Additional consideration:** Fiction character and plot events must be consistent across versions; emotional/mental state vocabulary stays in all versions (this is the core instructional target, not background)

### Picture Book Companions (K–3)

**Version model:** Not three-version Lexile model — read-aloud is the primary access mode for all students
**Differentiation:** Guided by IRA read-aloud structure; scaffold is in the partner's questions and the activity format, not in a text version
**AAC pathway:** Same vocabulary across all activity levels; Core/fringe maintained across levels

## Part 6: Passage Writing — Quality Standards

These standards govern original passage writing and passage adaptation for all CbD units.

### Historical and Factual Accuracy
- All events, dates, names, quotes, and statistics must be cited to a verifiable source
- Paraphrased historical content must accurately represent the source; interpretive language must be distinguishable from documented fact
- Quotations must be verified against the original source and not altered

### Language Standards
- Gender-neutral language throughout
- People-first OR identity-first language based on the subject's expressed preference (when known)
- Disability-affirming language: "people with disabilities," "wheelchair user" (not "wheelchair-bound"), "Deaf" (capital D for cultural identity) — follow subject's expressed preference
- Avoidance of inspiration framing: events are described for their political and historical significance, not for the emotional response they generate in non-disabled observers

### Strengths-Based and Accurate Framing
- Disability rights events are described as political actions, not charity or inspiration
- Agency language: "Jennifer Keelan crawled up the Capitol steps" — not "Jennifer Keelan was helped to crawl"
- Historical context included when necessary to understand why the event mattered

## Part 7: QC Checklist — Pre-Build

**Must be completed before any passage or activity is finalized.**

**Gate 1: Text Complexity**
- [ ] V1 Lexile confirmed within 900–1050 range?
- [ ] V2 Lexile confirmed within 650–800 range?
- [ ] V3 Lexile confirmed within 400–550 range?
- [ ] Word count within target range for each version?
- [ ] All factual information present in V3 that appears in V1?

**Gate 2: Syntactic Accuracy**
- [ ] No meaning-changing simplifications in V2 or V3?
- [ ] Passive → active voice changes preserve original meaning?
- [ ] Embedded vocabulary boxes in V3 use accurate, student-facing definitions?
- [ ] No new information added to V3 embedded boxes that contradicts V1?

**Gate 3: Activity Scaffolds**
- [ ] All versions address the same skill (Close Reading, CER, Text Structure, etc.)?
- [ ] V3 choice/cloze format still requires student to supply content (not just recall)?
- [ ] Sentence frames present in all versions?
- [ ] Sentence frames target same grammatical structure across versions?
- [ ] Response task category identical across versions?

**Gate 4: AAC Pathway**
- [ ] Vocabulary list identical across all versions?
- [ ] Fringe vocabulary embedded as Key Vocabulary in V3 only (not repeated in V1/V2)?
- [ ] All activity prompts written to be read aloud?
- [ ] Core response vocabulary present in activity scaffold across all versions?
- [ ] Partner guidance in teacher document addresses all three versions?

**Gate 5: Standards Alignment**
- [ ] Essential question identical across all versions?
- [ ] Standard code referenced in all three versions' activities?
- [ ] Version assignment guidance present in unit introduction?
