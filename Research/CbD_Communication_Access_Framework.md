# CbD Communication Access Framework
*Communicate by Design | Compiled: 2026-03-29 | Version 1.0*

---

## Purpose and Use

This document defines Communication Access — the CbD framework governing how AAC systems are integrated into every product across every product line, for every student who uses any form of AAC. It exists because AAC access is not a feature to bolt onto a finished product. It is a design principle that must be embedded from the first planning decision through the final QC gate.

Communication Access is the broader ecosystem concept. The V1/V2/V3 differentiation model is its instructional vehicle. The Communication Access Packet (CAP) is its delivery mechanism. The Communication Partner Framework governs the human implementation layer. All three must operate together for a student who uses AAC to have genuine access to grade-level content and meaningful communication practice within the instructional routine.

**This framework answers five operational questions:**

1. **What is Communication Access?** (Definition and scope)
2. **Who does it serve?** (All AAC users, any modality)
3. **How does it layer across the product ecosystem?** (K-2 phonics → fiction → nonfiction)
4. **How does it support progression and continual learning?** (Not a static overlay)
5. **What are the design rules for every product line?** (Non-negotiable build standards)

---

## Part 1: What Is Communication Access?

### Definition

Communication Access is the systematic embedding of AAC-compatible design principles into instructional materials so that any student who uses any form of AAC — speech-generating device (SGD), eye-gaze board, symbol card set, alternative pencil, low-tech communication board, or multimodal combination — can participate fully in the instructional activity and demonstrate their understanding through their own communication system.

Communication Access is not:
- A simplified version of the lesson
- A pull-out activity for AAC users
- An add-on layer applied after the unit is built
- The exclusive domain of the SLP

Communication Access is:
- A design standard embedded in every activity at the point of authorship
- Applicable to every student on every version (V1, V2, V3)
- Governed by research on what AAC-supported instruction actually requires
- A team responsibility shared by the special educator, the paraprofessional, and the SLP

### AT as a Wheel, Not a Hierarchy

AT is not a nested hierarchy (AT contains AAC contains SGD). That framing causes teams to fixate on the narrowest end — the SGD — while missing every other functional domain where a student may need support.

**The correct mental model: AT is a wheel with many spokes.** Each spoke represents a functional domain where a student's disability may create barriers:

- Reading
- Writing
- Organization and time management
- Hearing access
- Vision
- Communication ← where AAC lives
- Motor access
- Sensory regulation

A student may need AT support across multiple spokes simultaneously. Communication/AAC is one spoke. SGD is one tool at the tip of that spoke. The visual schedule, slant board, colored overlay — all AT, on different spokes.

**For all CbD content:** Use wheel/spoke framing when explaining AT scope. Never use 'AT → AAC → SGD hierarchy' language. This applies to Substack posts, product descriptions, and any practitioner-facing content.

### Within the Communication Spoke

- **AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication):** The specific AT category that supports expressive communication. Includes high-tech (SGDs, AAC apps) and low-tech (symbol boards, e-trans boards, word cards, gesture systems, alternative pencils).
- **SGD (Speech-Generating Device):** One type of AAC — a device that produces electronic speech output.

**CbD's Communication Access framework is built for AAC — not SGD-only.** Every design decision must work for a student who uses a laminated symbol board as readily as a student who uses a Tobii Dynavox.

## Part 2: The Research Foundation

### Layer 1: AAC System Integration in Academic Instruction

**Primary source: Light & McNaughton (2012); Beukelman & Mirenda (2013)**

The research on AAC system integration in academic settings identifies three conditions required for AAC users to participate meaningfully in instruction:

1. **Vocabulary availability:** The words needed to respond to an academic task must be present on the student's communication system before the lesson begins — not during or after.
2. **Partner competence:** The communication partner must have the skill to model AAC use, wait for a response, and expand on the student's output. Partner skill is the limiting variable most often cited in the research.
3. **Response opportunity design:** Activities must be designed to produce opportunities for AAC output — not just opportunities to point to an answer, but opportunities to construct a response using the student's communication system.

**The CbD implementation of these three conditions:**
1. The CAP delivers vocabulary to the SLP 1–2 weeks before the unit so vocabulary can be pre-programmed
2. The Communication Partner Framework governs partner behavior (Model, Wait, Expand, Offer Choice)
3. Every activity in every version is designed to produce a communicative response opportunity, not just a selection task

---

### Layer 2: The Communication Matrix and AAC Progression

**Primary source: Rowland (2011) — Communication Matrix; Light & McNaughton (2014)**

Communication Access is not a static design. A student who enters a CbD unit communicating at Level 5 (consistent use of symbols) and exits communicating with multi-symbol combinations has progressed — and that progression must be visible in the data the unit collects.

**The Communication Matrix (Rowland, 2011)** describes seven levels of communicative behavior:

| Level | Description | CbD Design Implication |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Pre-intentional behavior | Not the target CbD population; foundational AAC framework applies |
| 2 | Intentional but not symbolic | Choice prompts; partner attribution of intent |
| 3 | Unconventional symbolic | Object/gesture symbols; partner-modeled AAC |
| 4 | Conventional symbolic | Low-tech symbol board; consistent symbol use |
| 5 | Concrete symbols | SGD with core + fringe; partner-read-aloud primary |
| 6 | Abstract symbols (pictures) | Full SGD; multi-symbol utterance expected |
| 7 | Language | SGD or natural speech + AAC; full academic participation |

**The design principle for CbD:** Units are designed primarily for students at levels 4–7, where symbolic communication is established. The V1/V2/V3 system provides the academic access layer; the Communication Matrix level informs what partner support and vocabulary design are appropriate within that version.

**AAC progression within a unit:** A student at Level 5 who, by the end of a 4-week unit, produces consistent multi-symbol combinations when answering comprehension questions (Level 6 behavior) has demonstrated communication progress independent of academic progress. The Session Tracker captures this.

### Layer 3: Aided Language Stimulation (ALgS) and Modeling

**Primary source: Goossens' (1989); Cafiero (2001); Drager et al. (2006)**

Aided Language Stimulation (ALgS) is the evidence base for how communication partners model AAC use during instruction. The research finding is consistent: students increase their own AAC output when partners model using the same system during natural communication opportunities.

**The modeling principle for CbD units:**
- Partners do not wait for a student to initiate — they model AAC output continuously during instruction
- Modeling is most effective when it occurs at slightly above the student's current output level (1–2 symbols above current level)
- Modeling is not corrective — the partner models the "better" form while accepting the student's output as valid

**Every CbD product line embeds this principle:**
- Communication Access sections in teacher documents describe what vocabulary to model and when
- V3 units specify which fringe words to model during the vocabulary instruction step
- The Session Tracker includes a field for partner modeling tallies

---

### Layer 4: Participation Model

**Primary source: Beukelman & Mirenda (2013) — Participation Model**

The Participation Model identifies two types of barriers to AAC participation:

1. **Opportunity barriers:** Policies, practices, and attitudes that limit when and how often a student is offered the opportunity to communicate. "We don't expect her to respond to comprehension questions" = opportunity barrier.
2. **Access barriers:** Physical, sensory, or cognitive factors that prevent the student from using their communication system even when the opportunity exists. Vocabulary not pre-programmed = access barrier.

**CbD's Communication Access design directly addresses both:**
- Opportunity barriers: every unit activity is designed to produce a response opportunity for every student, regardless of modality. There is no activity in a CbD unit that assumes some students will not respond.
- Access barriers: vocabulary pre-programming via CAP, V3 vocabulary instruction, AAC-compatible activity formats (choice/cloze, symbol-accessible prompts, speakable response options).

### Layer 5: Core Vocabulary Research

**Primary source: Beukelman, Jones & Rowan (1989); Yorkston et al. (2012)**

Core vocabulary — a small set of high-frequency, high-utility words that account for the majority of what people say across contexts — is the research foundation for AAC system design.

**The finding:** Approximately 200–400 core words account for 80% of the words typically used in conversation. Core words are not topic-specific; they are relational and grammatical: *because, when, same, different, not, more, go, want, need, like, think, know*.

**CbD's application:**
- The response sentence frames across all activities use the same core vocabulary set: *because, show, prove, agree, different, same, not, true, wrong*
- Consistent core vocabulary = consistent motor patterns across units and across product lines
- A student who has learned the motor pathway to "because" in a nonfiction unit will use the same motor pathway in a fiction unit and a UFLI lesson — vocabulary transfers

**The fringe vocabulary layer:** Fringe words are topic-specific and must be pre-programmed before each unit. They do not transfer automatically. The CAP is the mechanism for ensuring fringe words are available when the unit begins.

## Part 3: The Communication Access Ecosystem

Communication Access is designed to layer systematically across CbD's full product ecosystem, from early phonics through secondary nonfiction. The goal is not a single product with AAC access — it is a coherent progression of communication and literacy development that a student can follow from K through Grade 10+.

### The Three-Layer Ecosystem

**Layer 1 — Foundation: K-2 Phonics (UFLI)**

The UFLI Phonics line introduces Communication Access at the earliest literacy level. A student learning phonics simultaneously learns the core vocabulary of phonics instruction on their AAC system. The Communication Partner Guide for each UFLI lesson is the delivery mechanism.

- Core vocabulary: *sound, word, say, same, different, try, more, done, again*
- Fringe vocabulary: phoneme-specific words per lesson (e.g., Lesson 8: /f/ words)
- Partner role: models each phoneme sound using AAC, attributes attempts, expands output
- Progression target: student produces consistent symbol output for word identification tasks by end of unit sequence

**AAC value at this layer:** Early phonics is often the first structured academic context where students with complex communication needs are expected to respond to instruction. Communication Access at this layer establishes the foundation for all later content-area communication.

**Chapter Book Line (in development):** The Fiction Anchor Text line will introduce shared reading and story-level communication access before the nonfiction complexity demands. Chapter books like Wonder and The Gift provide a natural bridge between the partner-read-aloud structure of UFLI and the text-with-activities structure of nonfiction. The chapter book line will embed:
- Shared reading structure (partner reads; student follows; designated response points)
- Story grammar vocabulary on AAC system (character, wants, feels, changes, problem, solution)
- Scene-by-scene comprehension checks using symbol-accessible choice prompts
- Multi-symbol utterance opportunities at designated response points in each chapter

This line is specifically designed to develop language and communication access *before* students face the full demand of secondary nonfiction with evidence-based writing tasks.

**Layer 2 — Bridge: Fiction Anchor Texts (Gr 3–8+)**

Fiction units provide a longer-form text structure with natural communication opportunities embedded in narrative. The Communication Access layer for fiction units:
- Emotional and mental state vocabulary: *feels, thinks, wants, believes, different from before, changed, understands* — these are the core fringe words for fiction access
- Scene-based AAC response: response opportunities tied to story events (not passage-level comprehension)
- Visual Scene Displays (VSDs): scene images from key moments in the text provide a visual anchor for vocabulary and communication about the story
- Multi-symbol utterance target: all fiction activities are designed for 2+ symbol responses

**Layer 3 — Advanced: Nonfiction Reading Units (Gr 6–10)**

Nonfiction units are the full-complexity endpoint of the Communication Access progression. Students who have built core vocabulary, fringe pre-programming habits, and partner communication routines in UFLI and fiction are prepared to use those skills in the most demanding academic context: text-based analysis with evidence.

- Communication Access at the nonfiction level requires established AAC habits — students cannot build a communication routine and engage with secondary-level text simultaneously
- The nonfiction CAP assumes vocabulary is being added to an existing, functional system
- The Session Tracker captures multi-symbol communication, not just selection behavior

## Part 4: Design Rules by Product Line

### Universal Rules (Apply to Every Product Line)

1. **Vocabulary first.** Every product ships with a clear vocabulary list (core + fringe). No product goes to build without a confirmed vocabulary plan.

2. **Partner guidance at point of use.** Communication Access instructions are embedded in the activity, not in a separate appendix the teacher will not read. If the activity requires partner AAC modeling, the guidance appears at the top of that activity page.

3. **No oral-only response opportunities.** Every response opportunity must include a pathway for a student who cannot speak. If the activity assumes the student will answer aloud, it is not Communication Access-compliant.

4. **Response mode flexibility.** Activities accept: AAC output (any modality), gesture + partner attribution, eye gaze + partner attribution, pointing, writing, circling, drawing. No activity accepts only one response mode.

5. **Speakable everything.** Every prompt, every sentence frame, every choice option must be readable aloud by a partner. No prompt depends on the student reading it independently.

6. **Consistent core vocabulary.** The response core set (*because, show, prove, agree, different, same, not, true, wrong*) appears across all product lines. Students build one motor pathway that transfers.

---

### UFLI Phonics

- Communication Partner Guide is the Communication Access document — not supplemental, not optional
- Vocabulary: phoneme-specific fringe + phonics core (*sound, word, say, try, same, different*)
- Response opportunity: built into each of the 8 lesson steps
- Progression measure: symbol output accuracy per lesson step (Session Tracker equivalent)

---

### Fiction Anchor Texts

- CAP delivers story-level vocabulary: emotional states + story grammar
- Visual Scene Display: one VSD per major scene; vocabulary anchored to image
- Response opportunities: 1 per chapter minimum; 3+ symbol response design target
- Partner role: shared reading with designated stop points for AAC response

---

### Nonfiction Reading Units

- CAP delivers content vocabulary (fringe) + confirms core vocabulary pre-programmed
- V3 units: vocabulary actively taught in unit using Descriptive Teaching Model
- Response opportunities: embedded in every activity for every version
- Session Tracker: per-session AAC data collection, assigned to paraprofessional

---

### Picture Book Companions (K–3)

- Read-aloud is the access model for all students; no silent independent reading assumed
- Three-reading structure (IRA + Dialogic Reading): each read targets a different AAC response type
  - Read 1: vocabulary introduction (student identifies target words on device)
  - Read 2: comprehension questions (student responds with 1–2 symbol utterances)
  - Read 3: extended response (student answers "why" or "how" using fringe + core)
- Vocabulary ceiling: 15–20 words per book; core words prioritized
- Partner role: reads aloud; models target vocabulary; waits 5 seconds per response opportunity

## Part 5: The CAP — Delivery Mechanism

The Communication Access Packet (CAP) is the operational delivery mechanism for Communication Access. It is not a product component that ships to students — it is a team communication document.

**What the CAP delivers:**

1. **To the SLP:** The fringe vocabulary list for the upcoming unit, with Fitzgerald Key categories and ARASAAC symbol references. Minimum 1–2 week lead time before the unit starts. SLP confirms programming completion before Day 1.

2. **To the paraprofessional:** Session Tracker and modeling vocabulary guidance. The para is the primary data collector for communication access data.

3. **To the classroom teacher:** Partner guidance embedded in the teacher document. The classroom teacher does not receive a separate CAP — their guidance is embedded at point of use.

4. **To the special educator:** Vocabulary confirmation checklist and Session Tracker summary form. The special educator reviews data and writes IEP progress notes using Session Tracker data.

**The CAP is not optional for V3 units.** It is the mechanism that makes V3 vocabulary instruction possible. A V3 unit without a pre-programmed CAP is a unit where the student cannot fully access the vocabulary instruction step.

## Part 6: Progression and Continual Learning

Communication Access is designed for progression, not maintenance. A student who uses a CbD product and does not move along the communication continuum — more symbols, longer utterances, more spontaneous output, more contexts — is a signal that something in the implementation needs to change.

### Progression Indicators by Product Line

| Product Line | Beginning of Unit | End of Unit Target | Evidence |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------------|----------|
| UFLI Phonics | Symbol identification (1 symbol/response) | Consistent symbol output for word ID tasks | Session Tracker step data |
| Picture Book Companions | Partner-prompted 1-symbol response | Spontaneous 1–2 symbol with reduced prompting | Session Tracker |
| Fiction Anchor Texts | 1–2 symbol response to partner prompt | 2–3 symbol utterances; story grammar vocabulary used | Session Tracker + permanent product |
| Nonfiction Reading Units | Pre-programmed fringe used with partner prompt | Fringe used across partners; multi-symbol CER responses | Session Tracker + rubric |

### What Progression Is NOT

- Moving from V3 to V2 is academic reading progress — not communication progress
- Increasing accuracy on a rubric is academic progress — not communication progress
- Both are valid and reportable; they are not the same thing
- The IEP should have separate measurable goals for academic skill and for communication development

## Part 7: QC Checklist — Communication Access Pre-Build

**Gate 1: Vocabulary Design**
- [ ] Core vocabulary set confirmed (consistent with cross-unit motor pathway)?
- [ ] Fringe vocabulary list complete for this unit?
- [ ] CAP delivery timeline documented (1–2 weeks before Day 1)?
- [ ] Vocabulary ceiling respected (max ~30 fringe per nonfiction unit)?

**Gate 2: Activity Design**
- [ ] Every activity in every version has at least one AAC response opportunity?
- [ ] No activity accepts only oral response?
- [ ] All prompts written to be read aloud?
- [ ] Response options are speakable (no print-only option)?
- [ ] Choice/cloze options in V3 compatible with eye gaze, scanning, pointing?

**Gate 3: Partner Guidance**
- [ ] Communication Access instructions embedded at point of use (not in appendix)?
- [ ] Partner guidance specifies what vocabulary to model and when?
- [ ] Partner guidance includes 5-second wait time with seconds named?
- [ ] Para assigned to Session Tracker data collection?
- [ ] SLP role (programming confirmation) documented?

**Gate 4: Progression Design**
- [ ] Session Tracker included and assigned to paraprofessional?
- [ ] Beginning-of-unit and end-of-unit communication targets identified?
- [ ] Data collection distinguishes academic progress from communication progress?
