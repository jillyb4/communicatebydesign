# CbD Communication Partner Framework
*Communicate by Design | Compiled: 2026-03-29 | Version 1.0*

---

## Purpose and Use

This document is the research-backed framework governing how Communicate by Design designs partner support guidance across all product lines. It exists because the instructional quality of every CbD product depends substantially on what the communication partner does — not just what materials are provided. An AAC-inclusive literacy lesson run by a partner who has no guidance on modeling, wait time, or language input may provide materials access without communication access. The materials alone are insufficient.

**This framework answers three operational questions:**

1. **Who is the communication partner in a CbD lesson?** (Role definition)
2. **What do partners DO during instruction?** (The four evidence-based behaviors)
3. **How do we build partner guidance into products without overloading teachers?** (Design principles)

It applies to every CbD product line. The specific guidance changes by product type (nonfiction vs. UFLI phonics vs. picture book companion), but the partner behavior framework is the same.

**Cross-reference:** Every new product line should include partner guidance built to this framework. Partner guidance is not optional supplemental content — it is a required instructional component.

## Part 1: The Research Foundation

### Layer 1: Aided Language Stimulation (ALgS)

**Primary source: Goossens, Crain & Elder (1989, 1992); Goossens (1994)**

Aided Language Stimulation (ALgS) is the evidence-based foundation for how communication partners model AAC use during instruction. The core principle: **the partner uses the student's communication system to model target vocabulary while providing natural spoken language input simultaneously.**

ALgS is not about prompting or correcting — it is about demonstrating. The partner points to or activates target symbols on the communication board while speaking, modeling how the system works as a natural part of communication rather than as a drill or correction.

Research findings:
- Students who received ALgS showed increased use of graphic symbols to communicate (Goossens et al., 1992)
- ALgS is most effective when it occurs throughout the natural activity, not only during designated "AAC practice" time
- Partners do not need to model every word they speak — only key vocabulary words, especially the current instructional targets

**CbD implementation of ALgS:**
- The Communication Access Packet (CAP) provides the vocabulary the partner models
- Partner guidance in every teacher document specifies which words to model during each activity
- The shared response core vocabulary (because, show, prove, agree, true, wrong) is modeled consistently across all units, building motor pattern automaticity for the student

### Layer 2: Partner-Augmented Input (PAI) and Partner Instruction Research

**Primary source: Kent-Walsh & McNaughton (2005); Kent-Walsh, Murza, Malani & Binger (2015)**

Kent-Walsh and McNaughton's research established a structured approach to teaching communication partners how to support AAC users in instructional settings. Their 8-step instructional program for partners (now widely replicated) identifies the partner behaviors most associated with increased AAC communication:

| Step | Behavior | Research Outcome |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Secure attention before communicating | Student engagement with partner increased |
| 2 | Ask open-ended questions | Student initiations and multi-symbol utterances increased |
| 3 | Use augmented input (model on device/board) | Symbol use increased |
| 4 | Allow response time (pause 5–10 seconds) | Student AAC output increased significantly |
| 5 | Acknowledge and expand student output | Longer and more complex student utterances |
| 6 | Avoid yes/no questions when possible | Student-initiated utterances increased |
| 7 | Offer choices rather than directives | Student participation and engagement increased |
| 8 | Follow the student's lead | Communication quality and spontaneity increased |

**The finding with the largest effect size in this research:** Wait time. Partners who learn to wait 5–10 seconds after asking a question or after the student begins to respond see the largest gains in AAC output. Most partners wait fewer than 2 seconds before re-prompting or answering for the student. The 5-second wait is the single most impactful behavior change — and the hardest one to sustain.

**CbD implementation:** Every teacher document includes a "Partner Guidance" section. The section always includes explicit wait time guidance, framed not as "give the student time" but as a concrete behavior: "After asking the question, count silently to 5 before speaking."

### Layer 3: Communication Partner Programs — Meta-Analysis

**Primary source: Kent-Walsh, Murza, Malani & Binger (2015) — Systematic Review; Sennott, Light & McNaughton (2016) — AAC Intervention Research**

Kent-Walsh et al.'s (2015) systematic review of 30 partner instruction studies found:
- Partner instruction consistently increases AAC-user communication
- Effects are most reliable when partner instruction includes: explicit training, practice with feedback, and a visual reference (cheat sheet or quick guide) during instruction
- **Without explicit partner instruction, materials access does not reliably translate to communication access**

Sennott, Light & McNaughton (2016) reviewed AAC intervention research and found that the quality of partner support is one of the strongest predictors of AAC system use in school settings. When partners model, wait, and expand — students use their systems more. When partners prompt, correct, and fill in — students use their systems less.

**The critical finding for CbD product design:** A quick reference card available at the point of instruction is more effective than a longer guide read before the lesson. Partner guidance built into the unit (not in a separate supplemental document) increases the probability that it is used.

### Layer 4: Social Networks and Communication Circles

**Primary source: Blackstone & Berg (2003) — Social Networks Communication Inventory**

Blackstone and Berg's research identifies five circles of communication partners for AAC users, from most familiar (family, close friends) to least familiar (unfamiliar strangers). Each circle requires different communication supports. In school settings, the relevant circles are:

- **Circle 1 (closest):** Family members and close caregivers — know the student's system and communication history
- **Circle 2:** Close school staff — special educator, primary para, SLP — familiar with the system, sometimes with training
- **Circle 3:** Less familiar school staff — content area teachers, substitute paras, lunch staff — rarely trained; often paired with students using AAC without any preparation

**The relevance for CbD:** Products typically land with Circle 3 partners — content area teachers and classroom paras who may have no AAC background. Partner guidance in CbD products must be written for Circle 3 competence: behavioral, brief, concrete, and implementable without prior AAC training.

This is the standard for how partner guidance is written in every CbD unit: **if a substitute paraprofessional who has never seen an AAC device read this guidance, would they know what to do during this activity?** If not, the guidance is insufficiently concrete.

### Layer 5: Literacy-AAC Research

**Primary source: Light & McNaughton (2012, 2014); Erickson & Geist (2016)**

Light and McNaughton's research on AAC and literacy establishes that:
- Literacy instruction for AAC users must address all four components simultaneously: phonological awareness, word identification, reading comprehension, and written expression
- Communication partners play a different role in each component — not interchangeable
- For reading comprehension specifically, partner support includes: reading the text aloud, pointing to key vocabulary, modeling a response using the student's system, and accepting AAC output as a valid response

Erickson and Geist (2016) found that when classroom teachers understand that AAC use during literacy activities is evidence of comprehension — not a distraction from it — they allow more AAC-integrated instruction. Product design that frames AAC as the comprehension output, not a support for "students who struggle," shifts teacher behavior.

**CbD alignment:** Partner guidance consistently frames AAC use as evidence of thinking. "When a student selects 'because' and 'wrong' in sequence, they are building an argument. Accept this as a complete response; expand it by modeling the next word."

## Part 2: The Four Partner Behaviors

All CbD partner guidance is built around four evidence-based behaviors. Every partner guidance section references at least the relevant behaviors for that activity type.

### Behavior 1: Model (Augmented Input)

**What it is:** The partner points to or activates target vocabulary on the communication board or device while speaking naturally.

**When to do it:** When introducing vocabulary, when giving directions, when asking a question, when accepting a student response.

**What it is NOT:** Pointing to every word spoken. Modeling is selective — it targets the instructional vocabulary and the response vocabulary for that activity.

**The rule:** Model the word BEFORE asking the student to use it. "Let me show you — [points to PROTEST] — protest. The protesters demanded their rights. Now you tell me — what did the protesters do?" Modeling is not prompting; it comes before the question, not after.

**CbD product implementation:** The CAP identifies which words to model. The teacher document specifies in the Partner Guidance section: "Model [word 1] and [word 2] during this activity."

---

### Behavior 2: Wait (Expectant Pause)

**What it is:** After asking a question or after a student begins a response, the partner waits silently for a minimum of 5 seconds before re-prompting or speaking.

**Why this is so hard:** Most communication partners in school settings wait 1–2 seconds. AAC users require additional processing time — for motor planning (navigating to the symbol), for visual scanning, and for formulating an utterance across multiple symbols. Filling that silence is the most common error partners make.

**The concrete guidance:** "After asking the question, put both hands in your lap. Count silently to 5. Do not nod, point, or redirect. Wait."

**What to do after 5 seconds with no response:** Offer a choice. "Do you think it was about the money or the rights?" — not "Can you answer?" or repeating the original question.

---

### Behavior 3: Expand (Language Expansion)

**What it is:** After the student communicates — in any modality — the partner accepts the response and adds one or two words to it, modeling a slightly longer utterance using the student's system.

**Example:** Student selects WRONG on their device. Partner says: "Wrong — yes! The company was wrong because they lied. Let me show you — [points to WRONG, BECAUSE, LIE] — wrong because they lied. Can you show me that?"

**What expansion is NOT:** Correcting. If the student selects an unexpected word, expansion works with it rather than redirecting. "You said 'money' — yes, and the disability community thought the delay was really about money AND about rights. Both."

**Why expansion matters:** Expansion models syntactically more complex language just above the student's current output. It is the AAC equivalent of the parent who says "ball" back to the child who said "ba" — it confirms the intent and models the target form.

---

### Behavior 4: Offer Choice (Rather Than Directive)

**What it is:** When a student does not respond or when the task demands more scaffolding, the partner offers a binary or constrained choice using the vocabulary from the activity.

**Example (directive):** "Tell me what Section 504 did." [No response] "Come on, we talked about this." [No response]

**Example (choice):** "Did Section 504 protect people with disabilities, or did it take their rights away?" — said while pointing to RIGHTS and PROTECT on the board.

**The research basis:** Directive questions require open recall. Choice questions reduce the cognitive load of retrieval while maintaining the conceptual demand. A student who correctly selects "protect" has demonstrated understanding — the response is valid evidence of comprehension.

**The limits of choices:** Choices should be genuine — both options must be defensible from the text (not one obvious right answer and one absurd wrong answer). A choice that only has one real option is not a comprehension check; it is a pointing exercise.

## Part 3: Partner Guidance Design Principles

### Principle 1: Built-In, Not Supplemental

Partner guidance belongs inside the unit, at the point of use — not in a separate document, not in the welcome materials only. Every CbD teacher document includes a Partner Guidance callout box for activities where AAC support is most critical.

### Principle 2: Behavior-Specific, Not Disposition-Based

Guidance written for partners must name concrete behaviors, not dispositions.

| ✗ Disposition-based | ✓ Behavior-specific |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| "Be patient with your student" | "Wait 5 seconds after asking a question before speaking again" |
| "Support AAC use" | "Point to PROTEST on the communication board when you say the word 'protest'" |
| "Help the student access vocabulary" | "Before the lesson, confirm these words are accessible on page 2 of the student's device" |

### Principle 3: Written for Circle 3 Competence

Partner guidance must be implementable by a para with no AAC training. Assume: the partner has never used the student's device, does not know what "aided language stimulation" means, and has 5 minutes to read the guidance before the lesson. This is the standard.

### Principle 4: Framed Around the Student's Thinking, Not Their Disability

Language in partner guidance refers to what the student is doing cognitively, not what they cannot do physically.

| ✗ Deficit framing | ✓ Thinking framing |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| "Because the student can't speak..." | "When the student selects a symbol, that is their answer. Accept it." |
| "Help the student who struggles with communication" | "When a student uses their AAC device or communication board, they are showing you their thinking" |

### Principle 5: The CAP is the Partner Preparation Document

The Communication Access Packet is not just for the SLP. It is the preparation document for every adult who will work with the student during the unit. The CAP introduction (page 1) always includes: what to send to the SLP, what vocabulary to pre-teach, and the 3 key partner behaviors for this unit.

## Part 4: By Product Line

### Nonfiction Reading Units (Gr 6–10)

**Primary partner context:** Paraprofessional or special educator supporting 1–3 students during content area instruction, possibly in a co-taught or pull-out setting.

**Partner guidance required in:**
- Unit introduction (general AAC orientation — 1 page max)
- Each Vocabulary Preview activity (model: which words; when)
- Each reading activity (read-aloud guidance; how to handle V3 embedded vocabulary)
- Each response activity (wait time; which choice to offer if no response; how to expand a one-symbol response)

**The non-negotiables:**
- Model vocabulary during the Vocabulary Preview using the CAP symbol cards
- Read V3 passages aloud; do not ask the student to read independently before modeling comprehension
- Wait 5 seconds after every question in the Perspective Comparison, CER, or ESR activities

### UFLI Phonics

**Primary partner context:** Special educator or interventionist delivering explicit phonics instruction.

**Partner guidance required in:**
- Each of the 8 lesson steps (the UFLI Lesson Activity player includes AAC access modes per step)
- Focus is on phoneme production + symbol/board access simultaneously
- Model: when to point to the symbol for the target word during blending practice

### Picture Book Companions (K–3)

**Primary partner context:** Special educator or SLP delivering read-aloud-based comprehension.

**Partner guidance required in:**
- Pre-reading vocabulary preview
- During-reading think-alouds (model language; pause for student input)
- Post-reading response activities (choice-access format; expansion guidance)

## Part 5: QC Checklist — Pre-Build

**Gate 1: Partner Guidance Completeness**
- [ ] Partner guidance included in unit introduction?
- [ ] Partner guidance included in vocabulary preview activities?
- [ ] Partner guidance included in reading activities?
- [ ] Partner guidance included in response activities?
- [ ] CAP page 1 includes partner preparation instructions?

**Gate 2: Behavior Specificity**
- [ ] At least one guidance callout references wait time with a specific number of seconds?
- [ ] At least one guidance callout names which vocabulary to model and when?
- [ ] At least one guidance callout explains how to respond to a student AAC output?
- [ ] No guidance language uses disposition-based framing?

**Gate 3: Circle 3 Test**
- [ ] Could a para with no AAC training read this guidance and know what to do during this specific activity?
- [ ] Is every instruction a behavioral direction (not a value statement)?
- [ ] Is guidance 5 lines or fewer per activity (longer guidance is not read)?
