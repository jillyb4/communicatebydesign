## What OpenAAC Is

OpenAAC is a small non-profit focused on increasing awareness, global access, and collaboration related to AAC. Their annual State of AAC report is drawn from practitioner surveys, AAC users, and families across the US and internationally. This is year three of the report. It is a credible, community-sourced pulse on the field — not a peer-reviewed study, but a meaningful snapshot of what practitioners and families are experiencing on the ground.

## Key Findings Relevant to CbD

### 1. S2C (Spelling2Communicate) Is Growing — and It's a Problem

The report notes that more S2C providers are appearing around the country, and despite the highly-contested nature of the approach, more families are defending it in online communities.

**Why this matters for CbD:** S2C falls in the same evidentiary category as Facilitated Communication (FC) and Rapid Prompting Method (RPM). ASHA, AAC-RERC, and most major professional bodies have position statements against these practices. The core concern: a partner physically holds or positions the letterboard, creating serious risk of inadvertent cueing or outright message authorship by the supporter rather than the communicator. Independent authorship cannot be confirmed.

**The harder truth the report surfaces:** Families are turning to S2C because they feel a need unmet by traditional practitioners and strategies. That gap is real. The AAC field has not done enough to meet families where they are with evidence-based tools that center the communicator's independent voice. This is the gap CbD products are designed to fill — every CbD activity is built around verified, independent communication access. The student selects, points to, or uses gaze to indicate their own response. That is not a small thing in this landscape.

**CbD Post #5 connection:** "Who Gets to Decide If the Voice Is Real?" (published April 10, 2026) speaks directly to this tension — facilitated spelling skepticism, presuming competence of both student AND partner, the ASL interpreter analogy, and what partner training standards actually require.

**The framing to hold:** The problem is not families wanting their child's voice heard and believed — that instinct is correct and essential. The problem is that S2C doesn't verify *whose* voice is being heard. A genuinely AAC-literate team using research-supported tools with independent message authorship can do what families are hoping S2C does — without the authorship question.

The report asked practitioners what would improve AAC adoption and usage. The list maps directly onto what CbD already does or is building toward:

CbD's Auditory Confirmation Loop content (Post #6, April 14, 2026) addresses the *pedagogical* side of this — what it means to confirm that a communicator's output was received and understood. The field is asking for a tech solution; CbD is documenting the human practice that has to exist regardless of whether the tech ever arrives.

### 3. The AAC Ecosystem GPA

Overall GPA: **1.9** (same as 2024). Key grades:

- Education: **D** (up from F — still a near-failing grade)
- Funding: **C-** (up from D+)
- Marketing/Outreach: **D** (down from D+)
- Families: **C+** (down from B)
- Individualization: **C+** (unchanged)

**SDI and teaching context:** The Education grade is the most directly relevant to CbD's product lines. An Education grade of D means the field has identified a near-systemic failure in how AAC is being taught and supported in schools. This is the exact gap CbD's instructional products (nonfiction units, fiction anchor texts, picture book companions, UFLI phonics companion) are designed to address — not by training SLPs, but by equipping the educators and paraprofessionals who are in the room every day.

The low Individualization score (C+) also matters: it means teams are not consistently tailoring AAC to the individual communicator. The V1/V2/V3 differentiation model in every CbD unit is a direct response to this — the scaffold varies, the expectation does not.

### 4. "Trouble With a Capital T" — Bad Habits Still in the Field

The report flagged practices that the community still struggles to eliminate:

- Hand-over-hand strategies still in use
- Requiring participation on AAC devices (compliance-based, not communication-based)
- Refusing AAC use during literacy instruction
- Taking AAC devices away as punishment

**CbD teaching context:** These are not fringe behaviors — they are documented, widespread, and the reason CbD's Communication Partner Framework and Communication Access Framework exist as built-in product components, not supplemental guides. The partner guidance in every CbD unit is written for Circle 3 competence (a paraprofessional with no AAC training) precisely because the field cannot assume training has happened.

**Refusing AAC during literacy instruction** is particularly relevant: every CbD nonfiction, fiction, picture book, and UFLI product is explicitly designed with AAC access integrated into the literacy instruction itself — not as an add-on after the lesson.

---

### 5. AAC Decision-Making and Inclusion

The report asked how often AAC users were included in decision-making about their own care and about AAC efforts in general. Results were poor — AAC users are frequently excluded from decisions that directly affect them.

**CbD philosophy alignment:** The CbD AAC philosophy holds that every access method is equally valid, and the SGD is never the goal — communication is the goal. That framing is inherently student-centered and presumes competence. It directly contradicts the compliance-based, adult-directed model that the OpenAAC data shows is still dominant.
