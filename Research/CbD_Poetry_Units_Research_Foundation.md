### Why Poetry Belongs in the SPED ELA Classroom

The Common Core State Standards explicitly include poetry as a text type at every grade band (CCSS Appendix B). Standards RL.4, RL.5, and RL.6 all reference the poem as a primary unit of analysis. Poetry is not optional enrichment in a standards-based curriculum — it is a required text type that every student, including students with IEPs, has the right to access.

For students with complex communication needs, poetry offers specific advantages that prose does not. Its brevity reduces decoding demand without reducing analytical expectation. Its reliance on sound, pattern, and rhythm creates multi-modal entry points. Its emotional vocabulary is among the highest-frequency AAC vocabulary in existence. The CCSS do not waive this standard for students with disabilities; CbD's access-layer model ensures the standard is met with appropriate support.

## Section 2: Professional Organization Grounding

### ASHA (American Speech-Language-Hearing Association)

ASHA's position on AAC in school settings establishes that students who use AAC have the right to participate in academic instruction across all content areas, including language arts. Vocabulary pre-programming prior to instruction is identified as an SLP responsibility that must occur in coordination with the educational team, not after instruction begins. The CbD CAP (Communication Access Packet) formalizes this handoff — delivering the SLP the fringe vocabulary list, Fitzgerald Key categories, and ARASAAC symbol references at least one to two weeks before Day 1 of the unit.

*Reference: ASHA. (2023). Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC). American Speech-Language-Hearing Association. https://www.asha.org/njc/aac/*

### CEC (Council for Exceptional Children)

The CEC Initial Preparation Standards for special educators ground the poetry line's design in four areas. Standard 3.1 requires systematic instruction aligned to IEP goals — every CbD poetry unit ships with IEP goal stems (academic + AAC) built into the teacher document. Standard 3.3 requires research-supported instruction — the NFMA strategy is grounded in cognitive strategy instruction research (Harris & Graham, SRSD framework). Standard 5.2 requires planning for AAC and AT support — the CAP is the delivery mechanism. Standard 6.2 requires collaboration with team members — the partner guidance embedded at point of use, written for Circle 3 partners, directly addresses this.

The CEC journal *Teaching Exceptional Children* has documented that special educators should leverage high-frequency expressive vocabulary students already have on their AAC systems to support academic participation. Poetry's emotional and observational vocabulary aligns directly with what is most likely to already be accessible on a student's communication system.

*Reference: Council for Exceptional Children. (2015). What every special educator must know: Professional ethics and standards (7th ed.). CEC.*
*Reference: Biggs, E. E., Carter, E. W., & Gilson, C. B. (2018). Facilitated AAC modeling and peer partner training. Teaching Exceptional Children, 50(4), 213–222.*

### CAST / Universal Design for Learning

The UDL Guidelines (CAST, 2018) provide the theoretical framework for the V1/V2/V3 access-layer model. Checkpoint 1.1 (offer ways of customizing the display of information) supports the variable annotation tools across versions. Checkpoint 5.1 (use multiple media for communication) grounds the read-aloud as instruction rather than accommodation. Checkpoint 6.1 (guide appropriate goal-setting) grounds the NFMA strategy as a metacognitive tool. Checkpoint 8.4 (increase mastery-oriented feedback) grounds the rubric design: evaluating skill application with evidence, not interpretation correctness.

The key UDL principle underlying the access-layer model for poetry is this: variability in the means of representation (how students receive the poem) and in the means of expression (how they respond) does not require variability in the standard. All three versions target RL.x.4. The access layer is the variable. The expectation is not.

*Reference: CAST. (2018). Universal Design for Learning Guidelines version 2.2. http://udlguidelines.cast.org*

### IRIS Center (Peabody College, Vanderbilt University)

The IRIS Center offers two directly applicable secondary reading modules. Secondary Reading Instruction, Part 1 (Teaching Vocabulary and Comprehension in Content Areas) establishes that literary terms — metaphor, simile, stanza, speaker, tone — function as Tier 3 domain vocabulary and should be taught using the same explicit instruction approaches applied to content-area vocabulary in science and social studies. This grounds CbD's use of the Descriptive Teaching Model for literary device terms in the V3 vocabulary teaching step.

Secondary Reading Instruction, Part 2 (Deepening Middle School Content-Area Learning) addresses comprehension strategy instruction and graphic organizer use for middle school learners, including students with disabilities. The strategy-graphic organizer pairing is the structural model for how NFMA is delivered across versions: the strategy is the same; the organizer scaffolds vary by version.

The IRIS Center's Explicit Instruction EBP summary establishes the research base for the modeling-first approach in all three versions. Before students analyze a poem for figurative language, the teacher models the complete NFMA sequence on a different poem. This is not optional enrichment — it is the instructional practice that research identifies as most effective for students with learning disabilities engaging with literary analysis.

*Reference: IRIS Center. (n.d.). Secondary Reading Instruction (Part 1): Teaching Vocabulary and Comprehension in the Content Areas. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/module/sec-rdng/*
*Reference: IRIS Center. (n.d.). Secondary Reading Instruction (Part 2): Deepening Middle School Content-Area Learning. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/module/sec-rdng2/*
*Reference: IRIS Center. (n.d.). Evidence-Based Practice Summaries. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/resources/ebp_summaries/*

## Section 3: High-Leverage Practices (HLPs)

*Reference for all HLPs: McLeskey, J., Barringer, M-D., Billingsley, B., Brownell, M., Jackson, D., Kennedy, M., Lewis, T., Maheady, L., Rodriguez, J., Scheeler, M. C., Winn, J., & Ziegler, D. (2017). High-leverage practices in special education. Arlington, VA: Council for Exceptional Children & CEEDAR Center.*

### Primary HLPs for Poetry Units

**HLP 12 — Systematically design instruction toward a specific goal**
Every poetry unit is sequenced: Vocabulary Preview (introduce literary terms before reading) → Partner Read-Aloud (access to the poem's sound) → NOTICE step (literal observation, lowest demand) → Figurative Language identification (apply the skill) → FEEL and MEAN steps (interpretive, higher demand) → Response Activity (demonstrate the standard) → Synthesis across poems (generalization). Each step prepares students for the next. No step is skipped.

**HLP 14 — Teach cognitive and metacognitive strategies**
NOTICE/FEEL/MEAN/ASK is a four-step cognitive strategy for poetry analysis grounded in the Self-Regulated Strategy Development framework (Harris & Graham). Students who have internalized the strategy can apply it to any poem, not just unit poems. The strategy is explicitly taught using the same steps as SRSD: activate background knowledge → discuss the strategy → model the strategy → support practice → independent practice. The Vocabulary Preview Routine and Descriptive Teaching Model for literary terms are vocabulary metacognitive strategies embedded within the unit sequence.

**HLP 15 — Provide scaffolded supports**
Scaffolds in poetry units follow a gradient: V1 has blank annotation columns and open response; V2 has annotation cards (physical symbol cards placed next to lines while reading) and structured sentence frames; V3 has symbol-coded response cards and a Descriptive Teaching Model pre-reading vocabulary step with active partner involvement. Scaffolds are calibrated to the reading access need — not the disability label. They are designed to be removed over time as vocabulary motor patterns develop.

**HLP 16 — Use explicit instruction**
The Modeling Lesson (section 10 of every unit) requires the teacher to model the complete NFMA analysis process on a different poem — not the unit poem — before students touch any poem in the unit. The think-aloud names every analytical move: "I'm going to use the NOTICE step first. I notice the word 'dark' repeats three times. Let me write N in the margin." This explicit naming-and-modeling is not assumed to happen naturally. It is scripted in the teacher document so any partner can deliver it with fidelity.

**HLP 22 — Provide intensive instruction**
The Checkpoint Protocol (section 11 of every unit) fires after Poem 1. The teacher reviews the NFMA Response Activity responses, checks the rubric, and uses the five-minute re-teach script if the student cannot identify the figurative device before Poem 2 begins. The checkpoint is formative, not evaluative. It serves as SDI documentation for the IEP.

**HLP 8 — Provide positive and constructive feedback**
Poetry is inherently interpretive. Two students reading "We Wear the Mask" may identify different figurative devices as most significant, and both may be correct. The rubric evaluates whether the student applied the skill with evidence — not whether their interpretation matches a key. This requires explicit guidance in the teacher document about how to respond when a student's interpretation is unexpected but defensible.

### The HLP 16 Tension and Its Resolution

Explicit instruction (HLP 16) and poetry's interpretive nature can appear contradictory. The resolution used in every CbD poetry unit: the procedure is taught explicitly; the interpretation is not. The teacher models what it looks like to notice a device, name it, and explain its effect — not which device is the "right" one to notice. Normalizing multiple valid answers is explicitly included in the modeling script.

## Section 4: Evidence-Based Instructional Practices

### 4.1 Explicit Instruction

Explicit instruction is among the most well-validated practices for students with learning disabilities engaging in literary analysis. It requires that teachers state objectives clearly, model the skill with think-aloud before students practice, provide guided practice with feedback, and gradually release responsibility.

For poetry analysis, explicit instruction means the teacher reads a poem aloud, models each NFMA step out loud ("I notice… I feel… I think this means… I wonder…"), and names the figurative device identified before any student practice begins. Research consistently shows that explicit instruction in when and how to use comprehension strategies — not just exposure to strategies — is what produces learning gains for students with disabilities.

*Reference: Archer, A. L., & Hughes, C. A. (2011). Explicit instruction: Effective and efficient teaching. Guilford Press.*
*Reference: Swanson, H. L. (1999). Instructional components that predict treatment outcomes for students with learning disabilities: Support for a combined strategy and direct instruction model. Learning Disabilities Research & Practice, 14(3), 129–140.*

### 4.2 Cognitive Strategy Instruction

The NFMA strategy is CbD's adaptation of the cognitive strategy instruction framework developed by Harris and Graham under the Self-Regulated Strategy Development (SRSD) model. SRSD has the strongest evidence base of any strategy-instruction approach for students with learning disabilities in written expression and reading comprehension tasks.

NFMA adapts three SRSD principles for poetry analysis: (1) the mnemonic reduces cognitive load by externalizing the procedure (students do not have to hold the steps in working memory — they are always visible); (2) the sequence moves from lowest cognitive demand (NOTICE — literal observation) to highest (MEAN — interpretation, ASK — generative questioning), matching the graduated cognitive release recommended in SRSD; (3) the strategy is explicitly taught using a structured instructional routine before any independent application occurs.

*Reference: Harris, K. R., Graham, S., Mason, L. H., & Friedlander, B. (2008). Powerful writing strategies for all students. Paul H. Brookes Publishing.*
*Reference: Graham, S., & Perin, D. (2007). A meta-analysis of writing instruction for adolescent students. Journal of Educational Psychology, 99(3), 445–476.*

### 4.3 Graphic Organizers

Research supports graphic organizers as a high-effect-size intervention for students with learning disabilities engaged in literary analysis, including text-based question answering and evidence-based written response. Graphic organizers reduce extraneous cognitive load by providing structure for information the student is building, freeing working memory for the analytical task itself.

In poetry units, the NFMA sequence functions as a graphic organizer: the four boxes (NOTICE / FEEL / MEAN / ASK) give students a visual framework for capturing observations as they read and listen. The V2 annotation cards are a physical graphic organizer — students place symbol cards (N, F, M, ?) next to the lines they respond to, externalizing the annotation act.

*Reference: Dexter, D. D., & Hughes, C. A. (2011). Graphic organizers and students with learning disabilities: A meta-analysis. Learning Disability Quarterly, 34(1), 51–72.*

### 4.4 Repeated Reading with a Purpose

Repeated reading with a purpose is one of the National Reading Panel's supported fluency interventions. Poetry is structurally suited to this practice: a 14-line poem can be read three times in the same lesson without fatigue or stigma. Each reading has a different purpose — first for experience (the teacher reads while students listen), second for structure (where does the poem break? what repeats?), third for analysis (where is the figurative device?).

For students who use AAC, repeated exposure to the same vocabulary in the same context is the primary vocabulary acquisition pathway. Hearing "mask" and "hide" and "smile" in three separate readings of the same poem — with the partner modeling the AAC symbols each time — creates the repetition needed for motor pattern development.

*Reference: National Reading Panel. (2000). Teaching children to read: An evidence-based assessment of the scientific research literature on reading and its implications for reading instruction. National Institute of Child Health and Human Development.*
*Reference: Kuhn, M. R., & Stahl, S. A. (2003). Fluency: A review of developmental and remedial practices. Journal of Educational Psychology, 95(1), 3–21.*

### 4.5 Multi-Sensory Engagement and the Read-Aloud as Instruction

Poetry is a multi-modal text type: it is written to be heard as much as read. Sound devices — alliteration, assonance, rhyme, rhythm — are auditory features. They cannot be fully experienced from silent reading. For students with reading difficulties who process auditory language effectively, the read-aloud may be the primary access pathway.

CbD's design decision: in all three versions of every poetry unit, the partner reads the poem aloud expressively before any analysis begins. This is not an accommodation — it is the instruction. Everyone in the classroom hears the poem before anyone analyzes it. The expressive read-aloud guidance in the partner callout specifies: read with expression, pause at line breaks, pause for 5 seconds at the end of each stanza before continuing. For students who use AAC, the 5-second pause after each stanza is the response window.

*Reference: Cunningham, A. E., & Stanovich, K. E. (1998). What reading does for the mind. American Educator, 22(1–2), 8–15.*

## Section 5: AAC and Poetry — Specific Research

### 5.1 AAC Users and the Right to Literacy Participation

Beukelman and Light establish that communication and literacy are inseparable for students with complex communication needs — reading and writing support language development, and language development supports communication. The implication is that students who use AAC should not be removed from literary instruction; they need literary instruction with appropriate access design. Poetry, with its emotional and imaginative vocabulary, is a particularly high-value genre for language development in AAC users.

*Reference: Beukelman, D. R., & Light, J. C. (2020). Augmentative and alternative communication: Supporting children and adults with complex communication needs (5th ed.). Paul H. Brookes Publishing.*

### 5.2 Vocabulary Pre-Programming as the Prerequisite for Participation

Without pre-programmed vocabulary, an AAC user cannot produce a symbol-based response to a poem. The fringe words in a poetry unit — metaphor, mask, speaker, stanza — are not on most AAC systems. They must be added by the SLP before instruction begins. Binger and Light's research demonstrates that when partners are trained to model AAC use during instruction (aided language stimulation), students produce more multi-symbol utterances. But modeling cannot happen if the vocabulary is not there to model.

The CAP is the mechanism that makes this possible: it delivers the fringe word list, ARASAAC symbol references, and Fitzgerald Key categories to the SLP with a one-to-two week lead time.

*Reference: Binger, C., & Light, J. (2007). The effect of aided AAC modeling on the expression of multi-symbol messages by preschoolers who use AAC. Augmentative and Alternative Communication, 23(1), 30–43.*

### 5.3 Non-Speaking Communicators and Poetic Expression

The Institute for AAC and Spelling to Communicate (I-ASC) has published documentation that non-speaking individuals who communicate through letter-boarding, spelling, and AAC systems are among the most poetic communicators. The organization notes: "Non-speaking writers are among the most poetic individuals, and there is overlap between autistic language use and the formal techniques associated with poetry, including repetition, meter, incantation, symbolism, and figurative language."

This is not an edge case. Pattern, repetition, rhythm, compressed meaning — the formal features of poetry — mirror how many AAC users process and produce language. Introducing poetry analysis to students who use AAC is not simplifying the task. For some students, it is finally providing the right genre.

CbD's original poem in Unit 1 — a disability-affirming poem about voice and communication access — acknowledges this directly. The goal is not only that students analyze poetry; it is that they recognize their own experience and communication in what poems can do.

*Reference: I-ASC. (n.d.). Poetry Thrives in the Non-Speaking Mind. https://i-asc.org/poetry-thrives-in-the-non-speaking-mind/*

### 5.4 Emotional Vocabulary as Dual-Purpose Academic Vocabulary

The vocabulary students need to respond to poetry — feel, think, wonder, notice, mean, describe, compare, same, different, because — is among the highest-frequency vocabulary on robust AAC systems. This creates an important advantage: students who use AAC are not behind on poetry response vocabulary. They may already have motor pathways for these words.

The instructional design implication: core response vocabulary for poetry units should be identified as such (words already on the device, accessible with one to two hits) and distinguished from fringe vocabulary that requires SLP programming. The CbD CAP makes this distinction explicit, giving the SLP the fringe list without duplicating vocabulary already pre-programmed.

*Reference: Brady, N. C., Bruce, S., Goldman, A., Erickson, K., Mineo, B., Ogletree, B. T., ... & Wilkinson, K. (2016). Communication services and supports for individuals with severe disabilities: Guidance for assessment and intervention. American Journal on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities, 121(2), 121–138.*

## Section 6: The NFMA Strategy — Research Basis

**NOTICE → FEEL → MEAN → ASK** is CbD's adaptation of cognitive strategy instruction for poetry analysis. It differs from TPCASTT (the most widely used poetry analysis framework in secondary ELA) in the following ways:

| Feature | TPCASTT | NFMA |
|---------|---------|------|
| Steps | 7 | 4 |
| AAC compatibility | Low — "paraphrase" demands high metacognitive language production | High — all 4 steps use core AAC vocabulary frames already on most systems |
| Cognitive demand progression | Not explicitly sequenced | Explicit: NOTICE (literal) → FEEL (affective) → MEAN (interpretive) → ASK (generative) |
| Partner delivery | Not designed for partner support | Designed for Circle 3 partner (substitute para, 5 min before class) |
| IDEA compliance | Not designed for standards alignment | Each step directly maps to standards: NOTICE=RL.x.1/4; FEEL=RL.x.2 mood/tone; MEAN=RL.x.2 theme/RL.x.4; ASK=RL.x.1 evidence gap |

**The NFMA-AAC Phrase Frames:**
- NOTICE: "I notice ___" — pairs with any core vocabulary + a content word
- FEEL: "This poem makes me feel ___" — core emotion vocabulary (feel + descriptor)
- MEAN: "I think this means ___" — think + content vocabulary
- ASK: "I wonder ___" — wonder + question construction

*Research basis: Harris, K. R., Graham, S., Mason, L. H., & Friedlander, B. (2008). Powerful writing strategies for all students. Paul H. Brookes Publishing. SRSD framework applied to literary analysis.*

## Section 7: IEP Goal Research Basis
                                               Every CbD poetry unit ships with two goal stems: one academic (targeting the RL standard) and one AAC communication goal.

**Academic Goal Structure (IDEA format):**
Given [condition: poem + version + supports], [student] will [observable verb] [skill + standard code] as measured by [NFMA Response Activity rubric], achieving [criterion: 3 of 4 / 4 of 5] across [consistency: 3 consecutive lessons / 2 sessions].

Observable verbs for RL.x.4 (figurative language): identify, name, explain, describe, distinguish, cite, support with evidence

**AAC Communication Goal Structure:**
Given [condition: poetry response activity with symbol cards and partner modeling], [student] will produce [a 2+ symbol utterance combining core + fringe vocabulary] as measured by [Communication Session Tracker], achieving [4 of 5 opportunities] across [2 partners before mastery is reported].

*Reference: Browder, D. M., Wakeman, S. Y., Flowers, C., Rickelman, R. J., Pugalee, D., & Karvonen, M. (2007). Creating access to the general curriculum with links to grade-level content for students with significant cognitive disabilities: An explication of the concept. Journal of Special Education, 41(1), 2–16.*
