## Part 1: ELA Standards Foundation for Poetry (Grades 6–10)

### The Standard Landscape

Poetry is not a strand unto itself in the Common Core. Instead, it lives inside **Reading Literature (RL)** — specifically the **Craft and Structure** cluster — and the **Language (L)** strand for figurative language vocabulary.

| Standard | Grade | What It Requires |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **RL.x.4** | 6–12 | Determine meaning of words/phrases including **figurative and connotative** meanings; analyze impact of specific word choices on meaning and **tone** |
| **RL.x.5** | 6–12 | Analyze how a sentence, stanza, or structural element fits into the overall text; at higher grades: how **form** (e.g., sonnet) contributes to meaning and **aesthetic impact** |
| **RL.x.6** | 6–12 | Analyze how **point of view** or purpose shapes content; poems require students to distinguish **speaker from author** |
| **RL.x.2** | 6–12 | Determine **theme** or central idea; in poetry, how it develops through imagery, refrain, and structural repetition |
| **RL.x.1** | 6–12 | Cite **textual evidence** (applies to all literature, including poetry — "the text" is the poem) |
| **L.5.5a–c** | K–12 | Understand figurative language: **simile, metaphor, personification, hyperbole, allusion** |
| **L.6** | 6–12 | **Domain-specific vocabulary acquisition** — literary terms are Tier 3 vocabulary |

### Grade-Band Progression: What Gets Harder

| Grade Band | Expectation Level |
|------------|------------------|
| **6–7** | Identify figurative language with support; note structural choices; understand speaker vs. author |
| **8** | Analyze *how* structure contributes to meaning; infer connotative/tonal shifts |
| **9–10** | Analyze **cumulative impact** of word choices; evaluate aesthetic choices |
| **11–12** | Independent evaluation of "particularly fresh, engaging, or beautiful" language |

**CbD grade band recommendation: 6–10** — same as nonfiction line. This captures the widest market and covers the entire RL.4/5/6 progression where poetry instruction is most intensive.

### Which Standard Is the "Unit Skill"?

Per CbD product architecture, each unit targets ONE skill. For a poetry line, the five viable unit skills are:

| Skill | Standard(s) | CbD Skill # |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Figurative language (simile, metaphor, personification) | RL.x.4, L.5.5 | NEW — call it **#12** |
| Speaker vs. author / point of view | RL.x.6 | NEW — call it **#13** |
| Structural choices (stanza, line breaks, form, refrain) | RL.x.5 | NEW — call it **#14** |
| Theme through imagery and repetition | RL.x.2 | Possible extension of existing #2 (summarizing) |
| Synthesis/comparison across poems | RL.9-10.9 | Extension of existing #10 (synthesis) |

## Part 2: Evidence-Based Instructional Practices for Poetry

### What the Research Says Works

**1. Explicit Instruction (HLP 16)**
The most robust evidence base for any student with a disability and literary analysis. Before students read and analyze a poem, the teacher must *model the entire analysis process on a different poem.* Not "let's figure this out together" — the teacher names the strategy, demonstrates it with think-aloud, then transfers responsibility. This is non-negotiable in SPED settings.

*Research base: IRIS Center "Secondary Reading Instruction" modules; Archer & Hughes (2011) Explicit Instruction framework; CEEDAR Center HLP documentation.*

**2. Cognitive Strategy Instruction — Adapted Mnemonic**
TPCASTT (Title, Paraphrase, Connotation, Attitude, Shift, Theme) is the most widely-used poetry analysis protocol in grades 6–12. It functions exactly like the annotation codes in CbD's nonfiction line — it gives students a PROCEDURE to follow, not an open-ended invitation to "think about" the poem.

For CbD, TPCASTT will need adaptation because:
- "Paraphrase" is cognitively demanding for V3 students (requires restating the poem line by line)
- The word "connotation" is Tier 3 vocabulary requiring explicit teaching
- AAC users need step-level specificity ("for each step, here is what you do and how you respond")

**CbD adapted strategy recommendation: NOTICE → FEEL → MEAN → ASK**

| Step | What It Is | AAC Response |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **NOTICE** | What do you observe? (imagery, repetition, sound words) | "I notice ___" — core + fringe |
| **FEEL** | What does this poem make you feel? | Core emotion vocabulary |
| **MEAN** | What do you think this poem means? | "I think this means ___" |
| **ASK** | What question do you still have? | "I wonder ___" |

This is CbD-original, standards-aligned, AAC-compatible, and simpler than TPCASTT without sacrificing rigor. The four steps scaffold toward RL.x.2 (theme) and RL.x.4 (word meaning/tone) across all versions.

**3. Graphic Organizers (HLP 15 — Scaffolded Supports)**
Poetry analysis benefits enormously from visual frameworks. Research shows graphic organizers reduce cognitive demand while maintaining rigor in literary analysis for students with LD, ASD, and ID. For poetry specifically, useful organizers include:
- Figurative language identification charts (device → example from text → what it means)
- Speaker/Author distinction T-chart
- Stanza-by-stanza meaning tracker (what happens in each stanza + how you feel)
- Poem annotation codes (parallel to CbD's annotation system in nonfiction)

**4. Repeated Reading (EBP — naturally embedded in poetry)**
Poetry is short. This means *repeated reading with a purpose* is structurally native to poetry instruction — reading the same 14-line poem three times (first for experience, second for structure, third for analysis) never feels like remediation. For students building fluency, this is a powerful, non-stigmatizing practice. For AAC users, repeated exposure to the same vocabulary in context is the vocabulary acquisition pathway.

*Research base: National Reading Panel (fluency strand); Kuhn & Stahl (2003); directly supports CAP vocabulary pre-programming strategy.*

**5. Visual Scene Displays for Imagery**
Poetry's imagery is inherently visual. A poem with strong concrete imagery ("I see the dark sky, / the tired hands, / the cracked earth") can be accompanied by a Visual Scene Display that maps vocabulary to the image. This is already embedded in the Fiction product line (V2) and extends naturally to poetry.

**6. Multi-Sensory Engagement — Sound Devices**
This is where poetry *uniquely* diverges from prose as a text type. Alliteration, assonance, rhyme, and rhythm are **auditory features**. They cannot be fully experienced from silent reading. For many students with disabilities — particularly those with autism who are drawn to sound patterns, or those with reading differences who process auditory language well — the *sound of the poem* may be the most accessible entry point.

**Instructional implication:** Every CbD poetry unit must include a **read-aloud protocol** in which the teacher/partner reads the poem aloud expressively before any analysis begins. This is not optional — it is the instruction.

## Part 3: HLP, CEC, and IRIS Resource Grounding

### High-Leverage Practices (HPLP for Students with Disabilities)

| HLP | What It Addresses | How It Applies to Poetry |
|-----|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **HLP 12** | Systematically design instruction toward a specific goal | Sequence: Vocabulary Preview → Partner Read-Aloud → NOTICE step → Figurative Language identification → Response Activity |
| **HLP 13** | Adapt curriculum tasks and materials | The V1/V2/V3 access layer system; annotation cards adapted to symbol-coded response cards |
| **HLP 14** | Teach cognitive and metacognitive strategies | NOTICE/FEEL/MEAN/ASK as a strategy; explicit instruction in what "metaphor" means (Descriptive Teaching Model) |
| **HLP 15** | Provide scaffolded supports | Graphic organizers for each poem; sentence frames; visual reference for figurative language terms |
| **HLP 16** | Use explicit instruction | Teacher models complete analysis of a DIFFERENT poem before students touch the unit poem; think-aloud names every move |
| **HLP 22** | Provide intensive instruction | Checkpoint after NOTICE step; re-teach if student cannot identify the figurative device before moving to MEAN step |
| **HLP 8** | Provide positive and constructive feedback | Response to poetry is often affective and subjective — there is no one "right" answer; rubric language must reflect this |

**Key HLP tension in poetry instruction:** HLP 16 (explicit instruction) and the creative/interpretive nature of poetry can feel contradictory. A poem about grief can mean something different to every reader, and that is *the point.* The resolution: CbD teaches the **analytical procedure** explicitly (HLP 16), not the "right answer." The rubric evaluates *whether the student applied the skill with evidence*, not whether their interpretation matches a key.

## Part 4: The AAC/Communication Access Framework — How Poetry Changes Everything

### The Most Important Insight in This Document

Poetry changes the CbD differentiation architecture in a fundamental way. This must be resolved before any unit is built.

**In nonfiction units:**
- V1/V2/V3 = three different TEXT versions at different Lexile levels
- Same activities, different reading demands
- The TEXT is the variable

**Poetry cannot use this model.** You cannot write a V1/V2/V3 version of "We Wear the Mask" by Paul Laurence Dunbar. A simplified version of a poem is a different poem. The vocabulary, the line breaks, the sound devices — these ARE the poem. Reducing them destroys the very thing you're teaching.

**In poetry units, the text does NOT change across versions.**
- V1/V2/V3 = SAME POEM, different ACCESS LAYERS
- Different annotation tools, response frames, vocabulary scaffolds
- The ACCESS LAYER is the variable

**This means poetry units follow the Fiction / Picture Book Companion model, not the Nonfiction model.**

This is a critical architectural decision that unlocks everything else.

---

### What This Means for V1/V2/V3

| Version | What Changes | What Stays the Same |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| **V1** | Open annotation with written codes; open-ended response | The poem; the essential question; the skill; the CAP vocabulary |
| **V2** | Structured annotation cards (symbols/codes printed on cards); sentence frames | The poem; the essential question; the skill; the CAP vocabulary |
| **V3** | Symbol-coded response cards; Descriptive Teaching Model for ALL unfamiliar vocabulary in the poem; partner reads aloud and teaches vocabulary before first read | The poem; the essential question; the skill |

**V3 is the most intensive version of access to the SAME rigorous text.** The student using V3 is being taught MORE vocabulary, more explicitly, by a more active partner — not being given a less demanding task.

---

### Why Poetry Is Uniquely Powerful for AAC Users

**1. Emotional vocabulary IS the academic vocabulary of poetry.**
The words students need to respond to poetry — *feel, think, wonder, notice, mean, compare, describe, sad, angry, hopeful, hidden* — are core and high-frequency AAC vocabulary. A student using AAC is not "behind" on poetry vocabulary. In many cases, these are words already on their system.

**2. The brevity of poetry creates natural processing time.**
A 14-line sonnet takes 90 seconds to read aloud. A partner can read the entire poem twice before any analysis begins. For AAC users who need processing time, this is built-in rather than an accommodation bolted on. Line breaks create literal white space on the page — visual wait time.

**3. Poetry is meant to be heard, not just read.**
The multi-modal nature of poetry (sound + print) is uniquely accessible for learners who process auditory language well but struggle with decoding. The read-aloud is the instruction, not an accommodation. This removes the "who gets to have it read aloud" stigma entirely — everyone hears the poem.

**4. Non-speaking individuals have always been poets.**
The I-ASC organization documents extensively that non-speaking autistic writers are often among the most poetic thinkers. Pattern, repetition, rhythm, compressed meaning — these mirror how many AAC users experience language. Introducing poetry to students who use AAC is not simplifying the task; for some students, it is finally giving them the right genre.

**5. Creating poetry is an AAC output pathway that prose writing does not offer.**
A student who cannot produce a 5-paragraph essay may be able to produce a 4-line poem. Poetry's compression makes it a natural expressive format for students with complex communication needs. If CbD's poetry line includes a **writing response option**, the poetry unit becomes a product that supports BOTH comprehension AND communication output.

### Communication Access Framework — Poetry-Specific Rules

Building on the existing CbD Communication Access rules, poetry instruction requires:

**Rule 1: Read-aloud IS the instruction.**
Every poem in every version must be read aloud by a partner before analysis begins. This is not the accommodation — it is the lesson. Partner reads with expression; pauses at the end of each stanza; repeats the full poem before comprehension work begins.

**Rule 2: Sound devices must be named and demonstrated in the read-aloud.**
When the partner reads a poem with alliteration or rhyme, they must name it: *"Did you notice how those words sound the same at the beginning? That's called alliteration."* This is explicit vocabulary instruction embedded in the act of reading.

**Rule 3: Literary terms are Tier 3 fringe vocabulary — ALL require pre-programming.**
Unlike nonfiction units where some fringe words can be skipped at V1/V2, literary device terms (metaphor, simile, personification, stanza, refrain, tone) must be pre-programmed for ALL versions because the academic task requires students to USE these terms in their responses.

**Rule 4: Emotional vocabulary is the core AAC response vocabulary for poetry.**
Add to the standard cross-unit core set: **feel, wonder, notice, mean, describe, compare** — these are the academic functions of poetry analysis AND high-frequency AAC vocabulary. Build motor pathway consistency across the product line.

**Rule 5: Visual Scene Displays for imagery-rich poems.**
Any poem with strong concrete imagery — a place, a person, an object — should include a Visual Scene Display as a V2/V3 resource. This is not a separate deliverable; it is a half-page illustration in the student packet.

**Rule 6: Writing a poem is a valid academic response.**
For students who are AAC users, producing a 4-line poem using core + fringe vocabulary IS a demonstration of the skill (RL.x.4 — using figurative language with meaning). The rubric must accommodate this response pathway.

## Part 5: Strategic Product Line Decisions

### Decision 1: Poem Selection Strategy

Three options, each with tradeoffs:

| Option | What It Means | Risk | Recommendation |
|--------|--------------|------|---------------|
| **A. Overlay existing curriculum (copyrighted poems)** | Build CbD access layers for widely-taught poems like "Still I Rise," "The Road Not Taken" | Copyright gray area; many commonly taught poems are post-1923 and actively copyrighted | ⚠️ Only viable with clear fair use review per title |
| **B. Public domain poems only** | Select from pre-1923 published works (Dickinson, Dunbar, Whitman, Markham) | Some poems are less familiar; CbD must curate deliberately | ✅ Recommended as primary anchor strategy |
| **C. Original poems written for the unit** | Write original poetry at target Lexile level; control all variables | Requires skilled literary writing; may lack "classroom currency" | ✅ Recommended as companion to public domain anchors |

**Decision recommendation:** **Use public domain poems as primary anchors + write 1 original CbD model poem per unit at V3 reading level.** The original poem:
- Demonstrates the skill explicitly (it is a "model" poem for teaching the device)
- Can be disability-affirming and AAC-positive without copyright restriction
- Gives CbD brand identity in a market where no one else is doing this

### Decision 2: Unit Architecture

| Option | Structure | Pros | Cons |
|--------|-----------|------|------|
| **Single-poem unit** | Deep dive into 1 poem, 5–6 lessons | Familiar; focused | Not how most teachers use poetry; limited evidence opportunities |
| **Thematic anthology** | 3–5 poems around 1 theme, 1 essential question | Matches classroom practice; richer vocabulary repetition; synthesis opportunities | More complex to build; copyright management harder |
| **Skill-based collection** | 2–4 poems chosen to exemplify 1 device | Direct standards alignment; market gap on TPT | Feels less like a "unit"; may not match teacher curriculum |

**Decision recommendation:** **Thematic anthology with a skill focus.** Each unit organizes 3–4 poems around:
1. A **theme** (Identity, Voice, Resilience, Change)
2. A **target skill** (figurative language, speaker's POV, structural choices)
3. An **essential question** students answer using evidence from multiple poems

This mirrors how most middle school and high school ELA teachers actually teach poetry — not as isolated single-poem analysis, but as a multi-text conversation about a shared idea.

### Decision 3: Grade Band

**Recommendation: Grades 6–10** — same as nonfiction line. This is where:
- RL.x.4/5/6 is most intensively taught and assessed
- Students with disabilities are most likely to have adapted support services
- The CbD buyer persona (SPED teacher in middle and high school) is concentrated
- RL.4 and RL.5 in grades 4–5 could be a future extension

### Decision 4: The TPCASTT Question

TPCASTT is widely used but not AAC-accessible. CbD's adaptation should be its own named strategy:

**CbD Recommended Strategy: NOTICE → FEEL → MEAN → ASK (NFMA)**

| Step | Cognitive Demand | Standard Addressed | AAC Phrase Frame |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| **NOTICE** | Literal observation | RL.x.1, RL.x.4 | "I notice ___" |
| **FEEL** | Affective response | RL.x.2 (mood/tone) | "This poem feels ___" |
| **MEAN** | Interpretive | RL.x.2 (theme), RL.x.4 | "I think this means ___" |
| **ASK** | Generative | RL.x.1 (evidence gap) | "I wonder ___" |

This maps directly onto the existing core AAC vocabulary set. The four-step structure is simple enough for the Circle 3 partner test (substitute para, 5 minutes before class). It is *still rigorous enough for V1 students* to produce college-prep level analysis when the response format demands evidence.

### Student Response Format (Each Poem — V1/V2/V3)

**V1 — Annotation + Open Response**
- Poem printed with annotation column
- NFMA annotation codes: N = I notice; F = I feel; M = I think this means; ? = I wonder
- Response prompt: "Select ONE figurative device from the poem. Identify the device, quote the line, and explain what it means. Use evidence from the poem."

**V2 — Structured Response Frame**
- Poem printed (no annotation column — full width)
- Annotation cards (pre-printed cards with N/F/M/? symbols to place next to lines while reading)
- Sentence frames: "The poet uses [device] in line ___ when they write '___.' This means ___."
- Box for partner to model one example first

**V3 — Symbol-Coded Response + Descriptive Teaching Step**
- Vocabulary Teaching Step before reading: 3–4 key literary terms taught using Descriptive Teaching Model
- Poem printed in large text with generous line spacing
- Partner reads aloud twice; student listens
- Symbol response cards for NOTICE step: student selects or points to the observation
- Response activity: "In this poem, the poet uses a [picture of metaphor/simile/personification] because ___" — student selects the device AND supplies the "because" (from choice options OR original AAC output)

## Part 7: Phase 0 — Concept Validation

### 0.1 — The Instructional Gap

**What the problem is:** Special education teachers and SLPs need poetry analysis units that include built-in communication access design — pre-programmed vocabulary, partner guidance, AAC response pathways — so that students who use any form of AAC can participate in poetry instruction and demonstrate grade-level literary analysis skills.

**Evidence:**
- TPT search for "poetry AAC" and "poetry adapted special education grades 6-10" returns sparse results — none with CbD's depth of AAC integration, partner guidance, or IEP goal language
- The I-ASC organization (AAC community) explicitly documents that non-speaking individuals are powerful poetic thinkers — there is no curriculum product designed to honor and develop this
- Facebook SPED groups regularly ask "how do I teach figurative language to my students who don't understand non-literal language" — this is a real Monday-morning problem
- RL.x.4 figurative language is one of the most tested standards in grades 6–8 — and one teachers report the most difficulty differentiating for SPED students

**Monday sentence:** A student who uses AAC can hear a poem, feel something, and have a meaningful thought about it — but without pre-programmed literary vocabulary and a partner who knows how to support interpretation, they can't show what they know.

### 0.2 — Standard

Primary: **RL.6.4 / L.5.5a–c** (figurative language, first unit)
Secondary: RL.x.5 (structure), RL.x.6 (speaker/POV) for future units

### 0.3 — Primary Buyer Persona

**SPED teacher in a 6–10 ELA co-teaching or pull-out setting** who needs:
- A ready-to-use unit they can implement without SLP present for basic instruction
- IEP goal language built in
- A unit that does not require removing the student from grade-level content

Secondary: SLP who wants to connect poetry analysis to expressive language goals using high-value emotional + literary vocabulary.

### 0.4 — Competitor Gap

**What exists on TPT:**
- Generic poetry analysis worksheets (no AAC, no differentiation)
- Autism-specific poetry writing activities (single-session, not standards-based)
- TPCASTT graphic organizers (no AAC, no partner guidance)
- Adapted nonfiction reading units (no poetry)

**CbD gap:** There is no AAC-adapted, standards-based, multi-poem poetry analysis unit for grades 6–10 on TPT with communication access design, partner guidance, and IEP goal stems. This is a confirmed gap.

## Part 8: Phase 1 Framework Gates — Poetry-Specific Notes

### 1.1 — Vocabulary Framework

**New vocabulary type needed:** *Literary Analysis fringe vocabulary*

This is a new category not yet covered by the existing vocabulary framework. It includes:
- **Tier 3 literary device terms:** metaphor, simile, personification, hyperbole, stanza, refrain, speaker, tone, mood, imagery, symbolism, alliteration
- **Tier 2 interpretive terms:** describe, compare, explain, identify, analyze, support, evidence
- **Affective/emotional vocabulary (semi-core):** feel, wonder, notice, hidden, mask, voice, silence, hope, pain, joy

⚠️ **The Vocabulary Framework document must be extended with a Section 4f: Poetry Units** before this product line can pass Phase 1 Gate 1.1. This is a research task, not a build task.

**Fringe word ceiling:** 15–20 words per unit (matching Picture Book Companion model, not nonfiction's 30-word maximum). Reason: literary device terms are highly transferable *across units* — once metaphor is pre-programmed, it stays pre-programmed. This reduces SLP setup burden over time.

### 1.2 — Differentiation Framework

**Critical modification:** The existing V1/V2/V3 Lexile differentiation model does NOT apply to poetry. The modification needed:

*Standard model:* Different text → same activities
*Poetry model:* Same text → different access layers

This requires a documented exception to the differentiation framework. Recommend adding a note to `_Operations/memory/differentiation_reference.md` — a "Poetry Line Exception" section — once this product line is approved.

**V3 for poetry:** Even more intensive than V3 nonfiction. The partner must:
1. Pre-teach 4–6 vocabulary terms before reading (Descriptive Teaching Model)
2. Read the poem aloud twice with expression
3. Lead the NOTICE step together before independent response
4. Model one complete NFMA sequence using AAC
5. Wait 5 seconds at each response prompt

### 1.3 — Communication Access

The rule changes: **read-aloud is the instruction, not the accommodation.** This needs explicit documentation in the Communication Access section. The CAP must include:
- Expressive read-aloud guidance (how to read the poem with intention)
- Specific vocabulary to model DURING the read-aloud (e.g., "When you read the line 'we wear the mask,' model 'hide' and 'face' and 'feel' on the AAC system")
- Wait time after each stanza (5 seconds minimum before prompting)
