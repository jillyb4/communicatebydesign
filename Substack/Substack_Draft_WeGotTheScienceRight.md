What does access to demonstrating literacy look like?

Sit with that for a second before you answer.

Not what literacy looks like. Not what reading instruction looks like. What does access to *demonstrating* it look like — for a student who doesn't speak, or who communicates differently than the curriculum expects?

Most of us were never asked that question. We were handed a definition of literacy that had the answer built in: a student who reads is a student who reads aloud.

---

## We got the science right and then built the wrong door

Science of Reading got something profoundly correct. Reading happens in the brain. Phoneme-grapheme mapping, decoding, comprehension — these are cognitive processes. They don't live in the mouth. They live in the mind.

That insight changed how we teach reading. Structured literacy. Explicit phonics. Systematic instruction. All of it rooted in what the brain actually does when it encounters print.

And then we assessed it entirely through speech anyway.

Timed oral readings. Read-alouds. Answer the comprehension question out loud. Tell me what happened in the story. Words per minute — spoken words, specifically — as the measure of fluency.

We followed the brain science all the way to the classroom and then stopped at the mouth.

---

## Writing isn't speech either

Here's the other side of it, because it applies just as cleanly to writing.

Writing is what happens when you take what's in your brain and get it onto a page. That's it. The cognitive act — organizing meaning, selecting words, building a sentence that says what you mean — none of that requires speech. It requires thought. It requires language. It requires a way to get what's inside out.

A student who selects symbols to build a sentence is writing. A student who uses eye gaze to choose between answer options is demonstrating comprehension. A student who types with a switch, or points to a letter board, or dictates to a partner — that student is writing. The act is the same. The access route is different.

What requires speech is the *assessment.* Not the skill. Not the knowledge. Just the specific door we've been asking everyone to walk through.

---

## The research found skills that were already there

Two studies came out this spring — Caron and colleagues in April, Babb and Caron in March — both looking at structured phonics instruction for minimally speaking autistic children who use AAC. Parents delivered the instruction at home after brief virtual training. The results: large to very large effect sizes. Generalization to words never practiced. Skills built in ten weeks.

What the research found wasn't new literacy. It was a way to see what was already happening.

Nobody handed these families — or their children's teachers — a clear picture of what literacy instruction and demonstration could look like for a student who doesn't read aloud. When someone finally did, the skills showed up.

That's not a research finding about parents. That's a research finding about access.

---

## What this doesn't require

It doesn't require a new curriculum. It doesn't require a specialist certification. It doesn't require anyone to lower the expectation.

It requires asking a different question about what counts as evidence.

A student who selects the correct response from a choice array just demonstrated comprehension. A student who builds a sentence using core vocabulary just demonstrated written expression. A student who identifies the rhyming word using eye gaze just demonstrated phonological awareness. The standard didn't change. The door did.

General education teachers weren't trained on this. Neither were most special educators. Everyone was handed a curriculum that assumed speech and told to adapt — without anyone defining what adaptation actually looks like when a student's access to literacy was never through their voice.

That's not a failure. That's an unanswered question that the field is only beginning to take seriously.
