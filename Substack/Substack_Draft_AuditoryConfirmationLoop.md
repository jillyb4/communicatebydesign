### What You Say After Your Student Responds Is the Whole Lesson

When a student using AAC selects the letter card for *m*, you say — what exactly?

Most partners say some version of "Good job." Some say "That's right, the letter *em*." Both responses feel supportive. Both responses leave the student with nothing they can use.

Here's the distinction that changes outcomes: a student who communicates using a speech-generating device, an e-tran board, symbol cards, or any other AAC system cannot hear themselves produce the phoneme. When a speaking student says "/m/," they hear themselves. They feel the air stop behind their lips. The sound closes the loop. For a student who uses AAC to respond, that loop stays open — unless a communication partner closes it.

The partner is the auditory model. What the partner says after the student responds isn't a reward. It's the instruction.

---

**The Auditory Confirmation Loop is a specific partner behavior.** It goes like this: after a student indicates the correct response — selecting, using gaze to identify, or pointing to a symbol or letter card — the partner produces the actual phoneme out loud. Not the letter name. Not a generic affirmation. The sound.

"/m/" (lips together, brief hum). Not "em." Not "correct!" Not "yes, that's the *m* sound."

The sound itself.

This isn't a small stylistic preference. Kent-Walsh and McNaughton (2005) found that the quality of partner phoneme delivery has a measurably greater impact on learning outcomes for students who use AAC than for students who speak — precisely because the AAC user has no auditory self-monitoring loop of their own. The partner's confirmation is doing work that speech does automatically for every other student in the room.

And yet this distinction — produce the phoneme, not the letter name, not a generic affirmation — is not in any standard para training manual. It isn't covered in most professional development sessions on AAC. It isn't in the IEP. It just isn't taught.

---

**What this looks like in a real lesson:**

Step 4 of a UFLI phonics lesson. The learning objective is the phoneme /m/. The student uses a gaze board to indicate the correct letter card.

❌ Partner: "Good job! That's right!"
→ Student heard: praise. No phoneme. Loop stays open.

❌ Partner: "Yes, the letter *em*."
→ Student heard: the letter name, not the sound. Still no phoneme. Loop stays open.

✅ Partner: "/m/." (Pause.) "/m/." (Then, optionally: "You've got it — /m/.")
→ Student heard: the actual phoneme, twice. Loop closes.

The difference between these responses isn't effort or care. Every version of this partner is trying. The difference is knowing what the student needs from the confirmation — and that distinction was simply never taught.

---

**The evidence behind the loop.**

The ATIA 2021 report on Project Core showed that 93% of students beginning AAC intervention started at the lowest communication level — and only 13% reached proficiency after one year. There are many reasons for that gap. Partner training is one of them. Not because partners are doing it wrong, but because the research on what effective AAC partnership looks like has not reliably made it into classrooms, onto data sheets, or into the four hours of professional development most paraprofessionals receive before working with a complex communicator.

The auditory confirmation loop is one specific, teachable behavior. It's not the whole picture. But it's a place to start.

---

**The Monday takeaway.**

This week, notice what you say after your student responds during any phonics activity. Just notice. Is it praise? A letter name? A complete word? Or the actual phoneme — the sound that closes the loop?

Then try this: after your student indicates the correct response, produce the phoneme twice in a row. "/m/." Pause. "/m/." That's it. No additional explanation required. Let the sound do the work.

If you work with a team, share this one shift. It doesn't require a new tool, a new system, or a new IEP goal. It requires knowing that the confirmation IS the instruction — and that what gets said in the three seconds after a student responds is not a formality.

It's the lesson.
