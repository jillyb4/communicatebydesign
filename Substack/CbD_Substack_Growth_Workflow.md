## Part 1: Brand & Voice Audit

### Primary Audience (Locked Apr 11, 2026)

Communicate by Design writes for two people.

The first is the parent of a child who uses AAC — someone who presumes their child is competent, who is watching skills develop in ways the system hasn't figured out how to measure yet, and who is looking for tools, language, and evidence to support what they already know is true about their kid.

The second is the teacher — general education or special education — who wants materials designed for all students from the start, not adapted after the fact. Someone who knows something isn't working in the way literacy is being taught or assessed for their students who use AAC, but hasn't been given a different framework to try.

These two readers are the same person in different rooms. They share the same belief: that communication is a right, that competence should be presumed, and that the gap is never the student.

IEP team members — SLPs, paras, OTs, RBTs, BCBAs, clinicians — are part of every conversation. The evidence base serves them directly. But the primary voice is always: here is what we know, here is what it looks like, go try it with your student or your child.

**Use this as the filter for every post:** If a parent of an AAC user and a classroom teacher both walk away with something they can try, the post is working. If only a credentialed specialist can use it, rewrite it.

---

### What the Posts Tell Us (Voice Audit)

**Voice:** Evidence-based but never academic. Names the thing people are quietly thinking and backs it with citations. Consistent structure: name the wrong assumption → show the evidence that dismantles it → tell people what to do about it. Written as an ally — the gap is always the system or the missing tool, never the person.

**Goals across post types:**
- *Welcome post:* Position and trust-build. No sell.
- *Origin story posts (Keiko):* Demonstrate what CbD materials look like before they're on TPT.
- *Policy posts:* Pure authority. Says the quiet part out loud, gives people the research they didn't know they needed.
- *Instructional Practice posts:* One concrete idea, one research anchor, one thing to try Monday.
- *Community posts (Poetry Month and beyond):* Open a conversation. Invite people to name what they're already doing.

**The consistent promise:** Here is what we know. Here is what it looks like. Go try it.

## Part 2: Content Pillars & Market Gap

### The 5 Pillars

**🔴 Pillar 1 — Policy Hot Takes**
Dismantling assumptions practitioners have been handed without evidence. The statistic post is the model. Fast, hot, citable. These are the posts that get shared in Facebook groups.

*Research mines:*
- The 90% / 70-90% literacy statistic (Koppenhauer & Yoder, 1992 vs. 30 years of evidence since)
- AT consideration is a legal requirement, not optional (IDEA 34 CFR §300.324)
- "They can't do phonics" — and what the research actually says (Bishop & Robson, 1989; Barton-Hulsey et al., 2018)
- Sight-word-only programs are not literacy programs (Browder et al., 2006; Erickson & Koppenhaver, 2020)
- The absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence (Barker, Saunders & Brady, 2012 — only 26 kids in the entire research base)

**🟠 Pillar 2 — Teach It Tuesdays**
Practical, actionable, family-and-teacher-facing. "This is what you do on Monday." Noticing, responding, environment engineering, expectant delay, aided language input. One skill per post.

*Research mines:*
- Auditory Confirmation Loop — what the partner says AFTER the student responds (CbD framework, drawn from Hanser 2007, CLDS protocols)
- Partner phoneme delivery quality matters more for nonspeaking students than for speaking ones (Kent-Walsh & McNaughton, 2005)
- Project Core outcomes: 93% at lowest level → 13% proficient in one year (ATIA, 2021 — most compelling single-school data point)
- Home literacy environments predict literacy outcomes (Koppenhaver, Evans & Yoder, 1991)

**🔵 Pillar 3 — Instructional Practice / UFLI**
The deep technical series, starting June. How adapted phonics actually works. Builds toward UFLI product launch.

*Research mines:*
- Speech production is not required for phonemic awareness (Bishop & Robson, 1989; Foley & Pollatsek, 1999; Card & Dodd, 2006)
- The AAC keyboard problem: letter names vs. phonemes, the digraph gap, PRC-Saltillo vs. AssistiveWare vs. Tobii Dynavox positions
- Inner voice instruction for AAC users (Erickson 2003; Farrall, janefarrall.com)
- The 8-step UFLI structure and where each step breaks for nonspeaking students
- Non-speech response formats validated in research (picture arrays, letter card selection, adapted Elkonin boxes, yes/no verification, sorting)

**🟢 Pillar 4 — Nonfiction Origin Stories**
Behind-the-scenes essays about why a particular unit or topic matters. Keiko post is the model. Seasonal-tied. Drives TPT traffic.

**🟡 Pillar 5 — Real Questions, Real Answers**
The highest-leverage, fastest content. Someone asks a question in a Facebook group. You write a 700-word post that answers it. Drop the link in the thread. 7.5 million viewers vs. a TPT listing.

*This is the engine. See Part 3.*

### The Gap Nobody Is Filling on Substack

Most special ed Substacks are either:
- Resource dump accounts (links to TPT, no analysis), or
- Parent advocacy accounts (emotional, not evidence-based)

Nobody is doing what you're doing: practitioner-credentialed, research-cited, short-form policy takes written in plain language for the people closest to students. That's the white space.

## Part 3: The Fragment-to-Post Workflow

### Source Type A — The Real-Time Question (Highest Priority)

This is today's model. Someone asks something in a Facebook group that deserves a real answer.

**Steps:**
1. Screenshot or note the question verbatim
2. Ask: Can I answer this in 700-900 words with at least one research citation? If yes, it's a post.
3. Write: Open with the question exactly as asked (paraphrased to protect identity). Answer it directly in the first paragraph. Don't make them wait.
4. Add one layer of evidence — one study, one legal cite, one data point.
5. Close with what they can do about it on Monday.
6. Publish. Then go back to the Facebook thread and drop the link. Keep the comment short: *"I actually wrote about this — [link]. The short version is..."*

**Examples ready to write:**
- Denise Decostal: is her work still used? → Post: "Yes. Here's what the 2025 research says happened to the framework she built."
- The Koppenhaver & Yoder question (today's post) — DONE ✅

---

### Source Type B — Research File Mining

Your research files contain 15+ ready-to-post findings that most practitioners have never seen. Each finding = one post.

**The research-to-post formula:**
1. Pick one finding from the research files (see the list in Part 2 under each pillar)
2. Frame it around a wrong assumption teams actually hold ("They can't do phonics because they can't speak")
3. Drop the citation in. One is enough. You're not writing a literature review.
4. Add one concrete implication: what this means for the next IEP meeting, data sheet, or device trial.
5. That's the post.

**Ready-to-write posts from existing research:**
- "The AAC keyboard is lying to your student during phonics instruction" (1.4, 1.5, 1.6 from AAC Phonics Research doc)
- "Only 26 kids. That's the entire research base. Here's why it still matters." (1.2)
- "What to say when the IEP team says 'we can't assess her phonemic awareness'" (1.11 — DAPA-AP and APAR)
- "The Auditory Confirmation Loop: a one-sentence shift that changes what your student hears during phonics" (CbD framework, Section 1.14–1.15)
- "PRC-Saltillo just did something no other AAC company has done" (1.6)
- "Project Core changed a school in one year. Here's the one number that proves it." (1.12)

## Part 4: Tool Stack & Automation

### Idea Capture

**The simplest system that works:** Apple Notes shortcut on your home screen labeled "CbD Ideas." When you see a Facebook question or have a thought, paste the question text in with the source. Review weekly. Anything with a research connection gets promoted to a post brief.

**Alternative:** Voice memo to yourself. Transcribe later.

The goal is frictionless capture — you saw something in a Facebook group at 9pm and you need to save it in three seconds.

---

### Cross-Promotion to Facebook

**The workflow:**
1. Post goes live on Substack
2. Wait 24–48 hours (Substack notifies subscribers first; this respects the subscriber relationship)
3. Find the Facebook thread that prompted the post OR post to a relevant group as a comment
4. Drop the link with a one-sentence teaser. Example: *"Someone in a group last week asked about [topic]. I wrote the full answer here — [link]. The short version: the research is clearer than most teams think."*

**Groups to prioritize:**
- The ones with 7.5M+ viewers (you know which ones)
- Groups where the question originated
- Tier 1 Substack accounts' associated Facebook pages (Tim Villegas, Ashley Nyce)

**Do not:** Auto-post to Facebook the moment a Substack goes live. Looks spammy. The time delay + personalized comment does the work.

---

### Instagram

For every post published, a 3-slide carousel is the standard. Slides:
1. The wrong assumption (provocative, shareable)
2. The one piece of evidence
3. The takeaway + "Full post at the link in bio"

## Subscription Tiers — Content Plan (Locked Apr 10, 2026)

**Threshold to activate paid tier: 200 free subscribers.**
Current strategy: every post + every FB group drop drives toward this number. Do not activate paid tier before reaching 200.

### Free Tier (All Subscribers)
All 5-pillar posts — public, no paywall. Every post Jill publishes is free to read. Free is the growth engine; paid is the retention layer.

### Paid Tier (After 200 Subscribers)
Suggested price: **$8/month or $80/year**

Paid subscribers get three things free subscribers don't:

**1. Monthly Resource Drop**
One classroom-ready tool or template per month. Not a full TPT product — a single-use, immediately actionable resource. Examples: a partner script card, a NFMA annotation template, a one-page AAC vocabulary reference for a specific unit, an IEP goal stem bank for a skill type. These are things that take 10 minutes to use on Monday morning. Build these from existing product material — they extend the value of what's already built without creating net-new work.

**2. Early TPT Access**
Paid subscribers get a 48-hour preview link before any new TPT product goes live. They see it first. This creates a loyalty signal and a pre-launch review window — early buyers become early reviewers, which drives TPT ranking.

**3. Monthly Q&A Thread**
One Substack thread per month where Jill answers 5 subscriber questions. Questions submitted via comments or email. This is the highest-value offer — direct access to Jill's expertise — and it costs nothing to produce beyond 30–45 minutes of focused writing once a month.

## The Big Picture

**The flywheel:**
Real FB question → 700-word Substack post → Link dropped in FB group thread → Substack subscribers grow → More practitioners find TPT → TPT sales fund the next product → Next product becomes the next Substack post

**The rule:** Every post should be able to answer "what does someone do differently on Monday because they read this?" If you can't answer that, the post isn't done.

**The frequency:** One post every 10–14 days is sustainable and enough. Two posts per month means 24 posts in a year. That's a significant body of evidence-based practitioner writing. Consistency beats volume every time.
