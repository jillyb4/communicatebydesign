# Who Gets to Decide If the Voice Is Real?
*Pillar: 🔴 Policy & Advocacy | Target: 700–900 words | Status: Draft — April 10, 2026*

---

I've spent 13 years in speech therapy waiting rooms. I'm also a special educator. So when I watch our field debate whether certain students are really communicating — I'm not watching from the outside. I'm sitting in both chairs at the same time.

I can't scroll through my feed without landing in the middle of a conversation about whether facilitated spelling is real communication. Whether it's evidence-based. Whether we can trust what these students are producing. And every time I do, I find myself asking the same question: why is our field — the one that exists to help people find their voice — the one leading the skepticism?

I'm not here to settle the debate. I'm here to ask why we're so comfortable being skeptical of these communicators specifically.

---

My daughter uses a Tobii eye gaze device. High tech, low gaze, the full ecosystem. She has worked harder to produce a single sentence than most people work for anything in their lives. And she has been questioned — not just by adults, but by her own peers — about whether her work is really hers. Whether her para helped her. Whether what came off that screen actually came from her.

She uses a Tobii. Nobody handed her a letterboard. And still, the question follows her.

I watched *Out of My Mind* on Disney+ with her recently. For probably the thousandth time — and if you want to tell me representation doesn't matter, watch a child see herself on screen and come back to me. There's a scene where the principal pulls Melody aside after she earns her spot on the Whiz Kids team and asks, gently, whether her aide might have helped her with the answers. It's okay if she did, the principal says. She's being kind. She genuinely thinks she's being fair.

That scene wrecked me. Because it's not fiction. That's Tuesday.

And here's what that scene understands that our field sometimes doesn't: the skepticism doesn't come from malice. It comes from a system that has never fully decided whether to believe these kids. The principal isn't a villain. She's a product of a culture that learned to doubt before it learned to listen. And our kids absorb that. Their classmates absorb it. And they reproduce it in the hallway, at lunch, in the middle of a group project. *Did your aide help you with that?*

We didn't protect my daughter from that question by giving her a high-tech device. The question followed the communication. Not the method.

---

Now let's talk about the evidence argument, because it's real and it deserves a straight answer.

The research on facilitated communication raises legitimate concerns. There are studies — controlled, careful studies — showing that in some cases the message was coming from the facilitator, not the student. That happened. It caused harm. I'm not asking anyone to pretend otherwise.

But here's what I want to know: when did we apply that same standard to everything else?

E-tran boards have been in classrooms for decades. Core vocabulary symbol systems. Partner-assisted scanning. Low-tech AAC in every form. The field built consensus around these methods through clinical experience, practitioner knowledge, and accumulated practice — not randomized controlled trials. We don't ask families to produce peer-reviewed evidence before we hand a student a communication book. We don't demand RCT-level proof before we accept that a student pointing to a symbol is communicating.

The scrutiny is not being applied evenly. And it is landing hardest on the communicators who were already the hardest to believe.

---

Here's the piece I don't hear enough: if we have concerns about partner-supported spelling, the answer is not to dismiss it. The answer is to build the infrastructure to do it right. And right now, we have almost none of that.

Think about what we require of an ASL interpreter. Years of training. Certification. Ongoing professional development. Multiple qualified interpreters for high-stakes situations — legal proceedings, medical appointments, IEP meetings. We understand that interpretation is a skilled practice, that a bad interpreter doesn't just fail to communicate but actively distorts the message, and that the relationship between the communicator and the interpreter carries risk that training and standards help manage.

Now think about what we require of a facilitated spelling partner. In most cases: nothing. We hand it to whoever is available and hope for the best. One para. One parent. One person who becomes the single point of access to a student's voice — with no training pathway, no credentialing, no accountability structure.

And then when it goes wrong, we blame the method.

What if the problem isn't facilitated spelling? What if the problem is that we haven't built what facilitated spelling actually needs to work — trained partners, clear standards, multiple people across a student's team who can facilitate, so that no child's voice lives or dies on one relationship?

A student whose only communication partner is an untrained para who calls in sick on Thursdays doesn't have a communication system. They have a single point of failure. That's not a facilitated spelling problem. That's a we-never-took-this-seriously problem.

---

The question I keep coming back to is this: what would it take to believe these students?

Not to stop asking questions. Not to abandon rigor. But to start from belief — the same belief we extend to my daughter when she uses her Tobii, the same belief we extend to a student who points to a symbol on a board — and build our questions from there instead of our doubts.

Because the alternative is what my daughter already lives. It's what Melody lives on that screen. A world where your communication is always one skeptical adult away from being erased.

The voice is there. The question is whether we've decided to hear it.
