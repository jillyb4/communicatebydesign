Two studies came out this spring. Same app. Same population. Two different journals, published weeks apart.

Both found the same thing: parents delivered structured literacy instruction to their minimally speaking autistic children, and it worked. Large to very large effect sizes. Ten weeks. Skills generalized to words they had never practiced.

The research didn't say "wait for the SLP to have a slot available." It said: give the family a plan, train them virtually, and get out of the way.

---

## What the research actually found

Jessica Caron's team published in *Folia Phoniatrica et Logopaedica* in April 2026. Salena Babb and Caron published in the *American Journal of Speech-Language Pathology* in March. Both studies used the Accessible Literacy Learning (ALL) app — an evidence-based tool designed specifically for children who use AAC — and both had parents deliver the intervention after virtual training.

In the AJSLP study: four children who use AAC improved in sound blending, decoding, and high-frequency word reading. Three of the four generalized those skills to untrained words — meaning they weren't just memorizing a word list. They were building a process.

In the Folia study: gains were large to very large across phonics skills. All participants showed generalization. The intervention ran ten weeks.

Parents trained virtually. Delivered instruction at home. Hit fidelity benchmarks.

The researchers' conclusion, across both papers: parent-implemented interventions can *increase* acquisition rates, *decrease* abandonment of literacy programs, and *increase* instructional time beyond what the SLP session alone could provide.

That last sentence matters. It's not just that parents *can* do this. It's that when they do, students get more. Not different. More.

---

## The mindset shift: literacy instruction has been treated like specialist territory

Here's where most teams get stuck. The assumption — usually unstated — is that structured literacy instruction for students who use AAC requires a specialist. An SLP. A certified reading interventionist. Someone with the training to navigate the complexity.

That assumption has a real cost.

When the SLP has a 60-minute weekly slot, and that's the only window where real phonics instruction happens, the student's literacy growth is capped at 60 minutes a week. Maybe less, if the session gets pulled for an IEP meeting. Maybe none, if the SLP is out sick.

The families were there the whole time. They just weren't given a plan.

The 2025 field consensus from Light, McNaughton, Holyfield, and colleagues put it plainly: students who need or use AAC can acquire a wide range of literacy skills *when provided with effective tools and evidence-based, adapted instruction.* The instruction is the variable. Not the setting. Not the person delivering it.

---

## What the research actually modeled

Here's the part that gets buried in the abstract: the families in these studies weren't trained by specialists in person over a semester. They received virtual training — a focused walkthrough of the app, the routine, what to do when a child gets stuck. Then someone checked in while they ran sessions at home.

That's it. That's the model.

The thing most IEP teams haven't been given isn't permission to involve families in literacy instruction. It's a concrete picture of what that actually looks like — what app, what routine, how long, what to do on day one. Nobody trained the team on that either. That's not a failure. It's a gap in what the field has made available.

What the Caron and Babb studies show is that once a family has a structured tool and a specific routine, they can run it. The ALL app was designed for exactly this — explicit, systematic phonics instruction adapted for students who use AAC, built for use outside a therapy room. (Trevino & Lund found the same pattern in vocabulary instruction this year: structured, explicit routines outperformed naturalistic exposure for every child in the study. The approach matters more than the setting.)

The virtual training piece is worth sitting with. Not because it's a workaround — but because it means the infrastructure for this already exists. A team doesn't need to invent a new service delivery model. They need a tool that was designed to be handed to a family, and a clear plan for what to show them.

## Bring this to your next meeting

You don't need a reading certification to have this conversation. You need to know what you're looking for.

*Screenshot this. These are the three starting points:*

**What does structured phonics practice look like for this student outside of school?**
Not an audit — an honest inventory. If the answer is "we're not sure," that's useful information. It means the team hasn't had this conversation yet, not that no one cares.

**Is there a tool designed for students who use AAC that the family could learn to use?**
The ALL app is one example — free, research-backed, built for this population. There are others. The question isn't whether the family is capable. It's whether anyone has shown them something concrete enough to try.

**Who on the team knows this routine well enough to walk a family through it?**
This might be the SLP. It might be the special educator. It might be someone who just spent an afternoon with the research and a tutorial video. The research doesn't require a credential. It requires someone willing to be the bridge.

The team already in that room knows this student. That's the right starting point.
