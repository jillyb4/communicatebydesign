# AT Consideration Is a Legal Requirement. So Why Does Every IEP Team Skip It?

Your student lives in 2026. They have access to technology that didn't exist when IDEA was written — eye gaze systems, dynamic display devices, text-to-speech on every phone, low-cost symbol apps, tools that would have seemed like science fiction in 1997.

Their IEP team is still running a 1997 compliance script.

That's not an accusation. It's a system failure. IEP teams have been handed a legal mandate — AT consideration at every single meeting — with almost no training on what it means, what counts, or what a real conversation actually requires. So they do what the form suggests: check the box and move on.

It's time to stop apologizing for that system and start using the law the way it was intended.

---

**What the law actually says**

IDEA §300.324(a)(2)(v) states that in developing each child's IEP, the IEP team shall:

> *"consider whether the child needs assistive technology devices and services."*

Every student. Every meeting. No exceptions for district size, no carve-out for students who don't already have AT, no option to skip because no one on the team specializes in it.

That sentence has been in the law for thirty years. Most IEP teams have never read it.

---

**The mindset shift: it's not about the $5,000 device**

Here's the problem. When most people hear "assistive technology," they picture a speech-generating device — something expensive, clinical, requiring a specialist to configure and a purchase order to obtain. So teams wait. They defer. They assume someone else will handle it when the right moment arrives.

IDEA defines AT as:

> *"any item, piece of equipment, or product system...that is used to increase, maintain, or improve functional capabilities of a child with a disability."*

Any item. That's a slant board. A pencil grip. A colored overlay. A visual schedule. A laminated communication board made from a file folder and velcro. A step-by-step audio prompt recorded on a basic tablet.

AT covers every functional domain where a student's disability creates a barrier — reading, writing, organization, hearing, vision, time management, and communication. It's a wheel with many spokes, not a single track. Communication tools are one spoke. A student might need support across three or four of them simultaneously, and every single one of those needs deserves a conversation.

When teams only think about the device — the SGD at the tip of the communication spoke — they miss most of what actually makes a classroom functional. We've been waiting for the $5,000 solution while the $5 solution sits right in front of us.

And here's the part that tends to land differently when teams hear it: most IEP teams are *already providing AT.* The visual schedule? AT. The slant board on the desk? AT. The laminated choice board the para brought from home? AT. The moment a team names what they're already doing, the consideration conversation has already begun.

---

**What "consideration" actually requires**

The law doesn't require the team to provide AT. It requires the team to *consider* whether the student needs it — and document what was decided.

Researchers who study why AT fails in practice consistently identify two barriers: teams that lack the right tools (access barriers) and teams that never create the conditions to use them (opportunity barriers). Skipping the consideration conversation creates both. You cannot address what you have not named.

A checked box with no conversation isn't consideration. A two-second "we don't have anyone who does AT" isn't consideration.

A documented team discussion — in any form — is.

---

**Bring this to your next meeting**

You don't need a certification to have this conversation. You need three questions and a willingness to write down what the team said.

> *Screenshot this. These are the three questions that turn AT consideration from a checkbox into a conversation.*

**1. Is there a task this student struggles to complete that a different tool, format, or support could make more accessible?**
Start with function. What does the student need to *do* — and what's in the way?

**2. Are there supports already in place — even informal ones — that are working?**
Name them. A strategy the student or team already uses is the strongest possible indicator of what formal AT might support.

**3. Is there anything we could trial before the next meeting, and how will we track what we learn?**
AT consideration isn't a closed decision. It's the beginning of an inquiry. A plan to try something and check back in is a legally sound and genuinely useful outcome.

That conversation, written into the IEP record, meets the legal standard. More importantly, it surfaces needs that have been sitting unnamed — sometimes for years.

The team already in that room knows this student. That is exactly the team the law had in mind.
