April is Poetry Month. And I keep thinking about why poetry works so well for students who use AAC — and why most literacy curricula treat it like a bonus unit instead of a foundation.

Here's the thing nobody says out loud: poetry is short. Intentionally, structurally short. Two lines can carry an entire idea. A single stanza can ask a question, sit with an image, and leave space for a response — all without requiring a student to read a chapter, track a plot, or write a paragraph before they've had a chance to think.

For a student who communicates differently, that brevity isn't a simplification. It's a design feature.

---

## What poetry actually asks of a reader

Poetry asks you to notice. To feel something. To ask what the words mean and why the poet chose them. It asks for a response — not a summary, not a retelling, not proof that you followed along from page one to page three hundred.

Notice. Feel. Mean. Ask.

Those four things don't require speech. They don't require a paragraph response. They don't require a fluency score or a timed reading or a standardized assessment that was built for a different student.

A student who selects *sad* and *quiet* from their communication system in response to a poem just did literary analysis. A student who indicates *I don't know* and then waits is demonstrating the most sophisticated reader move there is — sitting with ambiguity. A student who uses eye gaze to choose between two possible meanings of a line just did the thing AP English students are asked to do.

The expectation didn't change. The access route did.

---

## Why teachers skip it

Nobody skips poetry because they think it's worthless. They skip it because the curriculum didn't tell them what to do with a student who can't read it aloud, discuss it verbally, or write a five-paragraph response.

So poetry becomes the unit at the end of the year. The one you get to if there's time. The bonus.

That's a curriculum design problem — not a student problem and not a teacher problem. Poetry is actually the place where access is most natural — where a one-word response is a legitimate literary act, where the whole class can hold the same two lines and bring completely different meaning, where a student who communicates via AAC and a student who writes in a journal are doing the exact same cognitive work.

---

## What to try this month

Find a poem. Something short — two stanzas, maybe three. Read it together. Don't ask for a summary. Ask one question: *what do you notice?*

Let the answers come in any form. A word. A symbol. A pointed finger. A gaze. A gesture. A drawing. Write them all down in the same place, with the same weight.

That's the lesson. You don't need a unit guide to do it. You need a poem and the decision that every response counts.

If you want a place to start, the Poetry Foundation has a full library of poems — free, searchable, organized by theme and length. Start there. Pick something that surprises you and see what your students do with it.
