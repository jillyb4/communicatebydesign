# When the Framework Demands a Confession

---

Bob Sparks over at Science of Reading Classroom published a post this week walking through a complete TPCASTT routine for any grade level. It's practical, it's grounded, and it describes exactly how most of us were taught to teach poetry.

It also describes why I built a different framework for students who use AAC.

TPCASTT stands for Title, Paraphrase, Connotation, Attitude, Shift, Title (again), Theme. Every step asks the student to produce something — a paraphrase, a written identification of tone, a theme statement. That production orientation is intentional. It scaffolds literary thinking through writing.

But production is also the architecture's load-bearing wall.

---

When I started building poetry instruction for students who use augmentative and alternative communication, I tried to adapt TPCASTT. I added sentence frames. I replaced "write a paraphrase" with "select the sentence that says the same thing." I broke the steps into smaller chunks.

It didn't hold.

Not because my students couldn't engage with the thinking. Because the framework was organized around what you write, and I was asking students who communicate through symbol selection, eye gaze, and partner-assisted scanning to prove their thinking through a production model that assumed one specific output channel.

TPCASTT wasn't designed for them. You can't fix a load-bearing wall with a ramp.

---

That's the distinction that matters, and it's one I want to be careful about here: I am not saying TPCASTT is wrong. Bob's post describes a genuinely effective routine for classrooms where students can access the production steps. The research behind structured analytical frameworks for poetry is real.

I am saying that when a framework's architecture generates inaccessibility — when the steps themselves require a specific mode of output — adaptation isn't enough. You need a different structure.

This is the same argument we make about passage-only comprehension assessments, or about asking a student who uses a speech generating device to "read aloud." The problem isn't the student's ability to think. The problem is that the tool was built around an assumed output channel, and nobody said so.

---

NFMA — Notice, Feel, Mean, Ask — is the framework I built instead.

Every step is organized around observation and inquiry, not production.

**Notice:** What do you see in this poem? Identify the figurative language. This is a selection task. It requires recognition, not composition.

**Feel:** What is the effect? This is an interpretive inference, and it can be demonstrated through symbol selection, picture choice, gaze direction, or yes/no verification with a partner.

**Mean:** What does the figurative language reveal? This step is where literary analysis happens — and it can be accessed at V1 (open written response), V2 (sentence frame with word bank), or V3 (structured symbol array with partner guidance).

**Ask:** What questions does this poem raise? Genuine inquiry. A student who uses a low-tech communication board can ask a real question about a poem by Dunbar. That question is no less literary for having been indicated rather than written.

The standard doesn't change across access levels. The response channel does.

---

I want to be transparent about something: NFMA is not "TPCASTT with different letters." That would be cosmetic.

The structural difference is this: TPCASTT is organized around what you produce at each step (a paraphrase, an analysis, a theme statement). NFMA is organized around what you perceive and inquire about — and production, when it happens, follows from access-differentiated response options.

The sequence matters. An observation-first architecture can be scaffolded all the way down to a single symbol selection. A production-first architecture cannot — because you cannot scaffold your way to a paraphrase if the student doesn't have an output channel that supports paraphrasing.

---

This is also why I'm reluctant to frame NFMA as just another acronym in the pile. Every new framework in education has to justify its existence. The justification here isn't originality. It's structural necessity.

When the existing framework demands a specific mode of confession — prove you understood this poem by paraphrasing it — and a student has no access to that mode, the framework hasn't been differentiated. It's been failed.

NFMA asks a different question at every step: how would a student who communicates through any means demonstrate that they noticed this? That they felt this? That they can ask about this?

That's not a renamed acronym. That's a different starting place.

---

Bob's post is worth reading — the TPCASTT routine he describes is clean and teachable, and his framing of poetry as a "routine for ANY grade level" is exactly the mindset this field needs. I'm adding it to the CbD peer publications list.

The students I build for need the same thing: a routine for any grade level. This is just a different architecture for how you get there.
