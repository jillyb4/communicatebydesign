## 1. The Weeklyish (Tim Villegas) — "Defending Successes"
🔗 https://weeklyish.substack.com/p/defending-successes

**Topic:** Sevi, an included student, has to constantly "defend" his successes — achievements are questioned or treated as flukes rather than celebrated as foundation for the next opportunity.

**Comment:**
> This piece hit me hard. What you're describing with Sevi is something I see play out in IEP meetings constantly — and it's especially stark for students who use AAC. The moment a nonspeaking student communicates something complex or unexpected, the room gets quiet in the wrong way. Instead of "wow, let's build on that," it becomes "are we sure that was intentional?" The success has to be explained away before it can be celebrated.
>
> I think it comes from a deep discomfort with updating our assumptions about a student's capability. If we accept the success, we have to accept that we underestimated them — and that has implications. It's easier to call it a fluke.
>

**Topic:** Tim visits Inclusionary Practices Project (IPP) demonstration sites in Washington State — examining what real inclusion looks like in practice.

**Comment:**
> What you're describing in Washington is what I think about when people ask what "real inclusion" looks like vs. just inclusive placement. It's the difference between putting a student in a general ed classroom and actually building the team's capacity to support them there.
>
> In my experience, AT and AAC are often the missing piece in that gap. A student can be physically present in an inclusive setting and still be completely excluded from communication if the team doesn't have the knowledge or tools to support their device. Placement gets a student in the room. Capacity-building keeps them there as a full participant.
>
> Curious whether any of the IPP sites had AT or AAC-specific support built into their model — that would be worth a follow-up piece.

## 3. Simplifying Special Ed Law (Ashley Nyce) — "IEP Development"
🔗 https://ashleynyce.substack.com/p/iep-development

**Topic:** How the IEP is developed after eligibility — what goes in it, team obligations, timelines under IDEA.

**Comment:**
> Great overview of the IEP process. One thing I'd add for your readers that often gets missed: IDEA requires that the IEP team *consider* assistive technology for every student with a disability — not just students who are already using devices. It's buried in the considerations section and most teams either skip it or treat it as a checkbox.
>
> In practice, "consideration" should mean the team actually discusses whether AT could support access, participation, or communication. It doesn't mean every student needs a device — but it does mean no student should graduate from an IEP meeting without the question being genuinely asked.
>
> That one legal requirement, if actually implemented, would change outcomes for a lot of kids. I write about AT and AAC in educational settings at communicatebydesign.substack.com.
