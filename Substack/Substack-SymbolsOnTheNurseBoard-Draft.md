# Draft: The Symbols on the Door

---

**She told me which medication she needed. She didn't use her device.**

It's a no-school Friday. The alarm didn't go off at 6. I'm up at 8, already behind — timed meds, feeding pump, full assist to get her into her wheelchair.

Once she's settled, she starts looking behind her. Not upset. Not distressed. *Directing.* Something is back in her room she hasn't told me about yet.

I ask if she forgot something. She nods yes.

I turn her chair so she can use her eyes. She gazes up — above the whiteboard in the nurses' area, where her medication symbols live.

*Do you need medication?* Yes. I go through the list. She nods for the one she means.

Complete. Accurate. COMMUNICATION.

---

This didn't happen by accident.

It started before a surgery — not after. My daughter was on her second SGD at the time — she's on her third now — and we knew that using it during recovery might not be realistic. Not just access to the device, but whether she'd have the stamina or physical capacity to eye gaze on a full system while recovering. An e-trans board asks less of her. So we built one: low-tech, eye gaze, laminated. PRN medications she might need to request. Symptoms. What she was feeling. Not everything — the things that mattered most in that window.

That surgery was two years ago. Those symbols are still there, right where her eyes can find them from her wheelchair. The placement isn't random — everything on that board is intentional. As she recovered, those same concepts got programmed into her device too — both places, every day. We kept both. Because recovery isn't linear, and neither is access.

---

Here's the teaching piece.

Research on communication frequency consistently shows that a small set of words — around 200 to 400 — accounts for roughly 80% of what most people communicate (Beukelman et al., 1989). Those are core words. High-frequency. Cross-context. The foundation of every lesson. We teach them explicitly, every time.

But we don't skip fringe. We can't.

Core gets you the category. Fringe gets you the precision — and it's where the student gets to decide what's actually important to *them*. "Medication" tells me she needs something. "Zofran" tells me exactly what. Those symbols on that board are fringe vocabulary: specific, context-dependent, built around her life.

You have to teach it. That's what I can't say enough. It doesn't transfer on its own.

Before the surgery, we taught the vocabulary. During recovery, we modeled it — pointing to symbols, naming them, pairing them with every request. She used it throughout. What started on a low-tech e-trans board became a verbal list she could answer by nodding. Then it went into her device. Two years later, she still uses both.

On harder days — when the pain is visible before she says a word — I come back to the modeling. *Do you need medication? It looks like you're hurting. Tylenol?* I walk through it with her. She nods for the one she means.

And then some days it looks like this morning. No prompting. No board in her hands. Just a gaze, a yes, and a clear request from someone who knows exactly what she needs.

---

An AAC system isn't complete when the device is programmed. It's complete when the student can communicate across their whole day — with the device, and without it. Low-tech and high-tech in parallel, not in competition.

Those symbols on the door aren't backup. They're part of the plan.

Build it before you need it. Not after the surgery, not when the device fails. Before.

I was so excited I spent the rest of the day writing this.
