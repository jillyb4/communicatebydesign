## Who Jill is and what CbD does

Jill McCardel is a special education teacher and AT/AAC advocate. She runs **Communicate by Design (CbD)**, a TPT store and Substack focused on materials for complex communicators in inclusive and resource settings. Her tagline is *Where AT Meets Practice*.

Her students are in **resource rooms and co-taught classrooms, grades 6–10**. Many use AAC (speech-generating devices, PECS, communication books, gaze boards, yes/no systems). Her units need to work for all of them — not by lowering the bar, but by changing the scaffold.

Her primary writing framework in the classroom is **Step Up to Writing (SUW)**, but the units are designed to be **framework-agnostic** — they teach students to gather evidence while reading and organize it into an argument. By the time a student reaches the writing task, the essay is already outlined. The unit doesn't teach paragraph structure — it builds the evidence base that makes any paragraph structure work.

## Substack strategy

**Publication:** communicatebydesign.substack.com
**Tagline:** Where AT Meets Practice

The Substack is **not a product launch channel.** It is CbD's voice at the intersection of three equal pillars:

### Pillar 1: Policy & Advocacy
What's broken in the system, what's changing, and what teachers and parents need to know. IDEA enforcement gaps, AT consideration failures, AAC funding fights, state-level literacy mandates that leave complex communicators behind. This is the "why it matters" pillar — it gives readers a reason to care and a framework for understanding the other two pillars.

### Pillar 2: Instructional Practice
How to actually teach reading, writing, and critical thinking to students with complex needs. Evidence-based practices, HLPs in action, what the research says vs. what classrooms look like. Close reading, annotation, CER, SRSD, environmental mode — the instructional science that drives CbD units. This pillar speaks to both sped teachers building lessons and parents asking "what should good instruction look like for my kid?"

### Pillar 3: AT & AAC

## The nonfiction unit design philosophy

### The core insight

Every reading activity in the unit is a **pre-writing scaffold**. The activities students complete while reading — whether annotation codes, graphic organizers, evidence sorts, or source evaluations — are not busywork. They are building the final product piece by piece. Students who complete the reading activities well are, without knowing it, outlining their argument as they read.

When the unit uses an annotation lens (3-code system), each code maps directly to one body paragraph of the final essay. But annotation is not the only approach — the right skill-building activity depends on the Target Literacy Skill. What stays constant: the reading activities always scaffold toward the final product.

This is why the unit question has to be decided **before** writing a single word of the passage. Everything — the skill activities, the comprehension responses, the Evidence Recording Sheet, the final prompts — points back to that question.

### The reading-to-writing pipeline

```
Topic → Skill → Unit question → skill-building activities → passage → comprehension responses → evidence collection → final product
```

Each stage feeds the next. By the final lessons, the student is not writing from scratch. They are organizing what they already found. This is the research-backed principle from Hillocks' "environmental mode" — students who gather and analyze evidence before writing produce significantly better arguments than students given writing instruction alone.

### Differentiation: change the scaffold, not the standard

All three versions use the same unit question, the same skill-building activities (with different scaffolding levels), the same comprehension response topics (at different difficulty), and the same final product prompts.

What changes:
- **Version 1 / A (highest Lexile):** Full academic prose, challenging MCQ, extended Short Answer
- **Version 2 / B (mid Lexile):** Simplified language, same story and analytical framework
- **Version 3 / C (lowest Lexile):** Short sentences, in-text vocabulary boxes, yes/no and highlighter response options, simplified MCQ

Version labels are numbers or letters only — no reading-level language appears in student materials. Keiko uses A/B/C. Radium Girls uses 1/2/3 with Lexile ranges. **Lexile information is NEVER printed on student-facing pages.** Students should not know multiple versions exist unless the teacher chooses to tell them. Version label appears only as a small, discreet identifier in the page header (for teacher sorting purposes).

### Print-friendly design rule
All student handouts must be print-friendly. Avoid large solid-color fills (heavy ink). Use thin colored borders/lines for branding. Navy header backgrounds are acceptable for teacher reference tables but should be minimized on student handouts. Each standalone student handout (Word Bank, Sentence Frames, Annotation Guide, CER Strategy Card, Tools That Help Me Learn, Evidence Recording Sheet, etc.) starts on its own page.

### Grade range
CbD nonfiction reading units target grades 6–10. The three-version Lexile system accommodates the full range. Future topics should include a grade range recommendation based on the source material and content complexity (see Step 0d in the nonfiction unit architecture).

### AAC is not an add-on

Every unit includes:
- **Core word lists** (high-frequency words present on most AAC systems) — for annotation, discussion, and argument
- **Fringe word lists** (unit-specific vocabulary that needs to be pre-programmed) — coordinate with SLP/AAC team before the unit begins
- **Participation guidance** for annotation (pointing, eye gaze, yes/no confirmation, alternative pencil), Short Answer (any communication system counts — written output not required), and discussion (pre-loaded vocabulary, partner scribing)
- The lowest-barrier AAC tool is always on the table: an index card with the word and a drawing, velcro backing, mounted on a response board. No device required.

**Passage structure rationale (4 parts, decided March 16, 2026):**
4 parts. CER requires evaluative re-reading — students aren't just categorizing, they're judging whether claims hold up under evidence. That's more cognitively demanding per passage than close reading annotation (F/R/C). Five parts would push total V1 word count past 2,800, exceeding single-week reading capacity for the target population. The 4-part arc (introduction → escalation → revelation → evaluation) gives students 4 distinct claim-evaluation contexts with increasing independence. V3 lengths stay under 450 words, appropriate for Lexile 400–550 readers who need cognitive room for the evaluation task. Radium Girls uses 5 parts for close reading (simpler cognitive demand per passage); 4 parts here is a defensible reduction.

**Instructional design for Skill #5 — Claim, Evidence, Reasoning (research completed March 16, 2026):**

The core shift from Keiko/Radium Girls: those units asked students to *categorize* ("Is this F, R, or C?"). That's close reading — the analytical work is deciding which bucket something goes in. For CER, the annotation codes need to ask students to *evaluate* ("Is this claim supported? Is this evidence strong or weak? Does the reasoning connect them?"). That's a fundamentally different cognitive demand requiring different instructional moves.

**HLPs driving this unit:**

- **HLP 16 (Explicit Instruction) — pillar practice.** CER cannot be taught through discovery. Students with LD need the components modeled explicitly. The Modeling Session must demonstrate *how to evaluate a claim* — not just how to annotate. Think-aloud models the process: "What's the claim here? What evidence supports it? Does the reasoning actually connect them?" SRSD research (Harris & Graham) shows that when teachers model expert thinking aloud, every student gains access to expert reasoning. Effect sizes for SRSD are 3x those of traditional writing instruction.

- **HLP 14 (Teach Cognitive and Metacognitive Strategies).** Students need a repeatable internal process for evaluating claims, not just the ability to label them. The SRSD framework's TREE mnemonic (Topic sentence → Reasons → Explain → Ending) is the gold standard for persuasive writing with LD students. For Frances Kelsey, adapt into a CER-specific routine students internalize: "Find the claim → Ask what evidence supports it → Ask whether the reasoning connects them → Judge if it holds up." This routine should be posted in the classroom and referenced explicitly during annotation.

- **HLP 15 (Scaffolded Supports).** Temporary assistance, carefully calibrated, gradually removed. Three versions provide calibrated access, but for CER the scaffolds need to target the *evaluation* specifically. Version 3 might give students the claim pre-identified and ask them only to find evidence and judge it. Version 1 asks students to identify all three components independently. The scaffold is removed progressively across parts — by Part 4, even Version 3 students are identifying claims with less support.

- **HLP 12 (Systematically Design Instruction Toward a Specific Learning Goal).** Every lesson builds toward the goal of independent claim evaluation. Lesson 1: teacher models CER on parallel text. Lesson 2: guided practice with heavy scaffolding. Lessons 3–4: increasing independence. Lesson 5: students evaluate claims independently using their annotations as evidence base.

**EBPs for CER instruction with LD students:**

- **CER framework (McNeill & Krajcik).** Originally from science education but widely adapted for ELA. Breaks argumentation into three manageable components. The formulaic structure is itself a scaffold — students who struggle to organize ideas can focus on content because the structure is given. Graphic organizers for CER are well-established; CbD's innovation is embedding the CER structure into the annotation codes themselves so the organizer IS the reading activity.

- **Explicit instruction in argument structure (Archer & Hughes, 2011).** Students with LD benefit from argument components being taught one at a time, in sequence, with modeling and guided practice before independent work. Lesson 1 teaches "What is a claim?" only. Lesson 2 adds "What counts as evidence?" Lesson 3 adds "Does the evidence support the claim?" The full CER evaluation is not expected until students have practiced each component separately.

- **SRSD for persuasive/argument writing (Harris & Graham, 2006).** Effect sizes for SRSD exceed 0.85 with LD students. The six SRSD stages (Develop Background Knowledge → Discuss It → Model It → Memorize It → Support It → Independent Performance) map directly to the CbD lesson sequence. The annotation framework handles stages 1–3; the writing pipeline handles stages 4–6.

- **Structured evidence gathering before writing (Hillocks, 1986).** Environmental mode applies here as it did for close reading — but the evidence students gather is different. Instead of categorizing evidence by topic (F/R/C), they're evaluating evidence by quality and relevance to claims. The annotation codes must prompt this evaluation, not just categorization.

**How the annotation lens changes from close reading to CER:**

| Aspect | Close reading (Keiko/Radium Girls) | CER (Frances Kelsey) |
|---|---|---|
| What students mark | Textual evidence that fits a category | Claims, evidence that supports/undermines claims, and the reasoning connecting them |
| The cognitive demand | Categorize: "Which bucket does this go in?" | Evaluate: "Is this claim supported? Is this evidence strong?" |
| The annotation decision | "Is this H, Ha, or D?" / "Is this F, R, or C?" | "Is this a C (claim), E (evidence), or R (reasoning)?" AND "Does the E actually support the C?" |
| What makes it harder | Multiple valid answers (ambiguity) | Requires evaluating the *relationship* between codes, not just assigning them |
| Modeling Session focus | How to decide which code to assign | How to evaluate whether evidence supports a claim |
| MCQ design | "What happened?" (comprehension) | "Is this claim well-supported?" (evaluation) |
| Short Answer design | "Explain what you marked and why" | "Evaluate this claim using evidence from the text" |

**Modeling Session design (Frances Kelsey):**

Uses a parallel text (NOT the Kelsey text). Must model the *evaluation* process, not just labeling. Script framework:
- "I see someone making a claim here — they're saying this is true. I'll mark it C."
- "Now I need to ask: what evidence do they give? Is there data? A study? A quote? I'll mark what I find as E."
- "Now the hard part: does the evidence actually support the claim? What's the reasoning connecting them? I'll mark that R."
- "Notice — this claim has evidence, but the evidence doesn't actually prove the claim. That gap matters. That's what we're looking for."

Key teaching move: the gap between claim and evidence is where the analytical work lives. Students must learn to see it.

**MCQ design shift:**

Keiko/Radium Girls MCQs test comprehension: "What happened? What does this detail mean?"
Frances Kelsey MCQs test evaluation: "Is this claim well-supported based on the text?"

Example:
*"The drug company stated that thalidomide had been tested thoroughly. Based on the text, which of the following best evaluates that claim?"*
A. The claim is supported because the company submitted paperwork to the FDA
B. The claim is unsupported because Kelsey found the clinical trials did not test long-term effects
C. The claim is irrelevant because the drug was never sold in the United States
D. The claim is supported because doctors in Europe prescribed the drug widely

This question teaches RI.x.8 directly — students must evaluate whether a claim is supported by sufficient evidence.

**Short Answer design shift:**

Keiko/Radium Girls Short Answers: "Explain what you marked and why." Pre-writing evidence collection.
Frances Kelsey Short Answers: "Evaluate this claim using evidence from the text." Evidence collection AND evaluation practice.

Version 3 adaptation: "The company said the drug was safe. Is this true? Yes / No / Not sure. Circle the evidence: *the company's tests* / *Kelsey's findings* / *what happened in Europe*. The company's claim was: *strong* / *weak* / *missing evidence*."

| Vocabulary demands | Tier 2 words are general academic | Mix of general + domain-specific | Heavy domain-specific or discipline vocabulary |
| Analytical demand | Skill can be taught at introductory level | Skill scaffolds across the full range | Skill requires prior analytical experience |

Present a recommendation: "Based on the source material and content, I recommend grades [X–Y] for this product. Here's why: [reasoning]." The default is 6–10 unless there's a specific reason to narrow.

### 1. Lock the unit question
The question must: have no easy answer, be answerable with evidence from the text, connect to something students can have a genuine opinion about, and **require students to practice the selected Target Literacy Skill to answer it.** The question drives everything downstream — the activities, the scaffolds, and the final product all point back to this question.

🛑 **Checkpoint 1 — Present the question to the user.** Confirm it meets all criteria plus the skill alignment. Don't proceed until approved.

### 2. Design the skill-building activities and scaffold sequence

**This is where the instructional design happens.** The goal: every activity across the unit builds the Target Literacy Skill and scaffolds toward the final product. By the time students reach the final product, they've already practiced every component of it through the reading activities.

**An annotation lens (3-code system) is ONE possible activity structure — not the default for every skill.** Some skills (close reading, CER, sourcing/corroboration) naturally lend themselves to annotation codes. Others (summarizing, text structure, vocabulary in context, Socratic seminar) may use different activity structures entirely. Design the activities that actually teach the skill.

Questions to answer at this step:
- **What do students DO while reading to practice the skill?** (annotate with codes? complete a graphic organizer? identify structural elements? paraphrase sections? evaluate sources?) The reading activity IS the skill practice.
- **What comprehension responses demonstrate skill development after each passage?** Consult the Comprehension Response Formats section below. Every passage gets exactly 2 responses: one skill-focused, one comprehension-focused. Choose formats that build toward the final product.
- **How do the activities scaffold across parts?** Part 1 should be heavily supported (teacher models, scaffolds provided). Each subsequent part removes support. By the final part, students should be performing the skill with minimal scaffolding. This progressive release is the unit's instructional arc.
- **How does each activity feed the final product?** Every reading activity, every comprehension response, and every scaffold should produce something the student can use in their final product. If an activity doesn't connect to the final product, cut it.

If using an annotation lens: Three codes. Each code must require analytical thinking (not just recognition), be distinct enough that a struggling reader can feel the difference, and connect to the final product structure.

If NOT using an annotation lens: Define the alternative reading activity structure and explain how it teaches the skill and scaffolds toward the final product.

🛑 **Checkpoint 2 — Present the full activity design.** Show: (a) what students do while reading, (b) the comprehension response formats for each part, (c) how scaffolding decreases across parts, and (d) how each activity feeds the final product. If using annotation codes, explain how they connect to the skill standard. Get approval before proceeding.

### 3. Determine passage structure
Ask: what is the minimum amount of original source material needed? Does the story require multiple moments across time? Do not default to 4 parts — earn each one.

🛑 **Checkpoint 3 — Present the passage plan to the user and get explicit agreement before writing.** The AI must answer ALL of the following and present them for review:
- **How many parts?** (1–5) and why — what does each part contribute to the arc that can't be cut?
- **Target word count per part for V1?** Consider: cognitive demand of the ELA skill (evaluative skills like CER need shorter passages than categorization skills like close reading), single-period feasibility (reading + skill activity + comprehension responses must fit in 45–60 min), and total V1 word count across all parts (target under 3,000 for a single-week unit).
- **Target word count per part for V3?** Consider: Lexile 400–550 readers need cognitive room for the analytical task, not just decoding. V3 passages should generally stay under 450 words per part.
- **Does the part count match the pacing?** 4 parts = Lessons 2–4 cover reading. 5 parts = Lessons 2–5 cover reading, leaving only 1 core lesson for writing. Check the math.
- **What is each part's narrative role?** Name it (e.g., "introduction," "escalation," "revelation," "evaluation") and confirm the arc earns every part.

Do not write any passage text until the user confirms the passage plan.

### 4. Write passages at all three levels
Version A/1 first (full complexity), then adapt to B/2 (simplified), then C/3 (short sentences + vocab boxes + yes/no). Each part gets passage + comprehension responses (skill-focused + comprehension-focused) at all three levels.

### 5. Build teacher documents
CCSS Standards Alignment, Unit at a Glance / Pacing Guide, Vocabulary Reference + Preview Routine, Teacher Support Reference, Instructional Design Foundations, Differentiating for All Learners (MLL/AAC/IEP), Modeling Session (model the skill activity, not just annotation), Teacher Checkpoint + Record, Supplemental Resources + References.

### 6. Build student materials
Skill-specific reference materials (Annotation Guide if using annotation codes, or equivalent reference for the skill activity), Word Bank (half-page desk reference), Sentence Frames (half-page desk reference), "Tools That Help Me Learn" AT reference page, Unit Prompt + Self-Assessment + Rubric, Research Choice Board, Evidence Recording Sheet or equivalent skill organizer.

### 6b. Inclusive Design Diagnostic — run AFTER all materials are built

**This step evaluates the complete product against inclusive design standards before final assembly.** Run this diagnostic against the full set of built materials — passages, comprehension activities, teacher documents, and student handouts. The diagnostic can only be meaningful when it has actual content to evaluate, not just a design plan.

**Run the following evaluation prompt against the complete draft:**

> You are an expert Curriculum Specialist and Inclusive Instructional Designer. Your expertise is grounded in the Science of Reading, Universal Design for Learning (UDL), and the SETT Framework (Student, Environment, Tasks, Tools).
>
> Task: Perform a high-level diagnostic evaluation of this completed ELA unit/lesson. Your analysis must determine the product's effectiveness in supporting all learners, including students with complex communication needs (CCN) who utilize Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) and students with specific learning disabilities.
>
> **Evaluation Frameworks:**
>
> **1. CCSS Rigor & Depth of Knowledge (DOK):**
> - Identify specific Grade-Level ELA Standards addressed.
> - Rigor Check: Analyze if the tasks reach DOK Level 3 (Strategic Thinking) or 4 (Extended Thinking). If the unit focuses solely on recall, suggest ways to pivot to "sensemaking" and "disciplinary literacy" (e.g., reading like a historian or scientist).
>
> **2. High-Leverage Practices (HLPs) & Explicit Instruction:**
> - HLP 16 (Explicit Instruction): Evaluate the "I Do, We Do, You Do" sequence. Is there a clear model of metacognitive "self-talk"?
> - HLP 11 (Goal Setting): Are goals specific, measurable, and aligned with grade-level content?
> - HLP 15 (Scaffolded Supports): Identify the presence of "temporary scaffolds" (graphic organizers, sentence frames, or word matrices) and provide a plan for their systematic fading to foster independence.
>
> **3. UDL Guidelines 3.0 (Joy & Agency):**
> - Engagement: Does the unit provide "low floor/high ceiling" tasks that allow for "productive struggle"?
> - Representation (The "How"): Check for accessibility — is the text compatible with screen readers or text-to-speech? Is there a "picture walk" or background-building phase?
> - Action & Expression: Evaluate options for students with CCN. Can they demonstrate mastery through an AAC device, a digital interactive notebook, or an "alternative pencil"?
>
> **4. Evidence-Based Literacy & Vocabulary:**
> - Morphology & Tier 2: Is there explicit morphology instruction (prefixes, suffixes, bases) using a word matrix?
> - Text-Dependency: Evaluate if comprehension questions require deep evidence from the text or rely on outside personal experience.
> - Shared Reading/Writing: For students with extensive support needs, does the unit incorporate predictable chart writing or interactive shared reading routines (CAR/CROWD)?
>
> **Output Format:**
> - **Executive Summary:** A concise synthesis of the product's pedagogical strengths and its current level of "inclusive readiness."
> - **Gap Analysis:** A targeted list of missing elements specifically regarding AAC integration, morphology instruction, and systematic prompt fading.
> - **Quick Wins (3–5 Actionable Suggestions):** High-impact, evidence-based modifications (e.g., "Incorporate a Tier 2 Word Map" or "Add a Graphic Organizer for Critical Lenses").
> - **The "SETT" Perspective:** A brief concluding thought on what Tools or Environmental changes would further reduce barriers to the identified Tasks.

**How to use the diagnostic output:**
- **Quick Wins** → implement immediately in the relevant teacher docs or student materials before assembly.
- **Gap Analysis** → fix any missing elements (morphology, prompt fading documentation, shared reading protocol, AAC vocabulary lists) in the built materials.
- **SETT perspective** → verify the "Tools That Help Me Learn" handout and AAC/AT teacher reference sections address the identified tool and environment needs.
- **DOK check** → if comprehension questions are flagged as recall-only, revise them to require text-based evaluation or synthesis before assembly.

🛑 **Checkpoint 6b — Present the diagnostic results to Jill.** Flag any gaps that require material revisions. Get approval on which modifications to incorporate before final assembly. Do not proceed to assembly until the diagnostic is clean or Jill has approved the remaining gaps as acceptable.

Full assembly order, QC checklist (same question across versions, consistent skill activities, no ability-sorting language, AAC fringe list present, print-safe, Arial bold only), then TPT listing prep.

## Evidence-based foundations

This design is grounded in research across reading comprehension, strategy instruction, special education, and AAC:

1. **Students must interact with the text — not just read it.** The core principle of every CbD unit is that students are doing something purposeful with the text while they read. This interaction is what builds the skill and scaffolds the final product. Interaction can take many forms: annotation with analytical codes, graphic organizers, text coding with colors/symbols, summarizing sections, self-questioning, partner discussion with assigned roles, evidence sorting, source evaluation, or prediction/confirmation charts. The right form depends on the Target Literacy Skill, not habit or convenience.

2. **The right strategy combination matters more than the number of strategies.** Peng et al. (2024) found in a Bayesian network meta-analysis of 52 studies that main idea + text structure + retelling was the most effective combination for struggling readers (grades 3–12). Adding more strategies did not improve outcomes — the *fit* between strategies and the skill matters, not volume.

3. **Strategy instruction with LD students produces large effects — when the strategy demands thinking.** Highlighting alone has low utility (Dunlosky et al., 2013). But focused strategy instruction (annotation with analytical codes, self-monitoring, structured summarizing) produces effect sizes exceeding 0.85 for LD students (Swanson & Hoskyn, 1998). The key: the interaction must require elaborative processing, not just marking.

4. **The reading-to-writing pipeline is what produces good arguments.** Hillocks' "environmental mode" — structured evidence gathering before writing — produces effect sizes ~4x larger than direct writing instruction alone. Every text interaction activity in a CbD unit should produce something the student uses in the final product.

5. **Scaffolds must be temporary, calibrated, and gradually removed (HLP 15).** Different students need different interaction modes — and the same student needs less scaffolding as the unit progresses. Part 1 might provide the graphic organizer pre-filled; Part 4 gives a blank one. Version 3 might provide claims pre-identified; Version 1 asks students to find them. The scaffold changes; the skill target doesn't.

6. **Single-track instruction with variable scaffolding is more effective than separate curricula.** Students with LD in inclusive settings consistently outperform peers in pullout programs (Rea, McLaughlin, Walther-Thomas, 2002). All three versions work toward the same question with the same skill — only the support varies.

7. **AAC users interact with text through their communication system.** Presume competence, provide access, measure the same outcomes (Erickson, Koppenhaver). Text interaction for AAC users includes: pointing to responses on a choice board, using core words to answer comprehension questions, selecting evidence from pre-written options, partner scribing during discussion, eye gaze to indicate agreement/disagreement with a claim, and guided reading with set purpose. The interaction mode changes; the analytical demand does not.

8. **SRSD is the gold standard for argument writing with LD.** The CbD model builds self-regulation into the reading-to-writing pipeline itself (Harris & Graham). The text interaction activities ARE the self-regulation — students who complete them have already gathered, organized, and begun evaluating their evidence before the writing task begins.

## Text interaction across reader populations

**This section exists because "interact with the text" means different things for different readers.** When designing a new unit, the AI must consider how each population will physically and cognitively engage with the skill-building activities — not just what the activities are, but how students with different access needs will do them.

**Conventional readers (V1/V2):** Can write in margins, underline, highlight, fill in graphic organizers with pencil, write short answers. Text interaction looks like what most teachers picture: pen-to-paper engagement with the text. The challenge is making the interaction analytical, not mechanical.

**Emerging/developing readers (V3, Lexile 400–550):** Can interact with text but need reduced cognitive load on decoding so they can spend it on the skill. Text interaction may include: circling pre-identified elements, selecting from word banks, yes/no responses to analytical questions, highlighting with a specific color for a specific purpose, matching exercises (draw lines between claims and evidence), and choosing from pre-written response options. The interaction is just as analytical — the response format is simplified.

**AAC users (across all versions):** Interact with text through their communication system. Text interaction includes: pointing to a response on a choice board, using core vocabulary to answer ("I think... because..."), selecting evidence cards and placing them in categories, eye gaze to indicate which text segment is relevant, partner scribing (student directs, partner writes), using pre-programmed fringe vocabulary to discuss the text, and yes/no + follow-up ("Is this claim supported? Yes/No. Point to the evidence."). Every text interaction activity in a CbD unit must have an AAC access pathway that preserves the analytical demand.

**Students with motor access needs:** May use alternative pencils (stamps, stickers, eye gaze letter boards, switch scanning) for any written interaction. Text interaction may be oral with partner scribing, or use adapted response formats (larger checkboxes, digital annotation tools, pointing systems). The key: the student is making the analytical decision, even if someone else is recording it.

**Multilingual learners (MLLs):** May need bilingual glossaries, sentence frames in both languages, partner pairing with bilingual peer, and pre-taught vocabulary before text interaction begins. The interaction itself follows the same skill-building structure — the language support wraps around it.

## Comprehension response formats

### The six formats

Every passage section pairs exactly TWO comprehension responses — one that targets the unit's **Target Literacy Skill** and one that targets **general reading comprehension**. No passage gets more than two. This keeps cognitive load manageable for students with LD and prevents assessment fatigue.

| Format | What students do | Type | AAC access | Pipeline output |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **MCQ** | Select best answer from options | Reading comprehension | Point to choice | Checks understanding before deeper work |
| **Short Answer (SA)** | Written response with evidence | Skill or comprehension | Partner scribes; AAC response accepted | Pre-writing evidence for final product |
| **Evidence Sort (ES)** | Sort pre-written evidence statements into categories | Target Literacy Skill | Point to category; partner places card | Sorted evidence → Evidence Recording Sheet |
| **Evidence Strength Rating (ESR)** | Rate evidence as Strong / Medium / Weak with justification | Target Literacy Skill | Point to rating; circle/frame for justification | Evaluative judgment → reasoning paragraphs |
| **Claim-Evidence Match (CEM)** | Draw lines connecting claims to supporting evidence | Target Literacy Skill | Number pairs; point to matches | Mapped claim-evidence pairs → argument outline |
| **Quote Selection + Justification (QSJ)** | Choose the most important quote, explain why | Reading comprehension or skill | Point to quote; frame for justification | Key evidence identified → cited in final product |
| **Two-Column Organizer (TCO)** | Place evidence into Supports vs. Weakens columns | Target Literacy Skill | Point to column; binary yes/no decision | Pro/con structure → body paragraphs |
| **Perspective Comparison (PC)** | Side-by-side chart of what two sides claim + their evidence | Target Literacy Skill | Point to each cell; partner scribes | Claim + counterclaim → argument + rebuttal |

### Which formats pair with which Target Literacy Skills

Each skill has two BEST-FIT formats marked ★. These are the primary options. Others marked ● also work. When building a unit, pick from the ★ formats first.

| # | Skill | ★ Best-fit skill format | ★ Best-fit comprehension format | ● Also works |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Close reading / annotation | ★ Quote Selection + Justification | ★ MCQ | ● SA |
| 2 | Summarizing / paraphrasing | ★ Two-Column Organizer | ★ SA | ● MCQ |
| 3 | Text structure analysis | ★ Evidence Sort (by structure type) | ★ MCQ | ● Perspective Comparison |
| 4 | Author's purpose / perspective | ★ Perspective Comparison | ★ MCQ | ● Quote Selection |
| 5 | Claim, evidence, reasoning | ★ Evidence Sort or Evidence Strength Rating | ★ MCQ | ● Claim-Evidence Match, SA |
| 6 | Sourcing / corroboration | ★ Evidence Strength Rating | ★ Claim-Evidence Match | ● MCQ |
| 7 | Making inferences | ★ Quote Selection + Justification | ★ MCQ | ● SA |
| 8 | Vocabulary in context | ★ Quote Selection + Justification | ★ MCQ | ● SA |
| 9 | Narrative structure in NF | ★ Two-Column Organizer | ★ MCQ | ● Evidence Sort |
| 10 | Synthesis across texts | ★ Perspective Comparison | ★ Claim-Evidence Match | ● Evidence Sort |
| 11 | Socratic seminar | ★ Perspective Comparison | ★ Quote Selection | ● SA |

### The 2-per-passage rule and rotation

Every passage part gets exactly 2 response formats: 1 skill-focused + 1 comprehension-focused. Rotate formats across parts so students encounter variety, not repetition. Example rotation for a 4-part CER unit (Skill #5):

| Part | Skill format (teaches the TLS) | Comprehension format (checks understanding) |
|---|---|---|
| Part 1 | Evidence Sort | MCQ |
| Part 2 | Evidence Strength Rating | MCQ |
| Part 3 | Claim-Evidence Match | SA |
| Part 4 | Evidence Sort | SA |

This rotation ensures students practice three different skill formats across the unit while always having one comprehension check. The skill format output feeds directly into the Evidence Recording Sheet and final product.

### Version differentiation across formats

All six formats differentiate the same way as MCQ and SA:

**Evidence Sort:** V1 gets 8–10 statements including ambiguous evidence. V2 gets 6–8 with clearer signals. V3 gets 4–5 with obvious category membership and bolded key words.

**Evidence Strength Rating:** V1 rates evidence independently with written justification. V2 gets sentence frames for the justification. V3 gets pre-pulled evidence with a circle format: Strong / Medium / Weak + "because ______" frame.

**Claim-Evidence Match:** V1 gets distractors and evidence that could support multiple claims. V2 gets cleaner pairings. V3 gets color-coded hints and fewer items.

**Quote Selection + Justification:** V1 selects from the full passage with written justification. V2 selects from a highlighted paragraph with sentence starters. V3 chooses from 3 pre-selected quotes with a frame: "I chose this because ______."

**Two-Column Organizer:** V1 locates and places evidence independently. V2 gets pre-identified evidence to sort. V3 gets yes/no binary ("Does this SUPPORT or WEAKEN?") with pointing.

**Perspective Comparison:** V1 fills in both sides independently. V2 gets one side pre-filled. V3 gets both sides partially filled with gaps to complete.

### How to build a new nonfiction reading unit .docx

**File dependency chain — three files produce one product:**

| File | Role | Location |
|------|------|----------|
| `cbd_docx_template.js` | Reusable design system (fonts, colors, spacing, all layout helpers). Shared across every unit. NEVER put unit content here. | `mnt/outputs/cbd_docx_template.js` |

The markdown source (`Frances_Kelsey_Unit_DRAFT.md`) is the content authoring file. Passages, MCQs, and short answers are written there first, then transcribed into the build script as JavaScript strings.

**Build steps:**

1. Write or update the unit content in a markdown draft file.
2. Create (or copy from Frances Kelsey) a unit-specific build script. It must `require("./mnt/outputs/cbd_docx_template")` to get the template module.
3. The build script calls template functions with content data: `T.titlePage({...})`, `T.heading1()`, `T.makeTable()`, `T.studentHandoutHeader()`, `T.buildVersionSection()`, `T.checklistTable()`, `T.assembleAndWrite()`.
4. The build script contains ONLY unit content (text, tables, passage markdown). All design decisions live in the template module.
5. Run `node build_docx.js` from the session root to generate the .docx file.
6. If design changes are needed, update `cbd_docx_template.js` — the change will apply to ALL units on next rebuild.

## ⚠️ Build session warnings — READ BEFORE BUILDING A UNIT

**These warnings exist because the 504 Sit-In build consumed multiple sessions and ran out of context mid-debug. Follow these rules to avoid repeating that.**

### Scale warning
A full nonfiction unit build script is 1,500–2,000+ lines of JavaScript. The 504 Sit-In build script is ~2,036 lines. This is a MASSIVE file. A single AI session cannot reliably write, debug, AND visually verify a file this size. Plan for multi-session work.

### Phased build strategy
Do NOT try to write the entire build script in one pass. Build in phases, rebuilding and verifying after each:

**Phase 1 — Skeleton + teacher sections** (~600–800 lines): Title page, TOC, all teacher reference sections (CCSS alignment, pacing, vocabulary, modeling session, checkpoint, intervention strategies, accessibility statement). Build → verify → commit.

**Phase 2 — Student handouts** (~200–300 lines): Annotation guide / skill reference, word bank, sentence frames, tools that help me learn, writing prompts, self-assessment, rubric. Build → verify → commit.

**Phase 3 — Passages** (~200–400 lines): All 12 passages (4 parts × 3 versions). Build → verify → confirm all passages render with text (not blank).

**Phase 4 — Comprehension activities** (~400–600 lines): All comprehension pages (skill-focused + comprehension-focused × 4 parts × 3 versions). Build → verify → confirm page count matches expectations.

**Phase 5 — Answer keys + end matter** (~200–300 lines): MCQ answer key tables, sample response text, end matter. Build → final verify.

### Known bugs to avoid

1. **`writeOnLines()` returns an array.** ALWAYS use `children.push(...T.writeOnLines(n))` with the spread operator. Never `children.push(T.writeOnLines(n))`. Missing spread causes `<0/>` XML corruption that silently produces an unopenable .docx file.

2. **`extractPassageText()` regex alternation.** When writing regex to extract content from the draft markdown, end-markers must be in a lookahead `(?=marker1|marker2|$)` — NOT bare alternation `content([\s\S]*?)marker1|marker2|$`. Bare alternation makes the last alternatives top-level patterns that don't capture group 1. This bug caused blank Part 4 passages.

3. **Rebuild and validate XML after every phase.** Run `node build_script.js` and then: `cd /tmp && mkdir -p docx_inspect && cd docx_inspect && unzip -q "path/to/output.docx" && xmllint --noout word/document.xml`. If xmllint reports errors, fix them before adding more content. Catching XML corruption early saves hours.

4. **Convert to page images and spot-check.** After each phase: `libreoffice --headless --convert-to pdf "output.docx" --outdir /tmp/verify && pdftoppm -jpeg -r 150 /tmp/verify/output.pdf /tmp/pages/page`. Then read key pages to confirm content renders. Blank pages = extraction bug. Missing write-on lines = spread operator bug.

5. **Do not hand-edit the .docx output.** Always regenerate from the build script. The .docx is a build artifact, not a source file.

6. **ALL page header/builder functions return arrays — MUST use spread.** Not just `writeOnLines()` — also `studentHandoutHeader()`, `esrPageHeader()`, `mcqPageHeader()`, `saPageHeader()`, `passageHeader()`, `titlePage()`, `tocPage()`, `endMatter()`, `accessibilityStatement()`. Missing spread on ANY of these causes `<0/>` XML corruption. (Discovered in Capitol Crawl build — 20 corrupted elements from missing spreads.)

7. **`studentHandoutHeader(unitTitle, title, subtitle)` — 3 arguments, not 1.** First arg is the lesson/unit title (appears in the sub-header), second is the section title (appears large), third is the subtitle label. Do NOT pair with a preceding `heading1()` call — `studentHandoutHeader` already includes `pageBreakBefore: true`, so a `heading1` before it creates an unwanted blank page.

8. **`titlePage()` property names must match exactly:** `{ unitTitle, unitSubtitle, skillNumber, skillName, gradeRange, versions, parts }`. Using wrong property names (e.g., `title` instead of `unitTitle`) silently produces "undefined" on the title page.

9. **`assembleAndWrite()` argument order:** `(unitShortTitle, children, outputPath, meta)` — the short title string is the FIRST argument, not children. It returns a Promise — either chain `.then()` or use async/await. The 504 Sit-In script uses `.then()` implicitly; the Capitol Crawl script uses explicit `.then()/.catch()`.

### Line-saving strategies for future builds

The 504 Sit-In build script is ~2,036 lines. About 330 of those are hand-coded comprehension activities (MCQs, Short Answers, Quote Selection, Perspective Comparison) — each question written as individual `children.push(T.p(...))` and `children.push(T.mcqChoice(...))` calls. This is the biggest source of bloat and bugs.

**Use the template's markdown parsers instead of hardcoding.** The template has `T.buildMcqItems(markdownText, isV3)` and `T.buildSaItems(markdownText, isV3)` which parse formatted markdown into proper docx elements. Instead of writing 20 lines of push calls per MCQ page, write the questions in the draft markdown and call: `children.push(...T.buildMcqItems(extractComprehensionText(partNum, versionNum, 'mcq'), v === 3))`.

**Create an `extractComprehensionText()` helper** (like `extractPassageText()`) that pulls comprehension content from the draft markdown by part, version, and type. This way the draft file remains the single source of truth for ALL content — passages AND activities.

**For novel activity types** (Quote Selection, Perspective Comparison) that don't have template parsers yet, consider adding them to `cbd_docx_template.js` as reusable builders. A `buildQuoteSelectionPage(version, directions, prompts)` helper that takes structured data would cut ~80 lines of per-version code to ~15 lines of data + 1 function call.

**Target for future units:** A build script under 1,200 lines by using draft markdown as the content source and template parsers as the builders. Teacher sections (~600 lines) + student handouts (~200 lines) + passage/comprehension extraction loops (~200 lines) + answer key extraction (~200 lines).

## Voice and tone rules for teacher materials

These apply to ALL teacher-facing text in every unit.

1. **Never prescribe pacing.** Do not say "5-day window covers Lessons 1–5" or "teachers with more time add Lessons 6–8." Provide lesson count, estimated time per lesson, and approximate reading time. Teachers decide how to use the unit.

2. **Provide time estimates, not schedules.** Example: "8 lessons · 45–60 minutes each · Total reading across all 4 parts: ~30–45 min (varies by version and reader)."

3. **Do not tell teachers what they "should" do.** Use "can," "consider," or present options. Exception: safety and compliance language (e.g., "Coordinate with SLP before Lesson 1" is directive because it's a compliance step).

4. **Student materials are direct.** Use "you" and imperative voice. "Read the sentence. Ask yourself: is this a claim?"

5. **Teacher materials are collegial.** Write as one professional to another. Assume competence.

## V3 passage design standards

These rules apply to ALL Version 3 (Lexile 400–550) student passages across every unit. They exist because CbD presumes competence — V3 readers deserve the same reading experience as V1 and V2, with support provided around the text rather than inside it.

1. **Vocabulary goes at the top, not inline.** Collect all vocabulary boxes for a passage part and display them in a "Key Vocabulary" section above the reading text. Students preview the terms before they read, just as V1/V2 students encounter terms in context. This prevents the reading experience from being constantly interrupted.

2. **The reading text flows uninterrupted.** Once the reading begins, the text reads like a text — continuous paragraphs with no inline scaffolding boxes, no comprehension checks, no vocabulary interruptions. Claims may still be marked with [C] tags (which fade across parts per the graduated prompting schedule), but those are annotation cues, not interruptions.

3. **Comprehension checks go at the end.** Yes/No checks and circle-one items appear in a "Check Your Understanding" section after the reading text. This gives students the full context of the passage before checking comprehension, rather than quizzing them mid-read.

4. **Annotation checks stay at the end.** The "How many C/E/R marks do you have?" reflection goes after the reading, not between paragraphs.

5. **Same rigor, different access.** V3 passages use shorter sentences, simpler syntax, and plain-language vocabulary — but the cognitive demand (annotate, evaluate claims, sort evidence) is identical to V1/V2. The scaffolding is in the structure and the supports, never in reducing the thinking.

6. **Prompts fade, not disappear.** The graduated prompting schedule ([C] bold → [C] only → asterisk hints → independent) remains. This is CbD's signature differentiation move — it assumes the student will get there and designs the ramp.

**Template implementation:** `buildPassageParagraphs(text, isV3)` — when `isV3 = true`, the function collects blockquote vocab boxes into a top section, reading paragraphs into the middle, and ✅/(*) check lines into a bottom section. V1/V2 render inline (original behavior).

## V3 Short Answer design standards

V3 Short Answer pages have a fundamentally different structure from V1/V2. They are NOT open-ended questions followed by write-on lines. They are scaffolded response formats that maintain the same cognitive demand with different access.

1. **Numbered questions only.** The SA page has numbered questions (`**1.**`, `**2.**`, `**3.**`). Sub-elements within a question (Circle lines, evidence lists, sentence frames) are part of that question's scaffold — they are NOT independent questions.

2. **No write-on lines for scaffold sub-elements.** Lines like "Circle: YES / NO", "Did they track patients? Yes / No", and "The company's claim was: supported / unsupported" are circle-and-respond items. They get compact paragraph rendering, not write-on lines.

3. **Bulleted evidence/choice lists render compactly.** Lines starting with `- ` are evidence items or choice options. They render with left indent (480 twips, 240 hanging) and tight after-spacing (30 twips).

4. **Sentence frame prompts get 2 write-on lines.** Lines containing "Use this frame:" are the only V3 SA elements that need write-on space. They get 2 lines for the student to complete the frame.

5. **Reasoning prompts with fill-in blanks get 2 write-on lines.** Lines starting with "Reasoning:" that contain underscore blanks indicate a fill-in response.

6. **Everything else renders as compact paragraphs.** Circle prompts, Yes/No responses, and scaffold text get after-spacing of 40 twips — tight enough to fit on one page.

**Why this matters:** The V1/V2 SA builder uses a heuristic (`trimmed.includes("?") && trimmed.length > 20`) to detect questions and add write-on lines. This heuristic catastrophically fails on V3 SA content because scaffold sub-questions like "Did they collect safety data? **Yes / No**" are not independent questions — they're part of a circle-response scaffold. The V3 path avoids this heuristic entirely and only counts `**N.**` numbered stems as questions.

**Template implementation:** `buildSaItems(text, isV3)` — when `isV3 = true`, the function uses a separate rendering path that handles numbered questions, bulleted lists, sentence frame prompts, and scaffold text without the write-on-line heuristic. `buildVersionSection` passes `isV3` automatically based on the version label.

## Table splitting standards for tall content

Some teacher-reference tables (pacing guides, AAC core/fringe word tables, AT Resources) contain enough content that a single row may be taller than the remaining page space. The `cantSplit: true` property prevents a row from splitting across pages, which is good for student-facing handouts but can cause large blank gaps on teacher pages.

**Rule:** Use `allowSplit: true` in the `makeTable` options for any teacher-reference table where rows contain extensive multi-line content. This allows Word to split tall rows across pages naturally instead of forcing a full-page blank.

**Tables that use allowSplit:**
- Schedule A — Core Unit (5 sessions) pacing table: each row has 4 columns of arrow-bulleted content
- AAC core/fringe word table: categorized word groups with paragraph breaks between categories
- AT Resources table: 8-row reference with setup instructions

**Tables that do NOT use allowSplit (cantSplit stays true):**
- Student handout tables (Word Bank, Sentence Frames, etc.) — must stay on one page
- Evidence Sort / Evidence Strength Rating tables — student-facing, need clean page layout
- Compact teacher tables (Version Guide, Annotation Tool Options) — short enough to never overflow

**Template implementation:** `makeTable(headers, rows, colWidths, { allowSplit: true })` — sets `cantSplit: false` on data rows. Header rows always use `cantSplit: true` regardless.

## Spacing standards for teacher reference pages

Teacher reference pages that contain multiple bold-label paragraphs (MLL strategies, AAC participation notes, IEP goal stems) need consistent breathing room between items. Without explicit `before` spacing, paragraphs stack too tightly and lose visual hierarchy.

**Bold-label paragraph spacing:** Each bold-label paragraph (e.g., "Pre-teach vocabulary visually: ...") should have `before: 80` twips to create visible separation from the previous paragraph. This applies to MLL strategies, AAC participation notes, and similar bold-label reference content.

**Spacer after teacherRefLabel:** No spacer needed after teacherRefLabel. The heading2 `before: 200` provides sufficient separation. Redundant spacers were removed in session 3.

**IEP goal stem spacing:** Each goal stem paragraph should have `before: 40, after: 120` for readable separation between the 5 goal categories.

**Pacing table formatting:** The Schedule A pacing table should use `{ compact: true, allowSplit: true }` — compact for tighter font/padding (the table is content-dense), allowSplit because rows are tall enough to overflow.

## V3 page consolidation standards

Version 3 passages are significantly shorter than V1/V2 (Lexile 400–550, simple sentences, 330–450 words per part). When every section (passage, MCQ, SA) forces a page break, V3 ends up with many half-empty pages. The fix: V3 MCQ and SA sections use **inline section dividers** instead of forced page breaks.

**Rule:** For V3 only, MCQ and SA headers render as an inline teal divider line with section title — no page break, no repeated Name/Class/Teacher fields. Passage headers still force a page break (each passage starts on a fresh page). V1 and V2 continue using full-page headers with forced page breaks for all sections.

**Implementation:** `mcqPageHeader(unitTitle, partTitle, versionLabel, inline)` and `saPageHeader(unitTitle, partTitle, versionLabel, inline)` accept a 4th parameter `inline`. When `true`, they render a teal top-border + bold section title without a page break. `buildVersionSection` passes `isV3` as the `inline` parameter.

**Result:** V3 went from 15 forced page breaks to 7 (1 version divider + 4 passage headers + ES + ESR). MCQ and SA now flow directly after the passage on the same page.

## Page margin and table width standards

**Page margins:** 0.75 inches (1080 twips) on all sides. This provides a wider content area (10080 twips vs. the original 9360) while maintaining professional appearance. Tighter margins allow more content per page and reduce wasted whitespace.

**Table auto-scaling:** `makeTable` automatically scales column widths proportionally to fill the full content width (`CONTENT_WIDTH = 10080`). If the sum of provided column widths is less than CONTENT_WIDTH, each column is multiplied by the ratio `CONTENT_WIDTH / rawTotal`. This means existing column width arrays from the build script automatically expand without manual updates.

**Header/footer table:** The page header table is split `6720 / 3360` to accommodate the wider content area.

## Learning Progression Visual design standards

The Learning Progression Visual is a **classroom poster** — designed to be printed and hung where students can see it. Teacher instructions must be visually separated from the poster content.

**Layout:** teacherRefLabel at top + Teacher note callout (print/post/dry-erase instructions) → spacer(200) → poster content (EQ banner, 5-column lesson boxes, progress tracker table). The spacer(200) creates clear visual separation between "teacher reads this" and "students see this."

**Teacher note callout pattern:** Any page that includes teacher-only instructions alongside student-facing or classroom-display content should use `T.callout("instruction text", "Teacher note")` at the top, after the teacherRefLabel. This keeps teacher guidance visually distinct (amber left border, bold label) from the content below.

**Lesson boxes:** 5-column table sized dynamically with `Math.floor(CONTENT_WIDTH / 5)` so they always fill the page width regardless of margin changes. Increased internal padding and font size (22pt lesson label, 20pt "I can" statement) for wall readability.

**Progress tracker:** 8-cell table grid (Lesson 1–8) with navy header row and a tall blank data row below. The teacher marks progress with a dry-erase marker, sticker, or magnet. Replaces the old inline Unicode checkbox row that wrapped badly at any reasonable font size.

## Checklist and interactive element standards

Unicode checkboxes (☐ / ☑) are banned from CbD documents. They render poorly across platforms, look flat and unprofessional, wrap badly when used inline, and don't function as actual form controls. All checklists and interactive elements use table-based layouts instead.

**Teacher checklists** (Classroom Setup, etc.): Use `checklistTable(items, { headerLabel, doneLabel, numbered })`. This renders a branded table with navy header row, alternating gray data rows, and a narrow right column for teachers to initial or check. Bold key terms in each item using `**bold**` markdown. Example: `{ headerLabel: "What to Prepare", doneLabel: "Ready", numbered: true }`.

**Student self-assessment** (before final product submission): Use `checklistTable` with `{ headerLabel: "Did I...", doneLabel: "Yes / Not Yet" }`. Each assessment criterion gets its own row with bold key terms. Students circle or write in the response column.

**Progress trackers** (Learning Progression Visual, lesson tracking): Use a custom table with the number of cells matching the number of items (e.g., 8 lessons = 8-column table). Navy header row with labels, tall blank data row below for marking. Sized dynamically with `Math.floor(CONTENT_WIDTH / N)`.

**Never use:** `☐ Item 1  ☐ Item 2  ☐ Item 3` inline patterns. They wrap unpredictably, can't be interacted with in print, and look like a rendering error rather than a designed element.

**Template implementation:** `checklistTable(items, opts)` is the primary helper. For custom tracking grids, build a `Table` directly with dynamic column widths. The legacy `checkbox()` function still exists in the template but should not be used in new content.

## Student-facing readability standards

Student pages must be readable by students with learning disabilities, visual processing challenges, and attention difficulties. These standards apply to all student-facing content (passages, MCQs, SA, handouts, rubrics).

**Passage text:** 23pt font, line spacing 360 (1.5x), after-spacing 180 twips, first-line indent 432 twips. This creates generous paragraph separation and breathing room for annotation in the margins.

**MCQ questions:** 23pt bold stems with 280 twips before-spacing between questions (120 before the first). Answer choices at 22pt with 60/60 before/after spacing and line height 276. This creates clear visual groups — students can instantly see where one question ends and the next begins.

**Writing Prompts and Self-Assessment:** Split across two pages. Writing Prompts page has the EQ at 24pt, labeled prompts (Prompt 1/2/3 in teal bold) with generous line height (300), format options, and a "My choice:" fill line. Self-Assessment page uses `checklistTable` for the self-check and a compact rubric table below.

**Name/Class/Teacher fields:** Table-based 3-column layout (42% / 26% / 32% of CONTENT_WIDTH) with bottom-border fill lines. Never use underscore characters for fill lines — they wrap unpredictably and look different across fonts.
