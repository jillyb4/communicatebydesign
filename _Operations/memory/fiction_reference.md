# CbD Fiction Anchor Texts — Reference
*Last updated: 2026-03-27 | Session 18*

## What This Product Line Is

Fiction Anchor Text units are **SDI companion units for traditional K–12 novels and short stories already in classrooms.**

The text is not ours. The novel is the novel — *Wonder*, *The Giver*, *Refugee*, whatever the team is already reading. What we build is the **instructional and access infrastructure** around that text:

- Specially designed instruction targeting one fiction skill per unit
- AAC-designed activities with appropriate vocabulary and response structures
- Visual supports (story maps, Visual Scene Displays, character boards)
- Partner scripts for Modes 1, 2, and 3 during and after reading
- A vocabulary layer built for fiction — not informational text

**This is not a Lexile differentiation product.** We do not rewrite the book. The standard stays the same. The scaffold varies. The expectation does not.

## Evidence Base

### Story Grammar Instruction
**Anchor citation:** Spencer & Petersen (2020), *Language, Speech, and Hearing Services in Schools* — 10 evidence-based principles for narrative intervention. This is the primary research foundation for the fiction line.

Story grammar (character, setting, problem/conflict, events, resolution, theme) gives students an organizational schema for understanding any narrative. Research shows statistically significant improvements in comprehension strategy use, story-structure knowledge, and reading comprehension — with **equivalent gains for students with and without disabilities** (ERIC EJ871908).

Story grammar is recognized SDI practice. It adapts methodology and delivery, not the standard.

### Narrative Language Predicts Reading Comprehension
Narrative macrostructure — the overall quality and organization of how a student processes a story — accounts for significant variance in reading comprehension beyond what decoding and basic language measures explain (Spencer & Petersen, 2020). Teaching story structure is literacy instruction, not a "lite" version of it.

### Where Students with Disabilities Struggle Most in Fiction
**Mental state language.** Emotions, motivations, beliefs, thoughts of characters. This is the domain students with disabilities find hardest — and it is also the heart of literary analysis. Emotional and relational vocabulary is central, not supplemental.

### Visual Scene Displays in Shared Storybook Reading
VSDs embedded in narrative contexts increase participation of complex communicators, enable rapid vocabulary acquisition, and reduce cognitive load by situating vocabulary within the actual scene (Drager et al., 2003). For fiction, VSDs are more effective than decontextualized symbol cards for key scenes.

### Figurative Language and Theme Are Teachable
Students previously thought to be "stuck at literal" demonstrate generalization when instruction is structured, visual, multi-exemplar, and involves graduated prompting. The teaching strategy matters more than the communication mode.

### ASHA on Narrative Language and AAC
ASHA identifies narrative intervention as accessible and evidence-supported for complex communicators (Perspectives, 2022). Aided language stimulation during shared reading has moderate-to-strong evidence. Narrative ability predicts early word reading. Speaking and listening are the necessary foundation for reading and writing.

### HLP Alignment for Fiction
| HLP | Application |
|-----|-------------|
| **HLP 16** | Shared reading with AAC access — foundational delivery method |
| **HLP 13** | Adapting tasks and materials — story maps, response boards, graphic organizers |
| **HLP 14** | Predictable Chart Writing / Write-Ables — group narrative construction around key scenes |
| **HLP 12** | Descriptive Teaching Model — teaching character and theme using core vocabulary to describe, not label |

## Activity Design Principles — HARD RULES

These are non-negotiable for every fiction unit.

### 1. Design for Description, Not Recall of Names

**Never lead with "Who did it?" when the student may not have that name on their device.**

Character names are fringe words — specific proper nouns, often not programmed. A student may not be able to indicate "Auggie" but can absolutely indicate:
> *small / boy / face looks different / feels scared / wants a friend*

Design every activity so that **description, action, and attribute** are the access point — not name recall.

| ❌ Don't lead with | ✅ Lead with instead |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| "Who was at the park?" | "What did the person at the park look like? What were they doing?" |
| "Who felt angry?" | "Find the character who felt angry. How do you know? What did they do?" |
| "Who is the main character?" | "Describe the person this story is mostly about." |
| "What is her name?" | "What do we know about her? What does she do? How does she feel?" |

**The only time to lead with "who" is when identity/name is the actual instructional target** — e.g., a point of view unit where knowing the narrator matters to the standard. Even then, build a description path alongside it.

### 2. Description Can Be the Output — "Describe to Draw"

A student who builds a description of a character — *tall, old, angry, wears dark clothes, moves slowly, lives alone* — has demonstrated comprehension. That description could instruct someone to draw the character.

**"Describe to Draw" is a core fiction output mode:**
- Student builds a character description using core + accessible fringe vocabulary
- Partner, peer, or student uses the description to create a drawing
- The drawing becomes evidence of comprehension without requiring written output

This is not a workaround. It is comprehension demonstrated through generation — the highest-level evidence of understanding. It is also a collaborative literacy activity that naturally invites Mode 2 partner behavior.

### 3. Response Mode Is Always Multiple

Every activity must have at least three response pathways:
- **Symbolic/AAC** — symbol selection, device output, eye gaze board
- **Verbal/gestural** — pointing, nodding, gesture, partner-confirmed oral
- **Generative/constructive** — building a description, arranging symbols, completing a sentence frame

Never design an activity where the only valid response is selecting one correct answer from a closed set. Open response via description is always available.

### 4. The Scaffold Varies. The Expectation Does Not.

Every student engages with the same text, the same skill, the same standard. The activity structure, vocabulary access, and response mode differ — but the literary thinking being targeted is the same. Never frame scaffolded activities as easier or lower. They are differently accessed.

### 5. Mode 2 During Reading Is Not Optional

Fiction reading alongside students means being in **Mode 2 (Partnership)** — follow the student's lead, no demands, notice and note spontaneous communication. Do not run prompt hierarchy during the read. Mode 1 (Instructional) is for the focused activity before or after reading. Mixing modes during fiction reading is the most common partner error.

## Vocabulary Framework for Fiction

Fiction vocabulary is different from nonfiction vocabulary. The categories are not the same.

### Tier 1: Emotional and Mental State Vocabulary
This is the hardest domain for students with disabilities and the most important for fiction. Pre-teach before every unit. Build into every response board.

**Core emotional vocabulary (must be on every fiction AAC tool):**
feel, want, need, think, know, like, love, hate, sad, happy, angry, scared, confused, proud, ashamed, alone, together, afraid, surprised, worried, hope

**Relational / mental state (key to inference and character motivation):**
because, so, if, then, maybe, probably, wish, wonder, believe, decide, remember, forget, change, try, give up

### Tier 2: Narrative Fringe — Description, Not Name
Fiction fringe is **descriptive** — attributes, actions, appearances, settings. It is not the same as nonfiction content fringe (domain/topic vocabulary).

**Appearance vocabulary:** tall, short, old, young, big, small, dark, light, thin, heavy, wears, carries, holds, looks like
**Action vocabulary:** runs, hides, fights, cries, laughs, leaves, returns, finds, loses, helps, hurts, saves, lies, tells the truth
**Setting vocabulary:** home, school, city, woods, outside, inside, long ago, now, at night, in winter, far away

**Character names are fringe words — always provide a visual reference (image or VSD) alongside the name.**

### Tier 3: Skill-Specific Vocabulary
Each unit's targeted skill requires its own vocabulary set. Define these per unit.

| Skill | Key vocabulary to pre-teach |
|-------|----------------------------|
| Story Grammar | character, setting, problem, event, solution, beginning, middle, end |
| Inferencing | because, maybe, probably, think, feel, clue, evidence, wonder |
| Theme | message, lesson, idea, throughout, everyone, life, important, learn |
| Point of View | narrator, tells, knows, sees, thinks, I, we, they, outside, inside |
| Figurative Language | means, like, as, really, not really, image, feel like, compare |
| Character Analysis | trait, changes, stays the same, relationship, acts like, feels when |

## Differentiation Model: Same Text, Different Scaffold

We do not produce three versions of the text. We produce scaffolded access to the same text.

| Access Need | Scaffold |
|---|---|
| Needs full text in print | Standard novel page — no modification |
| Needs supported listening | Audiobook + follow-along visual or symbol track |
| Needs pre-reading context | Before-reading VSD and vocabulary preview |
| Needs visual story structure | Story map with symbols completed during reading |
| Needs modified response | Symbol selection, partner-confirmed verbal, "Describe to Draw" |
| Needs modified output length | Sentence frame instead of paragraph; 2-item description instead of 5 |
| Needs emotional vocabulary support | Pre-built emotion word board for the unit |

The text is the same. The thinking target is the same. The evidence of understanding is structured differently.

## IEP Goal Structures (Sample)

**Story Grammar:**
> "Given a grade-level fiction text and a visual story map, student will identify [character / setting / problem / solution] with 80% accuracy across 3 consecutive sessions using their AAC system."

**Inferencing:**
> "Given a narrative passage and visual character emotion supports, student will make inferences about character feelings or motivations with 80% accuracy, using AAC device, communication board, or partner-confirmed verbal response."

**Theme:**
> "Given a fiction text and structured discussion, student will identify the central message or theme using a sentence frame and core vocabulary with 80% accuracy across 3 sessions."

**Character Analysis:**
> "Student will describe a character's traits and actions using descriptive vocabulary (not name recall) with 80% accuracy, using any access method."

| Code | What It Marks | SDI Part | Body Paragraph |

## Language Rules

- Strength-based throughout — the gap is always the system, never the student
- "Communication Access" section heading — not "AAC Support"
- Never "point to" alone → "select, point to, or use gaze to indicate"
- Never assume SGD — works with any AAC system
- Never design for name-recall when description is the actual comprehension target
- Never frame scaffolded activities as "easier" — they are differently accessed
- Neutral response verbs: "indicate," "select," "identify," "describe," "show"
- Never: "struggling readers," "low students," "below grade level"
