# CbD AAC Philosophy — The Why

This is the foundation behind every CbD AAC rule. Apply it to all writing, products, and Substack content.

---

## AAC is not a device. It is an ecosystem.

Symbols on a nurse board. A gaze target practiced over years. A yes/no system built through trust. A communication partner who knows to pause and watch. The SGD is one part of the ecosystem — never the whole thing. When one piece isn't accessible, communication doesn't stop — *if the rest of the ecosystem is built.*

## Presumed competence — for the student, always.

Not "the student might have something to say." The student *has* something to say, right now, with whatever is in front of them. The gap is never the student. It is the environment, the partner training, the missing piece of the ecosystem.

## Communication is not gated by expertise.

A para, a parent, a sibling, a teacher — anyone in a student's life can be a communication partner. "Work with the SLP" is the system's way of closing a door that should stay open. The whole team has standing here.

## The student directs.

AAC exists so the student can say what *they* want to say — demonstrate knowledge, express a need, share a preference, say no. The goal is not compliance with a prompt. It is the student having a voice in their own life.

## Preferences are dynamic and must be honored.

What works at 7 may not work at 15. What works in the morning may not work in the afternoon. Building the ecosystem means building flexibility into it — so when one piece isn't accessible, communication doesn't stop.

## Explicit instruction of the AAC ecosystem builds literacy pathways.

Teaching AAC is not separate from teaching literacy. It is the pathway through which a student learns to communicate with intent, to sequence, to direct meaning. The instruction is not remediation. It is access.

## Teach before the moment arrives.

You don't know when the lesson will be needed. The vocabulary you build today is for a moment you can't predict — a surgery, a Friday morning, a conversation that hasn't happened yet. Build it in advance. Teach it before it's urgent.

## Core gives you the conversation. Fringe gives you the precision. Both are necessary.

Core vocabulary — high-frequency, cross-context words (~200–400, accounting for ~80% of communication, Beukelman et al. 1989) — is the foundation of every lesson. Teach it explicitly, every time.

But fringe is not optional and not secondary. Fringe is how the student gets specific. It's how "medication" becomes "Zofran." Without fringe, communication is functional. With fringe, it's precise enough to matter — to direct care, to express preference, to say exactly what they mean.

Critically: the student decides which fringe vocabulary is most important to them. The team builds and teaches exposure to fringe. The student determines what of that is *really* important in their life. That is student-directed communication.

## Honor the whole child.

Every product, every word, every tool is in service of a human being who has preferences, a history, a personality, and things they want to say.

---

*This philosophy was first articulated on 2026-03-27 in the context of a Friday morning with a 15-year-old SGD user who used gaze + symbols to communicate a missed medication when her device wasn't in reach — proof that an ecosystem built right doesn't stop communicating.*
