## Original CbD Poem — "The Words I Carry"
*© Communicate by Design. All rights reserved. May not be reproduced outside CbD products.*

```
My voice is a river
that runs in different ways—
sometimes a pointing finger,
sometimes a symbol on a page.

The words I carry
do not always make a sound.
But I hold them like stones in my pocket,
smooth and real and round.

Do not listen with your ears alone.
Watch my hands reach for the word.
That is how I speak.
That is how I am heard.
```

**Reading level:** V3 compatible (short lines; no Tier 3 vocabulary except implied concepts; every line partner-readable)

**Figurative language present:**
- Metaphor: "My voice is a river that runs in different ways" (voice = river; multiple pathways)
- Simile/Imagery: "I hold them like stones in my pocket, / smooth and real and round" (words = tangible, carried, real)
- Imagery: "a pointing finger," "a symbol on a page," "Watch my hands reach for the word" (three physical images of AAC use)
- Metaphor (implied): "Do not listen with your ears alone" (listening = broader act of receiving communication)

**Tone:** Matter-of-fact. Proud. Not inspirational-poster language. The speaker states truth, does not ask for understanding.

**NFMA mapping:**
- NOTICE: "My voice is a river" / "I hold them like stones" — which lines use figurative language?
- FEEL: What feeling does "smooth and real and round" create? (grounded, calm, certain — not sad)
- MEAN: What is the speaker really saying about how they communicate?
- ASK: Who is the speaker talking to with "Do not listen with your ears alone"?

## NFMA Activity Framework — All 4 Poems

### How NFMA Maps to RL.6.4 / RL.7.4

| Step | Full Name | What the Student Does | Standard Connection |
|------|-----------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| **N** | NOTICE | Identify the figurative language — what specific words stand out? | RL.x.4 — determine the meaning of words and phrases as used |
| **F** | FEEL | Name the mood or feeling the language creates | RL.x.4 — analyze cumulative impact; L.5.5 — connotation |
| **M** | MEAN | Interpret what the figurative language actually reveals about meaning or theme | RL.x.4 — effect on meaning, tone; RL.x.2 — theme |
| **A** | ASK | Generate a question and identify at least one piece of textual evidence | RL.x.1 — cite textual evidence |

**IDEA compliance check:** All four steps require skill demonstration, not recall. V3 frames support access — they do not remove the analytical task. The MEAN step (interpretation) and ASK step (evidence) must be present in all three access levels.

---

### V1 / V2 / V3 Scaffold Pattern (per NFMA step)

#### NOTICE — Identify Figurative Language

| Version | Activity Format | AAC Pathway |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| **V1** | "Identify one example of figurative language in this poem. Name the type and copy the line." | CAP vocabulary pre-programmed; partner models *figurative language, metaphor, imagery* |
| **V2** | "Circle one example of figurative language. Complete the frame: 'The poet uses _______ when they write: _____.'" | Same CAP; sentence frame reduces production demand; fill-in uses pre-programmed fringe |
| **V3** | Partner reads each stanza aloud. Student indicates the line that uses figurative language (point, gaze, AAC tap). [Symbol options per stanza]. Then: 'What kind is it?' [choice: metaphor / imagery / other] | Same CAP; partner reads; response is indicate + select; 5-second wait after each stanza |

#### FEEL — Name the Effect / Mood

| Version | Activity Format | AAC Pathway |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| **V1** | "What feeling does this figurative language create for the reader? How does it connect to the poem's tone? Explain." | Partner models *tone, feel*; device available |
| **V2** | "Circle the feeling this language creates: [4 options]. Complete: 'This language creates a _____ tone because _____."' | 4-choice field; sentence frame; fringe vocabulary pre-programmed |
| **V3** | "How does this part of the poem make you feel?" [AAC symbol choice board: 4 feeling options]. Partner asks: 'Why?' Student responds using sentence frame: 'It feels _____ because _____.' | Choice board is low-tech backup; frame pre-loaded on device; partner expands response |

#### MEAN — Interpret the Figurative Language

| Version | Activity Format | AAC Pathway |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| **V1** | "What is the poet really saying? What does this figurative language reveal about the speaker's message or the poem's theme?" | Full open response; partner models *mean, show, prove* |
| **V2** | "What does this figurative language mean? Complete: 'The poet uses [term] to show that _____. This is important because _____." | Partial frame; student supplies both content slots |
| **V3** | "What is the poet really saying? Choose one:" [3 options — 2 at different interpretive levels; 1 distractor]. "Use your AAC to tell your partner why you chose it." [frame: 'I chose _____ because _____.'] | 3-option field; frame with 2 fill-ins; selection + explanation = skill demonstrated |

#### ASK — Question + Evidence

| Version | Activity Format | AAC Pathway |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| **V1** | "What question does this poem raise for you? Find one line in the poem that supports or complicates your thinking." | Full open response |
| **V2** | "Write one question about this poem." [frame: 'I wonder _____.']. "Then find one line that begins to answer it. Write the line." | Frame scaffolds question formation; evidence identification open |
| **V3** | "Ask your partner one question about the poem." [frame: 'I wonder _____.'] "Then show your partner one line that answers it. Point to it, tap it, or use your AAC." | Question frame; evidence = pointing/indicating (no production demand); partner scribes |

### Poem-Specific NFMA Notes

#### Poem 1: "We Wear the Mask" (Dunbar)
- **Primary device:** Extended metaphor — the mask = a social performance of happiness hiding true pain and suffering. Critical that students reach MEAN — that the mask is not a choice for pleasure, it is a survival strategy.
- **V3 NOTICE caution:** "We wear the mask that grins and lies" — the word *lies* here means "deceives," not "lies down." Flag for partner pre-teaching.
- **FEEL step:** Students may identify *sad, hurt, tired* — all valid. Accept and expand. Do not redirect to the "correct" feeling; poem supports multiple valid emotional readings.
- **AAC note:** *mask* is a literal object on most systems. Partner must explicitly teach the figurative use before MEAN step: "In this poem, the mask is not a real mask. It means hiding how you really feel."

#### Poem 2: "I'm Nobody! Who are you?" (Dickinson)
- **Primary device:** Metaphor — Nobody/Somebody = categories of identity (outsider vs. public figure); Frog/Bog = symbol of empty self-promotion.
- **V3 NOTICE caution:** Dickinson's capitalization (*Nobody, Somebody, Frog, Bog*) signals these are figurative categories, not proper nouns. Flag for partner guidance.
- **FEEL step:** Students often feel *proud* or *happy* about being a "Nobody" with Dickinson — this is the correct interpretive direction. Tone is playful, conspiratorial, not sad.
- **MEAN step:** The interpretive leap — "Nobody" is not bad; it is being with others like you. This is a productive ASK: "Who is Dickinson talking to when she says 'Who are you?'"

#### Poem 3: "The Man with the Hoe" (Markham)
- **Primary device:** Imagery — physical description of a laboring body as evidence of social conditions; allusion to Millet's painting (background only for V2/V3 — do not require knowledge of painting for V1 either).
- **V3 access note:** This is the most complex poem linguistically. V3 partner reads one stanza at a time with a pause between. The NOTICE step focuses on one or two key lines of imagery only — not the full poem.
- **FEEL step:** *tired, heavy, bent, empty* — all in the poem's physical vocabulary. V3 students can respond with feeling vocabulary without needing to name the device.
- **Background for all versions:** The hoe is a farming tool. Define before reading. Do not require students to know what it looks like.

#### Poem 4: "The Words I Carry" (CbD Original)
- **Primary devices:** Voice = river (metaphor); words = stones (simile/imagery); listening with ears alone (extended metaphor of communication).
- **AAC note:** This poem IS about AAC communication. Partners should read this poem without editorializing. Let students respond to it on its own terms. The speaker is not seeking sympathy — the speaker is stating how things are.
- **FEEL step:** *proud, calm, sure, real* — the tone is declarative, not sad. If students identify sad or lonely, ask: "Is the speaker asking for anything? Or are they just telling you something?"
- **ASK step:** "Who is the speaker talking to?" — the last stanza explicitly addresses a listener. This is a strong evidence-based discussion point.

## Unit 1 Rubric — RL.6.4 Figurative Language Analysis
*3-level rubric · 4 criteria · Identical across V1/V2/V3 · Assessment Framework compliant*
*Created: 2026-03-30*

### Design Rules (Assessment Framework)
- 3 levels only: Does Not Yet Meet / Approaching / Meets
- Behavioral descriptions — observable actions, not inferred understanding
- Identical wording across V1/V2/V3 — the scaffold changes; the standard does not
- All valid response modes count equally: AAC output, gesture, pointing, speech, writing
- Mastery default: 80% accuracy across 3 consecutive trials, generalized across 2+ partners
- Data collection: special educator administers rubric; para uses Session Tracker

---

### Rubric: RL.6.4 — Figurative Language in Poetry

**Response Mode Note (print at top of rubric page):**
> All response modes are valid and equivalent. "Indicates" means any of the following: selects on AAC device, points, uses eye gaze, writes, or speaks. Do not require one mode over another.

---

#### Criterion 1: Identify Figurative Language (NOTICE — Step 1)
*The student indicates a line or phrase that contains figurative language.*

| Level | Behavioral Description |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Does Not Yet Meet** | Student does not respond to the identification prompt, or indicates a line from the poem that does not contain figurative language (e.g., selects a literal line; selects any line without discrimination). Response occurs even with a reduced choice field (2 options). |
| **Approaching** | Student indicates a line containing figurative language when the partner reduces the choice field to 2 options, OR indicates the correct line after one direct cue (partner points to the figurative language region of the poem without naming it). Responds inconsistently across trials (1–2 out of 3). |
| **Meets** | Student independently indicates a line containing figurative language without a reduced choice field or a direct cue beyond Level 1 wait (5 seconds). Responds consistently (3 out of 3 trials, or across 2 poems in the same session). |

---

#### Criterion 2: Name the Type of Figurative Language (NOTICE — Step 2)
*The student names or selects the correct type of figurative language (metaphor, imagery, or other device used in this unit).*

| Level | Behavioral Description |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Does Not Yet Meet** | Student does not respond to the naming prompt, or names/selects a type that does not match the example identified in Criterion 1 (e.g., identifies a metaphor but then selects "rhyme" as the type). |
| **Approaching** | Student names or selects the correct type of figurative language when given a 2-choice field (the correct type plus one distractor), OR names the correct type after one direct cue (partner models the term on the AAC system without giving the answer). Selects correctly on 2 of 3 trials. |
| **Meets** | Student independently names or selects the correct type of figurative language from a field of 3+ options without a reduced choice field or direct cue beyond wait. Correct on 3 of 3 trials, or across 2 poems in the same session. |

---

#### Criterion 3: Interpret the Figurative Language (MEAN)
*The student explains what the figurative language means — what the poet is really saying — beyond the literal meaning of the words.*

| Level | Behavioral Description |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Does Not Yet Meet** | Student does not produce an interpretation, OR produces a response that restates the literal meaning of the words without identifying the figurative meaning (e.g., "the mask covers a face" for Dunbar's mask). Response does not advance past the literal layer even with a sentence frame provided. |
| **Approaching** | Student produces a response that identifies the figurative meaning (what the language means beyond the literal) but does not connect it to the speaker's message or the poem's broader significance. May require a sentence frame with one content slot left blank, or one Level 3 cue (partner points to the "MEAN" prompt without filling in the answer). Response is partially complete: identifies "what" but not "why it matters." |
| **Meets** | Student produces a response that identifies both (a) what the figurative language means beyond the literal AND (b) what it reveals about the speaker's message, using a sentence frame or open response. Response contains at least two connected elements (the figurative meaning + its significance), using any valid response mode. No cue beyond wait required. |

---

#### Criterion 4: Explain the Effect on Tone or Mood (FEEL + MEAN)
*The student names the mood or tone the figurative language creates AND connects that mood to at least one word or phrase from the poem.*

| Level | Behavioral Description |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Does Not Yet Meet** | Student does not name or select a mood/tone, OR names a mood that has no logical connection to the figurative language cited (e.g., names "excited" for Dunbar's mask without any poem evidence supporting that reading). No self-correction with wait. |
| **Approaching** | Student names or selects a mood/tone that is logically connected to the figurative language (any defensible reading is acceptable), but does not connect the mood to specific language from the poem. Response stops at the feeling label without citing evidence ("It feels sad" with no poem connection). |
| **Meets** | Student names or selects a mood/tone connected to the figurative language AND provides at least one word or phrase from the poem as evidence of why that mood is created (e.g., "sad/hiding — because the mask hides"). The poem connection may be indicated (point, gaze, tap on text) rather than stated — indicating the word counts as citing it. No cue beyond wait required. |

---

### Scoring Notes for the Rubric Page

**All four criteria must be scored for each poem.** A student does not need to score "Meets" on all four criteria to demonstrate growth. Track across sessions to identify which NFMA steps are consistent and which need additional instructional focus.

**IEP data threshold:** "Meets" on Criteria 3 and 4 (the interpretive criteria) across 3 consecutive sessions = mastery on the academic goal. Criteria 1 and 2 support the skill but the standard requires interpretation, not identification alone.

**AAC note for scoring:** If a student indicates a word/phrase in the poem by touching, pointing, or using eye gaze, that is equivalent to writing or saying the word. Score accordingly — do not require verbal or written output for "Meets."

**Modification flag:** If a student is working toward an alternate achievement standard and identification only (Criteria 1–2) is the IEP target, document this explicitly. Do not use this rubric's "Does Not Yet Meet" language to describe that student's performance — they are working toward a different standard, not failing this one.
