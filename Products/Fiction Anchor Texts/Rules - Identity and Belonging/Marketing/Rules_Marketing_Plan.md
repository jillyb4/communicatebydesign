## Full Description
```
Rules: Identity and Belonging
A Fiction Anchor Text Unit for Special Education · Grades 4–6

Your students are reading Rules. This unit gives every student access to what it's actually about.

This is a complete character analysis unit built for special education classrooms, grades 4–6. Whole book. AAC support designed in from the first page. The standard does not change — the access layer does.

THE CENTRAL QUESTION:
What does it really mean to belong — and what gets in the way?

This question drives all five parts of the unit and builds toward a full character analysis response by Part 5. Students track how Catherine's beliefs about belonging shift across the novel using the Belonging Evidence Chart — by the synthesis, the chart is the outline.

A NOTE ABOUT JASON:
Jason communicates using word cards — a form of low-tech AAC. This unit treats him as a full communicator: not a symbol of disability, not a lesson about inclusion, but a character with perspective, humor, preferences, and something important to say. Students who use AAC see themselves in the text. All students encounter communication access as a feature of character — not a footnote.

UNIT STRUCTURE — 5 PARTS:
Part 1: The Rules (Chapters 1–3) — What rules does Catherine write? What do they reveal about how she thinks belonging works?
Part 2: Meeting Jason (Chapters 4–9) — How does Catherine feel meeting Jason? What does Jason want that surprises her?
Part 3: Rules Break Down (Chapters 10–16) — What happens when Catherine's rules stop working? How do her beliefs shift?
Part 4: What Catherine Learns (Chapters 17–24) — What does Catherine choose? Why is it difficult? What does it reveal?
Part 5: Whole-Book Synthesis — Students build a full character analysis showing how Catherine's thinking changes from beginning to end.

THE BELONGING EVIDENCE CHART:
Instead of margin annotation codes, this unit uses a cumulative Belonging Evidence Chart built across all five parts. Each row captures: what Catherine believes → what happens → how her thinking shifts. By Part 5, the chart is the outline for the synthesis response.

WHAT'S INCLUDED:

Teaching Materials (teacher document) — Research base, novel overview, all 5 parts with activities and partner guidance at point of use, communication access vocabulary table (23 words: 12 core, 11 fringe), before-reading activities including Jason's Communication Book Primer, Belonging Evidence Chart template with sentence frames, IEP goal stems for RL.4.3/4.6/5.3, three AAC communication goals, rubric, and answer key

Fiction Printable Packet (13 pages) — Communication Environment Setup guide · Core word symbol cards (12 words) · Fringe word symbol cards (12 words, chapter order) · Board A: Character Description Board — LOOKS LIKE / DOES / FEELS / WANTS for Catherine and Jason (landscape) · Board B: Emotion + Belonging · Board C: Literary Discussion Moves · Vocabulary Map · AAC Session Tracker · Student Response Pages for all 5 parts

STANDARDS:
RL.4.3, RL.5.3 — Describe in depth a character, drawing on specific details in the text
RL.4.6, RL.5.6 — Compare and contrast point of view; author's perspective
RL.5.3 — Compare and contrast two or more characters, settings, or events

AAC ACCESS:
Every part has a clear AAC pathway — device, e-tran board, symbol cards, gaze access, or alternative pencil. Partner guidance is written at point of use, not in a separate supplement. Three ready-to-laminate communication boards and symbol cards organized by chapter order are included in the Printable Packet. Board A has a dedicated section for both Catherine and Jason — every student is tracking both characters.

Every student reads Rules. The text does not change. The communication access tools — symbol cards, communication boards, the Belonging Evidence Chart, and partner guidance — are what make the text reachable for every student in the room. The access layer varies. The expectation does not.

IEP GOAL STEMS INCLUDED FOR:
RL.4.3 / RL.5.3 — character description using textual evidence
RL.4.6 / RL.5.6 — point of view and perspective comparison
RL.5.3 — comparing Catherine and Jason as characters
AAC: building spontaneous use of belonging and identity vocabulary
AAC: using communication board during character analysis discussion
AAC: partner-supported response using sentence frames

Perfect for: resource room, inclusion support, co-teaching, SDI pull-out, summer extended school year
```
