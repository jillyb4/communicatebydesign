## Option A: Question Hook (drives comments)

**Caption:**
When does your student use their AAC device?

If the answer is "mostly during structured activities" — it might be time to rethink.

Play is where language lives. It's where kids practice, experiment, fail safely, and try again. AAC belongs there too.

This week I'm thinking about what it looks like to be a true communication partner during play — not just a prompter, but a player.

What does play-based AAC look like in your setting? Drop it below 👇

## Option B: Bold Statement (drives saves/shares)

**Caption:**
Play is not a break from communication practice.
Play IS communication practice.

When we only pull out the AAC device for structured tasks, we're sending a message: *communication is for work, not for life.*

But language development doesn't pause for recess. Kids learn words through doing things — building, pretending, negotiating, laughing. That's where AAC needs to show up too.

This month, I'm looking at how we can be better communication partners in unstructured moments. Not as therapists. As players.

Save this if it made you think about your practice 🔖
